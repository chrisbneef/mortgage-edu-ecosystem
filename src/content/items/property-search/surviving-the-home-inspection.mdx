The inspection report lands in your inbox, it's twenty pages long, and it has photos with red circles all over your dream house. Before your heart sinks, know this: that's what every inspection report looks like. Even brand-new homes generate a list. A long report doesn't mean you bought a lemon.

An inspection is not the same thing as an appraisal, and people mix them up all the time. An appraisal is about value, what the home is worth. An inspection is about condition, the actual state of the house.

## What the inspector is looking at

The inspector walks the whole property and checks the parts that matter most: the roof, the foundation, the heating and cooling system, the electrical, the plumbing, and any signs of water where water shouldn't be. They poke around the attic and crawl space, run the faucets, and flip the breakers.

What they hand you is a snapshot of the house's health on that day. Some of it will be genuinely useful. Some of it will be tiny stuff that any home of that age would show.

## Sorting the serious from the noise

Here's the calming truth that gets lost in all those red circles. The findings that deserve your attention are the big-ticket and safety items: the roof, the foundation, the major systems, water intrusion, and electrical hazards. A failing furnace or a cracked foundation is worth a hard conversation. A loose cabinet hinge or a missing outlet cover is not.

Let the cosmetic stuff go. You'll fix a dozen little things in your first year of owning any home, and that's just part of the deal. Spending your negotiating energy on a $15 fix is energy you don't get back for the $8,000 one.

When something real does turn up, get a contractor out for a quote. Walking into a negotiation with "the roof needs work" is weak. Walking in with "here's a licensed roofer's estimate for $6,200" gives you a number the seller has to take seriously.

## Your options when there's a real problem

You're not stuck. With a real issue on the table, you generally have three moves:

- Ask the seller to make the repair before closing
- Ask for a credit or a price reduction so you can handle it yourself
- Walk away, if you still have an inspection contingency in your contract

Which one fits depends on the issue and how much you want the house. A credit is often cleaner than a repair, because you control who does the work and how well.

<Callout type="warning">You usually pay for the inspection yourself, often somewhere around $300 to $500. It feels like a lot when you're already spending big, but finding a $9,000 problem before you buy is money very well spent.</Callout>

Take the report seriously, but don't let it scare you out of a good house over small things. Focus on what's structural, safe, and expensive. Handle those with real numbers, and let the rest be tomorrow's weekend projects.
