The inspection report lands in your inbox, it's twenty pages long, and it has photos with red circles all over your dream house. Before your heart sinks, know this: that's what every inspection report looks like. Even brand-new homes generate a list. A long report doesn't mean you bought a lemon.

An inspection is not the same thing as an appraisal, and people mix them up all the time. An appraisal is about value, what the home is worth. An inspection is about condition, the actual state of the house.

## What the inspector is looking at

The inspector walks the whole property and checks the parts that matter most: the roof, the foundation, the heating and cooling system, the electrical, the plumbing, and any signs of water where water shouldn't be. They poke around the attic and crawl space, run the faucets, and flip the breakers.

What they hand you is a snapshot of the house's health on that day. Some of it will be genuinely useful. Some of it will be tiny stuff that any home of that age would show.

## Sorting the serious from the noise

Here's the calming truth that gets lost in all those red circles. The findings that deserve your attention are the big-ticket and safety items: the roof, the foundation, the major systems, water intrusion, and electrical hazards. A failing furnace or a cracked foundation is worth a hard conversation. A loose cabinet hinge or a missing outlet cover is not.

Let the cosmetic stuff go. You'll fix a dozen little things in your first year of owning any home, and that's just part of the deal. Spending your negotiating energy on a $15 fix is energy you don't get back for the $8,000 one.

A few findings sound scary but usually aren't dealbreakers on their own: minor settling cracks, a water heater near the end of its life, a few double-tapped breakers, older but functioning windows. They're worth noting and budgeting for, not panicking over. The flip side is a short list that should make you slow way down: active roof leaks, foundation movement, signs of a chronic moisture or mold problem, knob-and-tube or aluminum wiring, a buried oil tank, or anything that hints at past flooding. Those can carry costs and insurance complications well beyond the repair itself.

When something real does turn up, get a contractor out for a quote. Walking into a negotiation with "the roof needs work" is weak. Walking in with "here's a licensed roofer's estimate for $6,200" gives you a number the seller has to take seriously.

## A real example of how this plays out

Say your inspection on a $340,000 home flags three things: a roof with maybe three years left, a cracked heat exchanger in the furnace, and a slow drip under the kitchen sink. You let the sink go; that's a $40 fix you'll handle the first weekend. The furnace is a safety issue, so you get an HVAC quote at $5,500. The roof isn't urgent, but a roofer pegs replacement at $11,000.

Now you have $16,500 of real, documented cost.

Instead of asking the seller to manage repairs on their way out the door, you ask for an $11,000 credit toward the roof and a seller-completed furnace repair before closing, since the furnace is a safety item you'd want done right. That's a focused, defensible ask backed by paper, which is far more persuasive than a vague complaint about an old house.

## Your options when there's a real problem

You're not stuck. With a real issue on the table, you generally have three moves:

- Ask the seller to make the repair before closing
- Ask for a credit or a price reduction so you can handle it yourself
- Walk away, if you still have an inspection contingency in your contract

Which one fits depends on the issue and how much you want the house. A credit is often cleaner than a repair, because you control who does the work and how well. One trap to know about: a repair credit can sometimes bump up against your loan program's limits on seller-paid costs, and lenders won't always let a credit exceed your actual closing costs. A price reduction can be cleaner in that case. Ask your loan officer which path keeps your financing intact.

It also helps to know what an inspection is not. It's a visual, non-invasive snapshot, so inspectors don't open walls, dig up the yard, or guarantee a system won't fail next month. In a hot market, some buyers waive the inspection contingency to win a bid; you can still get an inspection for your own information, but you give up the right to renegotiate or walk based on what it finds. That's a real risk, not a formality.

## What to do next

Read the full report, not just the summary, then sort every item into three buckets: safety and structural, worth a quote and a possible ask, and small stuff you'll handle yourself. Get written estimates on anything in the first bucket before you talk numbers with the seller, and lean on your agent to shape the request. Take the report seriously, but don't let it scare you out of a good house over small things. Focus on what's structural, safe, and expensive, handle those with real numbers, and let the rest be tomorrow's weekend projects.
