A listing that says "rents for $1,800" tells you almost nothing about whether it's a good deal.
The rent is only the top line. What makes or breaks a rental is what's left after every expense
gets paid, and most of those expenses don't show up in the listing.

That leftover is your cash flow. Take the rent, then subtract the mortgage, property taxes,
insurance, and a few costs that are easy to forget: vacancy, repairs, and management. If the
number that remains is positive, the property pays you each month. If it's negative, you're paying
to own it, no matter how nice it looks on a tour.

## The screens investors actually use

The 1% rule is a fast first filter. If the monthly rent is close to 1% of the purchase price, the
deal is worth a closer look. A $200,000 property renting for around $2,000 clears it.

But it's a
screen, not a promise. Plenty of properties pass the 1% test and still lose money once you run the
full expenses, so treat it as a way to skip the obvious duds, not a green light. The 1% rule also
breaks down in pricier coastal markets, where almost nothing clears it; there you may be buying for
appreciation and tax benefits as much as monthly cash flow, which is a different game with different
risks.

Cap rate goes a step deeper. It's the net operating income, meaning the income after operating
expenses but before the mortgage, divided by the price. A property throwing off $12,000 of net
operating income on a $200,000 price has a 6% cap rate. It lets you compare two properties on the
same footing, and it tells you what kind of return the building itself produces before financing.
One caution: cap rate ignores your mortgage entirely, so two investors can buy the same building at
the same cap rate and have wildly different cash flow depending on how much they put down and what
rate they got. Use cap rate to compare buildings, and cash flow to judge your actual deal.

<Callout type="tip" title="Run two versions">
Run two versions of every deal: the seller's rosy numbers and your own conservative ones. Buy on
the conservative version. If the deal only works on the optimistic one, it doesn't really work.
</Callout>

## The expenses people skip

Here's where new investors get burned. They look at rent minus mortgage, see a fat margin, and buy.
Then reality shows up.

Vacancy is real. Even a good rental sits empty between tenants, so budget something like 5 to 8% of
the rent for the months no one's paying. Maintenance is constant, the steady drip of leaky faucets
and worn carpet. On top of that sit the big-ticket capital expenses: a roof, a furnace, a water
heater. Those don't come every year, but when they do they're thousands of dollars, and you want a
reserve already set aside.

Management is the last one people wave away. Even if you self-manage at first, price in something
like 8 to 10% of rent for a property manager. Your time has value, and someday you may not want to
take the 2 a.m. call. A deal that only works because you're working for free isn't a deal.

## A full deal, run honestly

Let's put real numbers on it. A duplex lists at $250,000 and rents for $2,500 total, so it clears
the 1% rule. You put 25% down ($62,500) and finance $187,500, and your principal and interest run
roughly $1,250 a month. Property taxes and insurance add another $500. That leaves $750, and a new
investor sees that and feels great.

Now subtract the costs that don't show on the listing. Vacancy at 7% is about $175. Maintenance at
5% is $125. Capital expense reserves at 7% is another $175. Even self-managing, set aside 8% for
management, call it $200. Those four lines total $675. Your real monthly cash flow is closer to $75,
not $750. On $62,500 invested, that's modest, and a single bad month can erase it.

That's not a reason to walk away. It's the reason to run the real numbers first, so the $75 is a
surprise you planned for instead of one that wrecks your year.

<Callout type="key" title="Cash flow lives in the hidden costs">
Rent minus mortgage is not your profit. Vacancy, maintenance, capital reserves, and management can
turn a $750 margin into $75. Run the conservative numbers before you offer, not after.
</Callout>

## What to do next

Before you make an offer, build the full expense picture from the conservative side: pull the actual
tax bill for the address (don't trust the listing's number, which often reflects the seller's older
assessment), get a real insurance quote, and ask a local property manager what they'd charge and
what vacancy really looks like on that block. Then talk to a lender about how much down payment and
what loan terms you're working with, because financing is half of whether the deal cash flows. If
the numbers hold up on the conservative version, you've got a deal worth chasing. If they only work
when everything goes perfectly, keep looking.
