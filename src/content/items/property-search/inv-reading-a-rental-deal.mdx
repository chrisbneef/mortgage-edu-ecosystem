A listing that says "rents for $1,800" tells you almost nothing about whether it's a good deal.
The rent is only the top line. What makes or breaks a rental is what's left after every expense
gets paid, and most of those expenses don't show up in the listing.

That leftover is your cash flow. Take the rent, then subtract the mortgage, property taxes,
insurance, and a few costs that are easy to forget: vacancy, repairs, and management. If the
number that remains is positive, the property pays you each month. If it's negative, you're paying
to own it, no matter how nice it looks on a tour.

## The screens investors actually use

The 1% rule is a fast first filter. If the monthly rent is close to 1% of the purchase price, the
deal is worth a closer look. A $200,000 property renting for around $2,000 clears it. But it's a
screen, not a promise. Plenty of properties pass the 1% test and still lose money once you run the
full expenses, so treat it as a way to skip the obvious duds, not a green light.

Cap rate goes a step deeper. It's the net operating income, meaning the income after operating
expenses but before the mortgage, divided by the price. A property throwing off $12,000 of net
operating income on a $200,000 price has a 6% cap rate. It lets you compare two properties on the
same footing, and it tells you what kind of return the building itself produces before financing.

<Callout type="tip">
Run two versions of every deal: the seller's rosy numbers and your own conservative ones. Buy on
the conservative version. If the deal only works on the optimistic one, it doesn't really work.
</Callout>

## The expenses people skip

Here's where new investors get burned. They look at rent minus mortgage, see a fat margin, and buy.
Then reality shows up.

Vacancy is real. Even a good rental sits empty between tenants, so budget something like 5 to 8% of
the rent for the months no one's paying. Maintenance is constant, the steady drip of leaky faucets
and worn carpet. On top of that sit the big-ticket capital expenses: a roof, a furnace, a water
heater. Those don't come every year, but when they do they're thousands of dollars, and you want a
reserve already set aside.

Management is the last one people wave away. Even if you self-manage at first, price in something
like 8 to 10% of rent for a property manager. Your time has value, and someday you may not want to
take the 2 a.m. call. A deal that only works because you're working for free isn't a deal.

Add all of that up and the picture changes. A property that looked like it cleared $400 a month
might actually clear $80 once vacancy, repairs, capital expenses, and management are in the math.
That's not a reason to walk away. It's the reason to run the real numbers first, so the $80 is a
surprise you planned for instead of one that wrecks your year.
