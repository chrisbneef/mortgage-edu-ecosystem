Your offer got accepted. Take a breath, because the hard part of finding a house is behind you. What comes next is mostly a sequence of steps that other people run for you, as long as you keep things moving on your end.

Most closings take 30 to 45 days from the day your offer is accepted. Some stretch to 60 if there's a tricky title issue or a busy appraiser. Knowing the rough shape of those weeks makes the whole thing feel a lot less mysterious, so here's how it usually plays out.

## The first two weeks

Right after your offer is accepted, a few things kick off at once. You deposit your earnest money, which is the good-faith cash that shows you're serious. Escrow opens to hold that money. The inspection gets scheduled. And you start sending documents to your lender: pay stubs, bank statements, tax returns, whatever they ask for.

The inspection happens in this window too. If the inspector finds something, you and the seller negotiate repairs or a credit. That back-and-forth can take a few days, so don't panic if it isn't instant. This is normal, and it's exactly why you have a contingency period.

## The middle stretch

Around week two to three, the lender orders the appraisal to confirm the home is worth what you agreed to pay. At the same time, an underwriter reviews your file line by line, the title company runs a title search to make sure no one else has a claim on the property, and you shop for homeowners insurance.

That's a lot happening in parallel, and most of it is out of your hands. Your job is to answer questions fast and pick your insurance policy so the lender can finish their checklist.

By week three to four you're clearing the last few conditions, the small items underwriting still wants. Once those are done, you get the words you've been waiting for: "clear to close."

## The final days

<GlossaryTerm slug="closing-disclosure">Closing Disclosure</GlossaryTerm> is a five-page form that lays out your final loan terms and exact cash to bring. By law it has to reach you at least three business days before you sign. Read it carefully and compare it to your earlier estimate. If a number looks off, ask right away.

Just before closing you'll do a final walkthrough to confirm the home is in the shape you expected and any agreed repairs got done. Then comes signing day, where you sign a stack of documents and the loan funds. After funding records, the home is yours.

<Callout type="warning">The single biggest cause of delay is a buyer who's slow to send documents back. Watch your email and reply the same day, even if it's just to say you're working on it.</Callout>

None of these weeks are set in stone, and yours might shuffle around a little. That's fine. If you keep your paperwork moving and your money boring, you'll be standing in your new kitchen sooner than you think.

<Disclaimer>Every transaction is different. Your lender and closing agent will confirm the exact dates that apply to you.</Disclaimer>
