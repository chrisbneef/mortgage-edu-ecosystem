Your offer got accepted. Take a breath, because the hard part of finding a house is behind you. What comes next is mostly a sequence of steps that other people run for you, as long as you keep things moving on your end.

Most closings take 30 to 45 days from the day your offer is accepted. Some stretch to 60 if there's a tricky title issue or a busy appraiser. Knowing the rough shape of those weeks makes the whole thing feel a lot less mysterious, so here's how it usually plays out.

## The first two weeks

Right after your offer is accepted, a few things kick off at once. You deposit your earnest money, which is the good-faith cash that shows you're serious. It usually runs 1 to 3% of the purchase price, so on a $350,000 home that's roughly $3,500 to $10,500, and it sits in escrow until closing, where it gets credited toward your down payment and costs. Escrow opens to hold that money. The inspection gets scheduled. And you start sending documents to your lender: pay stubs, bank statements, tax returns, whatever they ask for.

The inspection happens in this window too, usually for somewhere around $300 to $600 out of your pocket. If the inspector finds something, you and the seller negotiate repairs or a credit. That back-and-forth can take a few days, so don't panic if it isn't instant. This is normal, and it's exactly why you have a contingency period. Pay attention to the dates in your contract here. Your inspection and financing contingencies have hard deadlines, and if you let one slip past without acting, you can lose the right to walk away and get your earnest money back.

## The middle stretch

Around week two to three, the lender orders the appraisal to confirm the home is worth what you agreed to pay. At the same time, an underwriter reviews your file line by line, the title company runs a title search to make sure no one else has a claim on the property, and you shop for homeowners insurance.

That's a lot happening in parallel, and most of it is out of your hands. Your job is to answer questions fast and pick your insurance policy so the lender can finish their checklist.

This is also the stretch where a deal can wobble, so it helps to know the two usual culprits. If the appraisal comes in below your offer price, the lender will only finance off the lower number, which means you either renegotiate with the seller, cover the gap in cash, or walk if your contract lets you. And underwriting sometimes surfaces a "condition," a follow-up item like a sourced deposit or an updated pay stub, that you'll need to clear before things move on. Neither is a disaster. Both just need a quick, complete response.

By week three to four you're clearing the last few conditions, the small items underwriting still wants. Once those are done, you get the words you've been waiting for: "clear to close."

## The final days

<GlossaryTerm slug="closing-disclosure">Closing Disclosure</GlossaryTerm> is a five-page form that lays out your final loan terms and exact cash to bring. By law it has to reach you at least three business days before you sign. Read it carefully and compare it to the Loan Estimate you got earlier. Your interest rate, monthly payment, and the cash to close should line up closely. If a number jumped, ask right away. Be careful here too: a major change, like switching loan products or a big rate move, can reset that three-day clock and push your closing date.

Just before closing you'll do a final walkthrough to confirm the home is in the shape you expected and any agreed repairs got done. Then comes signing day, where you sign a stack of documents and the loan funds. Bring your down payment and closing costs as a wire or cashier's check (lenders rarely accept a personal check or cash at this stage), and bring a government photo ID. After funding records with the county, the home is yours.

<Callout type="warning" title="Reply the same day">The single biggest cause of delay is a buyer who's slow to send documents back. Watch your email and reply the same day, even if it's just to say you're working on it.</Callout>

## Keep your finances boring

One more thing that quietly sinks closings: changing your financial picture mid-process. Don't open a new credit card, finance a car, or make a big purchase on credit between offer and closing, because the lender re-checks your credit and debts near the end. A new $500 car payment can push your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">DTI</GlossaryTerm> over the line and cost you the loan. Don't move large sums between accounts without keeping records, don't change jobs if you can help it, and don't let a balance go past due.

<Callout type="key" title="Two things you control">Most of closing runs without you, but two habits keep it on track: send every document back fast, and keep your finances boring from offer to signing. Do those and the 30-to-45-day timeline takes care of itself.</Callout>

None of these weeks are set in stone, and yours might shuffle around a little. That's fine. If you keep your paperwork moving and your money boring, you'll be standing in your new kitchen sooner than you think.
