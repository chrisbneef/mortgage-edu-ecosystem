A lot of people assume that without a Social Security number, a mortgage is off the table. That's not true. If you have an ITIN, there are lenders who will work with you, and homeownership is genuinely within reach.

An ITIN is an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number. The IRS issues it to people who need to file U.S. taxes but can't get a Social Security number. It's a tax tool, not an immigration document, and many people who've been living and working here for years already have one. This guide is general education, not immigration or tax advice, and your status is a conversation for a qualified professional, not your loan officer.

## How an ITIN mortgage works

The idea is straightforward. Instead of pulling your file by Social Security number, the lender uses your ITIN. From there, the process rhymes with a regular mortgage: they look at your income, your savings, and your ability to repay. They're just doing it through a different door.

ITIN loans are a niche product. Most lenders don't offer them, which means you can't walk into any bank and expect a yes. The ones who do specialize in this kind of lending and understand the borrowers they're serving. Finding one of those lenders is the most important step.

## What it costs you

Because these loans are specialized, the terms are a little tougher than a standard mortgage.

Expect a larger down payment. Where a typical buyer might put down 5%, an ITIN borrower often needs more, sometimes in the range of 15% to 20% or higher depending on the lender. The rate tends to be higher too, since fewer lenders compete in this space and the loan sits outside the usual rulebook.

Let's put real numbers on it. On a $250,000 home, 15% down is $37,500 and 20% is $50,000, versus the $12,500 a 5% conventional buyer might bring. That's a meaningful gap, and it's the single thing most worth planning for early. The higher rate also nudges your monthly payment up compared to a standard loan on the same house, so when you estimate affordability, run the numbers with a cushion rather than the best case. Those terms aren't a punishment. They reflect a smaller market and a lender taking on a profile that conventional programs weren't built for. Knowing the numbers up front lets you plan instead of getting blindsided.

## What you'll need to show

The paperwork centers on proving two things: that you pay your taxes and that you earn a stable income.

You'll typically provide tax returns filed under your ITIN, often a couple of years' worth. You'll show proof of income, like pay stubs or, if you're self-employed, your business records. And because you may not have a traditional credit score, lenders often accept alternative credit.

<Callout type="info" title="What alternative credit is">
Alternative credit means a payment history outside the usual credit bureaus. Think rent paid on time for a year or two, plus utility bills, a phone account, or insurance. Save those records. They can stand in for a credit score and tell the lender you reliably pay what you owe.
</Callout>

## What Trips People Up

A few things catch ITIN borrowers off guard. The first is filing history. If you've under-reported income to keep your tax bill low, that low number is the income a lender can use, which can shrink how much home you qualify for. Reporting honestly over a couple of years before you apply usually serves you better here. The second is the alternative credit record. People who pay rent in cash or split utility bills informally often have nothing to show, even though they've paid reliably for years. Start a clean paper trail now: pay rent by check or transfer, keep your name on at least one utility, and hold onto the statements.

One more: an ITIN loan is a real mortgage with a real lien on your home. That's a feature, not a catch, but it means the same rules apply about keeping up payments, taxes, and insurance. Treat it as seriously as any other loan, because the stakes are the same.

## What to Do Next

ITIN mortgages are a well-worn road for immigrant families. Plenty of households that have built a life here, raised kids, and run businesses use exactly this kind of loan to buy a home. You're not asking for something unusual. You're using a product designed for your situation.

The work on your end is mostly about preparation and finding the right lender. Get a couple of years of tax returns filed under your ITIN, keep proof of your rent and utility payments, and put together a record of your income. Then shop specifically for lenders who advertise ITIN loans, and ask how many they close.
