In this episode we walk through the five loan programs you're most likely to meet and, more
importantly, who each one actually fits. The names get thrown around like everyone already knows the
difference, but most buyers don't, and picking the wrong starting point can cost you real money. Here
are the show notes if you'd rather read than listen, or want to come back to a point later.

## The big idea: there's no "best" loan

The single most useful thing to take away from this episode is that loan programs aren't ranked from
worst to best. They're built for different situations. A loan that's perfect for a veteran with no
down payment savings is useless to a buyer in a pricey suburb borrowing well over the local limit.
The whole game is matching the program to your credit, your cash, and where you're buying. Keep those
three levers in mind as we go, because almost every difference between these loans traces back to one
of them.

## Conventional: the default for strong files

Conventional is the loan most people end up with. It isn't backed by a government program, which means
it follows the rules set by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. If you've got solid credit and some savings,
it's usually the cleanest path. You can put down as little as 3 percent on some programs, though
anything under 20 percent down means you'll pay private mortgage insurance, or
<GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm>, until you build enough equity.

The big advantage over the government loans is that PMI drops off once you reach about 20 percent
equity. You don't carry it for the life of the loan. So conventional tends to win over time for
buyers who can clear the credit bar and put down a reasonable chunk.

## FHA: flexible when credit or cash is tight

FHA is the program that bends the most. Lower credit scores, a higher debt load, a thin down payment:
FHA was built for exactly these situations. You can get in with as little as 3.5 percent down, and the
credit requirements are noticeably more forgiving than conventional.

The trade-off is the mortgage insurance. FHA charges an upfront premium plus a monthly one, and on a
low-down-payment loan that monthly premium typically sticks around for the life of the loan unless you
refinance out of it later. So FHA is often the loan that gets you in the door, with the plan to
refinance into something cheaper once your credit and equity improve. That's a completely normal path,
not a failure.

## VA: the standout for those who've served

If you're an eligible veteran, active-duty service member, or in many cases a surviving spouse, the VA
loan is hard to beat. Zero down payment, no monthly mortgage insurance at all, and competitive rates.
There's a one-time funding fee, which can often be rolled into the loan, and some borrowers with
service-connected disabilities are exempt from it entirely.

The catch is simply eligibility. It runs through your service record, not your credit or your
location. If you qualify, this is usually the first loan to look at, because skipping both the down
payment and the monthly insurance is a meaningful long-term saving.

## USDA: zero down in the right zip code

USDA is the other zero-down option, aimed at low and moderate income buyers in eligible areas. The
"rural" label is a little misleading. Plenty of ordinary suburbs qualify, not just farmland. The two
gates are location and income: the home has to sit in a USDA-eligible area, and your household income
has to fall under the limit for your region and family size.

Instead of mortgage insurance, USDA charges a guarantee fee that's usually lighter than FHA's. If you
clear both gates, the no-money-down structure is genuinely tough to beat. The mistake people make is
assuming they earn too much or live too close to a city to qualify, then never checking the map.

## Jumbo: when the loan amount is the issue

Jumbo isn't about a fancy house. It's any loan larger than the conforming limit in your county. Cross
that line and you're in jumbo territory, which means stricter rules: a higher credit score, a lower
debt-to-income ratio, a bigger down payment, and cash reserves the lender wants to see sitting in your
accounts after you close. In expensive markets, jumbo loans are routine, and the rates are often
competitive for strong borrowers, so don't read "jumbo" as a penalty.

## What to do after this episode

Don't try to pick your program in a vacuum. Start with the three levers. Check whether your target
area is USDA-eligible, confirm whether you have VA eligibility, and pull your credit so you know
where you stand. That narrows the field fast. From there, listen above for the full walk-through, then
read the matching guide in each track for the details that apply to your situation. When you talk to a
loan officer, you'll be comparing real options instead of nodding along to names.
