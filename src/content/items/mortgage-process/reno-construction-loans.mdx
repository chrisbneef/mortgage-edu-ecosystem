Building a home from scratch doesn't work like buying one. There's no house to lend against yet, just a lot, a set of plans, and a builder. So the financing works differently too. A construction loan funds the build in stages, paying for the work as it gets done rather than handing over one lump sum at the start.

## Draws: paying as the house goes up

The money comes out in pieces called draws. Each draw is tied to a milestone: the foundation goes in, then the framing, then the roof, then the systems, and so on. As each stage finishes and gets inspected, the lender releases that portion of the funds to keep the build moving.

This is also why your payments during construction look unusual. You usually pay interest only on the amount that's actually been drawn so far, not the full loan. Early on, when only the foundation is funded, the interest is small. It grows as more of the home gets built and more money is released. That keeps your carrying cost manageable while you're not yet living in the place.

## One-time close vs. two closes

Here's the part worth understanding before you start, because it affects your costs and your paperwork.

A construction-to-permanent loan, often called a one-time close, is the cleaner setup. You close once, the loan funds the build through draws, and when the home is finished it converts into a regular mortgage. One set of closing costs, one signing, one rate locked at the start. For a lot of people, that simplicity is the whole appeal.

A two-close setup splits it in two. You take out a short-term construction loan to fund the build, and then, once the home is done, you get a separate permanent loan to pay off the construction loan and carry you long-term. That means two closings and two sets of costs. It can offer flexibility in certain situations, but it's more moving parts and more expense.

<Callout type="info">A one-time close means a single closing and the loan rolls into your permanent mortgage automatically. A two-close means a construction loan now and a separate mortgage later, with closing costs both times.</Callout>

## How you qualify

Since the house doesn't exist yet, the lender can't appraise what's there. Instead, they qualify you based on the plans and the as-completed value: what the finished home is expected to be worth once it's built to spec. The builder's contract, the blueprints, and the cost breakdown all feed into that number. Your credit, income, and the size of the loan against that projected value all factor in, the same as any mortgage.

Expect more scrutiny than a standard purchase. The lender wants a qualified, vetted builder, a detailed budget, and a realistic timeline before they commit. They're funding something that doesn't exist yet, so they want confidence it'll get finished.

## Plan for things going sideways

Construction almost never lands exactly on the original number. Materials cost more than quoted, a hidden problem turns up when they dig, a change you make mid-build adds expense. The smart move is to build a contingency reserve into the budget from the start, a cushion of extra funds set aside specifically for overruns.

Without that buffer, a surprise can stall the whole project while you scramble for cash. With it, you absorb the bump and keep going. Talk through how much contingency to set aside with your lender and builder early, and treat the timeline they give you as a hopeful estimate rather than a promise. Builds run long more often than they run short.
