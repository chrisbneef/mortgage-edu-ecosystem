Building a home from scratch doesn't work like buying one. There's no house to lend against yet, just a lot, a set of plans, and a builder. So the financing works differently too. A construction loan funds the build in stages, paying for the work as it gets done rather than handing over one lump sum at the start.

## Draws: paying as the house goes up

The money comes out in pieces called draws. Each draw is tied to a milestone: the foundation goes in, then the framing, then the roof, then the systems, and so on. As each stage finishes and gets inspected, the lender releases that portion of the funds to keep the build moving. A typical build runs on something like four to six draws, each one verified before the next dollar moves.

This is also why your payments during construction look unusual. You usually pay interest only on the amount that's actually been drawn so far, not the full loan. Early on, when only the foundation is funded, the interest is small. It grows as more of the home gets built and more money is released.

A quick example makes it concrete. Say your total construction loan is $400,000. In month two, only $80,000 has been drawn for the foundation and initial framing. At a rough 7% rate, you're paying interest on that $80,000, which works out to around $467 for the month, not interest on the full $400,000. By month eight, with $320,000 drawn, that monthly interest climbs to roughly $1,867. Your payment ramps up alongside the house. That structure keeps your carrying cost manageable while you're often still paying rent somewhere else.

## One-time close versus two closes

Here's the part worth understanding before you start, because it affects your costs and your paperwork.

A construction-to-permanent loan, often called a one-time close, is the cleaner setup. You close once, the loan funds the build through draws, and when the home is finished it converts into a regular mortgage. One set of closing costs, one signing, one rate locked at the start. For a lot of people, that simplicity is the whole appeal. The trade-off is that you lock your permanent rate early, before the build is done, so if rates fall during construction you may not capture the drop without a renegotiation clause.

A two-close setup splits it in two. You take out a short-term construction loan to fund the build, and then, once the home is done, you get a separate permanent loan to pay off the construction loan and carry you long-term. That means two closings and two sets of costs, often several thousand dollars in the second round. It can offer flexibility, including the chance to shop your permanent rate once the home is finished, but it's more moving parts, more expense, and the risk that you have to requalify if your finances changed mid-build.

<Callout type="info" title="One close or two">A one-time close means a single closing and the loan rolls into your permanent mortgage automatically. A two-close means a construction loan now and a separate mortgage later, with closing costs both times.</Callout>

## How you qualify

Since the house doesn't exist yet, the lender can't appraise what's there. Instead, they qualify you based on the plans and the as-completed value: what the finished home is expected to be worth once it's built to spec. The builder's contract, the blueprints, and the cost breakdown all feed into that number. Your credit, income, and the size of the loan against that projected value all factor in, the same as any mortgage.

Expect more scrutiny than a standard purchase. The lender wants a qualified, vetted builder, a detailed budget, and a realistic timeline before they commit. They're funding something that doesn't exist yet, so they want confidence it'll get finished. Down payment expectations often run higher than a standard purchase too, and many lenders count the value of land you already own toward your equity.

## What trips people up

A few things surprise first-time builders. People assume they can lock a great rate and forget it, but on a one-time close that rate is set early, well before move-in. They underestimate the requalification risk on a two-close, where a job change or new debt during the build can complicate the second loan. And many forget they're often paying rent or an existing mortgage at the same time as the construction-loan interest, a double carry that can stretch a budget thin if the build runs long.

This also isn't the loan for buying a finished spec home from a builder; that's a standard purchase. Construction loans are for ground-up builds and sometimes major rebuilds, where the home genuinely doesn't exist yet.

## Plan for things going sideways

Construction almost never lands exactly on the original number. Materials cost more than quoted, a hidden problem turns up when they dig, a change you make mid-build adds expense. The smart move is to build a contingency reserve into the budget from the start, a cushion of extra funds set aside specifically for overruns. Many builders and lenders suggest somewhere around 5% to 10% of the build cost.

Without that buffer, a surprise can stall the whole project while you scramble for cash. With it, you absorb the bump and keep going. Talk through how much contingency to set aside with your lender and builder early, treat the timeline they give you as a hopeful estimate rather than a promise, and confirm in writing who covers overruns before the first shovel hits the ground. Builds run long more often than they run short.

<Callout type="key" title="The takeaway">
A construction loan funds the build in inspected draws and you pay interest only on what's drawn. Decide between one-time and two closes up front, set aside a 5% to 10% contingency, and plan for a longer timeline than quoted.
</Callout>
