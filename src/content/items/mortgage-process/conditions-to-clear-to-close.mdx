You got a "conditional approval" and your stomach dropped a little at the word conditional. Don't let it. This is good news with a short to-do list attached. It means the underwriter has reviewed your file and is ready to approve your loan once you hand over a few specific items.

Think of it like a teacher saying you passed, just turn in the last worksheet. The loan is on track. You're filling in blanks, not starting over.

## What a condition actually is

A condition is a request for one piece of evidence the underwriter needs to feel confident. They aren't trying to trip you up. They're checking that the picture you painted in your application still holds together on paper.

The common ones show up again and again. An updated pay stub or bank statement, because the older ones aged out. A letter of explanation for a large deposit, or for a gap in your address or employment history. Proof of homeowners insurance for the new place. A gift letter, if family is helping with the down payment. Proof of where your down-payment money came from. Sometimes a copy of a divorce decree, a tax return, or an explanation of a credit inquiry.

Most of these you can pull together in an afternoon. A few might need a quick email to your employer, your insurance agent, or whoever sent you that gift, so start those early because they depend on someone else's timing, not yours.

## A real example of how it flows

Say your underwriter sends three conditions on a Monday: an updated pay stub, a gift letter for the $10,000 your parents sent, and proof of insurance for the new house. You email your parents the gift letter template that morning, call your insurance agent to bind a policy and send the declarations page, and pull your latest pay stub from your payroll portal.

By Tuesday afternoon all three are back in. The underwriter reviews them Wednesday, accepts the pay stub and insurance, but notices the gift letter is missing a signature. That's a fresh condition. You get the signature that evening, resend it Thursday morning, and the file clears. What could have been a two-week back-and-forth took four days because you moved fast and read each request carefully.

## What trips people up

The two big delays both come from rushing without reading. The first is sending a similar document instead of the exact one: last quarter's bank statement when they asked for the most recent, page one of three when they need all three, a screenshot when they need the full PDF with your name and account number visible. Each near-miss bounces back and costs a day or two.

The second is making the file moot by changing your finances mid-stream. While conditions are open, the underwriter is verifying a snapshot. If you open a credit card, move a large sum between accounts, or change jobs, you can reopen questions you already answered or trigger brand-new ones.

<Callout type="warning" title="Freeze Your Finances">While your conditions are open, freeze your finances. No new credit accounts, no moving large sums between banks, no job change. Any of those can reset the clock or reopen questions you already settled.</Callout>

If a condition confuses you, ask your loan officer to translate it instead of guessing. One clear question saves a week of mismatched documents. And don't go quiet if a condition is hard to satisfy; tell your loan officer early so they can find another way to document it.

## How conditions differ by situation

Not every borrower gets the same list. Self-employed buyers often face more conditions around income, since the underwriter may want tax returns, profit-and-loss statements, or proof a business is still active. Buyers using gift funds get extra scrutiny on where that money came from. And large or irregular deposits almost always draw a question, because the lender has to confirm the money is yours and not an undisclosed loan. None of this means you're in trouble; it just means your situation has a few more blanks to fill.

## Getting to clear to close, and what to do next

Once every condition is satisfied, your file moves to "clear to close." That's the green light. The lender can prepare your final documents and lock in your closing date.

There's a simple pattern worth remembering: the faster you answer, the faster you close. Underwriters work through a queue, and a file that responds quickly keeps its spot. A file that goes quiet drifts to the bottom while everyone waits on you. So keep your phone close for the next couple of weeks, set up the documents you can guess you'll need (recent pay stubs, bank statements, insurance contact) before they're even asked, and treat each new request like a small fire to put out today, not a chore for the weekend. Clear them quickly and the keys come quickly too.
