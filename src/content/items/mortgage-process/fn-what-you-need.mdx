Once you know a non-resident can finance a U.S. home, the next question is the practical one: what does it actually take to qualify? The honest answer is that the bar is higher than it is for a citizen with local credit, but it's very reachable if you come prepared.

Let's walk through what a lender wants to see.

## The down payment is the big one

Plan on putting more money down. Where a citizen might buy with 5% or 10%, non-resident buyers usually start around 20%. Depending on the lender and your situation, it can climb to 30% or even 40%.

That sounds like a lot, and it is. But there's logic to it. A bigger down payment lowers the lender's risk, and that's exactly what helps them say yes to a borrower whose history sits outside the U.S. system. On a $400,000 home, 30% down is $120,000. Worth knowing early so the number doesn't surprise you.

## Proof you're here legally

Lenders want to see that your presence in the country is legal and documented. Usually that means a valid visa or similar paperwork showing your status. This isn't the lender playing immigration officer. It's a basic step that protects everyone in the deal and confirms you are who your application says you are.

## Reserves: money left over after closing

This one surprises people. Beyond the down payment and closing costs, lenders want to see reserves, meaning extra savings that could cover several months of mortgage payments if your income hit a rough patch.

If your monthly payment including taxes and insurance is $2,500, and the lender wants six months of reserves, that's $15,000 sitting in an account after you've paid for everything else. Reserves tell the lender you have a cushion, which matters more when your financial life is partly overseas.

<Callout type="info">
Good news on credit: with the right program, not having a U.S. credit history is often fine. Many foreign national lenders expect it. They lean on your down payment, reserves, and documented income instead of a domestic credit score.
</Callout>

## Documenting income and assets

You'll need to show where your money comes from and where it's parked, and yes, foreign income and foreign assets can count. Expect to provide pay records, bank statements, and tax documents. Because some of this comes from abroad, it can take more effort to gather and verify, which is why we cover it in its own guide.

The theme is the same throughout: paper trail. A lender saying yes to a non-resident is leaning on documentation rather than a familiar credit profile, so clean, complete records do a lot of the heavy lifting.

## A couple of other practical pieces

Rates run higher for non-resident loans, often by a few percentage points compared to what a citizen would get. It reflects the added risk and the specialty nature of the loan. Build that into your math when you estimate a monthly payment.

You'll also usually need a U.S. bank account. Closing costs, your down payment, and your monthly payments flow through the domestic banking system, so having an account set up here removes a lot of friction. Opening one early is a smart first move.

None of this is a wall. It's a checklist. Larger down payment, proof of legal status, reserves, documented income, a U.S. account, and a lender who does these regularly. Line those up and you're in strong shape.
