Once you know a non-resident can finance a U.S. home, the next question is the practical one: what does it actually take to qualify? The honest answer is that the bar is higher than it is for a citizen with local credit, but it's very reachable if you come prepared. This is general education, not financial, tax, or immigration advice, and the exact requirements shift from lender to lender.

Let's walk through what a lender wants to see.

## The down payment is the big one

Plan on putting more money down. Where a citizen might buy with 5% or 10%, non-resident buyers usually start around 20%. Depending on the lender and your situation, it can climb to 30% or even 40%.

That sounds like a lot, and it is. But there's logic to it. A bigger down payment lowers the lender's risk, and that's exactly what helps them say yes to a borrower whose history sits outside the U.S. system. On a $400,000 home, 30% down is $120,000. Worth knowing early so the number doesn't surprise you. And remember the down payment isn't the whole bill. Closing costs typically add a few percent of the price on top, so on that same home you might need closer to $135,000 to $140,000 all in before you even think about reserves.

## Proof you're here legally

Lenders want to see that your presence in the country is legal and documented. Usually that means a valid visa or similar paperwork showing your status. This isn't the lender playing immigration officer. It's a basic step that protects everyone in the deal and confirms you are who your application says you are. If you're a true non-resident buying from abroad, the documentation looks different, often a passport and supporting paperwork rather than a U.S. visa, since you're not living here at all.

## Reserves: money left over after closing

This one surprises people. Beyond the down payment and closing costs, lenders want to see reserves, meaning extra savings that could cover several months of mortgage payments if your income hit a rough patch.

If your monthly payment including taxes and insurance is $2,500, and the lender wants six months of reserves, that's $15,000 sitting in an account after you've paid for everything else. Reserves tell the lender you have a cushion, which matters more when your financial life is partly overseas. Stack that on the example above and the full picture comes into focus: roughly $120,000 down, plus closing costs, plus $15,000 in reserves. Seeing the whole number now beats discovering it two weeks before closing.

<Callout type="info" title="No U.S. credit history is often fine">
Good news on credit: with the right program, not having a U.S. credit history is often fine. Many foreign national lenders expect it. They lean on your down payment, reserves, and documented income instead of a domestic credit score.
</Callout>

## Documenting income and assets

You'll need to show where your money comes from and where it's parked, and yes, foreign income and foreign assets can count. Expect to provide pay records, bank statements, and tax documents. Because some of this comes from abroad, it can take more effort to gather and verify, which is why we cover it in its own guide.

The theme is the same throughout: paper trail. A lender saying yes to a non-resident is leaning on documentation rather than a familiar credit profile, so clean, complete records do a lot of the heavy lifting.

## What Trips People Up

The most common stumble is underestimating cash to close, because people anchor on the down payment alone and forget closing costs and reserves on top. The second is timing. Money sitting in a foreign account often needs to be transferred to a U.S. account and "seasoned," meaning it sits there long enough for the lender to verify it's really yours, so moving funds at the last minute can stall a closing. A third is assuming a higher down payment erases every other requirement. It helps a lot, but you'll still need documented income and a clean source for your funds.

## What to Do Next

Rates run higher for non-resident loans, often by a percentage point or more compared to what a citizen would get. It reflects the added risk and the specialty nature of the loan. Build that into your math when you estimate a monthly payment, and lean toward the higher end when you're testing whether a payment fits your budget.

You'll also usually need a U.S. bank account. Closing costs, your down payment, and your monthly payments flow through the domestic banking system, so having an account set up here removes a lot of friction. Opening one early, and moving your down payment and reserves into it well ahead of time, is one of the smartest first moves you can make.

<Callout type="key" title="It's a checklist, not a wall">
Line up six things and you're in strong shape: a larger down payment, proof of legal status, reserves, documented income, a U.S. bank account, and a lender who does these regularly. Budget for closing costs and reserves on top of the down payment so the cash to close doesn't surprise you.
</Callout>

None of this is a wall. It's a checklist. Larger down payment, proof of legal status, reserves, documented income, a U.S. account, and a lender who does these regularly. Line those up and you're in strong shape.
