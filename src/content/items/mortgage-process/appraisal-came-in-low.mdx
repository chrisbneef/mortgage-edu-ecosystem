You agreed to pay $400,000. The appraisal came back at $385,000. Now there's a $15,000 hole in the middle of your deal, and it feels like the whole thing might fall apart. It usually doesn't. A low appraisal is a problem with several solutions, and knowing them ahead of time keeps you steady.

First, what the appraisal actually is. It's the lender's independent opinion of what the home is worth, done by a licensed appraiser who has no stake in your deal. The lender will only lend against that number, not against the price you and the seller shook hands on. So when the appraisal comes in low, the lender's loan shrinks, and you're left covering the difference somehow.

## Your four real options

You have more room to move than it feels like in the first hour. Here's what's usually on the table:

- **Renegotiate the price.** Ask the seller to come down toward the appraised value. They may not love it, but they know the next buyer's lender will likely see the same number.
- **Cover the gap in cash.** If you have it and you love the house, you can pay the difference out of pocket on top of your down payment.
- **Ask for a reconsideration of value.** This is a formal appeal to the appraiser, where your lender submits better comparable sales the appraiser may have missed. It doesn't always work, but a strong comp can move the number.
- **Walk away.** If you have an appraisal contingency in your contract, you can usually exit and get your earnest money back.

Often the answer is a blend. The seller drops the price $7,000, you cover $8,000, and the deal closes in the middle. A gap is a negotiation, not a verdict.

## Why this happens more than you'd think

In a fast-moving market where prices are climbing, appraisals lag behind. The appraiser is looking at homes that sold a month or two ago, while today's buyers are paying more. That mismatch produces low appraisals regularly, so don't read it as a sign you overpaid or made a mistake.

<Callout type="tip">Decide your gap plan before the appraisal even comes back. Know your cash ceiling and how much you'd ask the seller to absorb. Buyers who've thought it through stay calm and negotiate better than buyers caught flat-footed.</Callout>

One practical note: the buyer typically pays for the appraisal, often a few hundred dollars rolled into your closing costs. That's already spent, so the question now is simply how to bridge the difference.

Stay calm and treat the number as a starting point for a conversation. Most low appraisals get worked out with a little give from each side, and you end up in the house anyway.

<Disclaimer>Appraisal contingency rights and reconsideration processes vary by lender and contract. Talk to your loan officer and agent about your specific options.</Disclaimer>
