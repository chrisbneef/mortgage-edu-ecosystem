You agreed to pay $400,000. The appraisal came back at $385,000. Now there's a $15,000 hole in the middle of your deal, and it feels like the whole thing might fall apart. It usually doesn't. A low appraisal is a problem with several solutions, and knowing them ahead of time keeps you steady instead of panicked.

First, what the appraisal actually is. It's the lender's independent opinion of what the home is worth, done by a licensed appraiser who has no stake in your deal. The lender will only lend against that number, not against the price you and the seller shook hands on. Here's the part that surprises people: a low appraisal doesn't automatically shrink your loan dollar for dollar. The lender lends a percentage of the lower of the price or the appraised value. So if you're putting 20% down, the loan is based on $385,000 instead of $400,000, and the gap lands on you as extra down payment, not as a smaller loan you can shrug off.

## Your four real options

You have more room to move than it feels like in the first hour. Here's what's usually on the table.

Renegotiate the price. Ask the seller to come down toward the appraised value. They may not love it, but they know the next buyer's lender will likely see the same number, so they'd be back here with someone else in a few weeks.

Cover the gap in cash. If you have it and you love the house, you can bring the difference to closing on top of your down payment. Just be careful not to drain the reserves your lender wants to see you keep.

Ask for a reconsideration of value. This is a formal appeal where your lender submits better comparable sales, or points out factual errors, the appraiser may have missed. It doesn't always work, but a strong, recent, nearby comp can move the number.

Walk away. If you have an appraisal contingency in your contract, you can usually exit and get your earnest money back.

Often the answer is a blend. The seller drops the price $7,000, you cover $8,000, and the deal closes in the middle. A gap is a negotiation, not a verdict.

## A quick worked example

Picture that $400,000 contract with 10% down. You planned on a $40,000 down payment and a $360,000 loan. The appraisal lands at $385,000. The lender now sizes the loan off $385,000, so your loan caps lower than you expected, and the $15,000 difference between price and value has to come from you in cash if the seller won't budge.

Say the seller agrees to drop to $393,000 to keep the deal alive. The gap shrinks to $8,000. You decide you can cover $8,000 from savings without touching your emergency fund. You close, you're in the house, and the only real change is a slightly bigger check at the table. That's how most of these actually end.

## When the rules are different

A couple of edge cases are worth knowing. If you waived your appraisal contingency to win a competitive bid, walking away cleanly may not be an option, and your earnest money could be at risk. Read your contract or ask your agent before you assume you can exit.

Cash buyers can ignore the appraisal entirely, since there's no lender setting the number. And with VA or FHA loans, a low appraisal can carry extra weight because the value gets tied to the property for a set period, which can affect the next buyer who uses the same loan type. That sometimes gives you more room to renegotiate, since the seller can't easily escape the number.

## Why this happens more than you'd think

In a fast-moving market where prices are climbing, appraisals lag behind. The appraiser is looking at homes that closed a month or two ago, while today's buyers are paying more. That mismatch produces low appraisals regularly, so don't read it as a sign you overpaid or made a mistake. It can also happen with unique homes that have few good comparables, or after a bidding war pushed the price above what recent sales support.

<Callout type="tip" title="Have a Gap Plan Ready">Decide your gap plan before the appraisal even comes back. Know your cash ceiling and how much you'd ask the seller to absorb. Buyers who've thought it through stay calm and negotiate better than buyers caught flat-footed.</Callout>

## What to do next

Don't react in the first hour. Get a copy of the appraisal report and read why the value came in where it did, then talk to your agent and loan officer the same day. Ask whether the comps used were recent, nearby, and similar in size and condition, because that's where a reconsideration of value gets its footing.

One practical note: the buyer typically pays for the appraisal, often a few hundred dollars folded into closing costs. That money's already spent, so the question now is simply how to bridge the difference. Treat the number as a starting point for a conversation, not a final verdict. Most low appraisals get worked out with a little give from each side, and you end up in the house anyway.
