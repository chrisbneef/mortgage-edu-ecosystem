An FHA loan is a mortgage insured by the federal government, and it exists for one main reason: to help people buy homes who might get turned away from a conventional loan. If your down payment is small, or your credit has a few bruises, this is often the loan that opens the door.

The headline number is the down payment. With a <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">credit score</GlossaryTerm> of 580 or higher, you can put down just 3.5%. On a $300,000 home, that's about $10,500 instead of the $60,000 a 20% conventional down payment would ask for. If your score sits between 500 and 579, you can still qualify, but you'll need 10% down. Below 500, FHA isn't an option.

## Why It's More Forgiving

Conventional loans tend to want clean credit and a comfortable income-to-debt ratio. FHA loosens both. Lenders look at your debt-to-income more generously, so a car payment or some student loans won't sink your application as fast. Past credit trouble, even a bankruptcy or foreclosure a few years back, doesn't automatically disqualify you the way it might elsewhere.

That flexibility is the whole point. FHA was built to widen access, not to reward people who already have a thick financial cushion.

## The Trade-Off: Mortgage Insurance

Nothing's free, and here's the catch. FHA loans come with <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">mortgage insurance</GlossaryTerm>, and it works differently than the insurance on a conventional loan. You pay an upfront premium when you close, and then an annual premium that gets split into your monthly payment.

The part that surprises people: on most FHA loans, that annual premium sticks around for the life of the loan. It doesn't fall off automatically once you build equity. The usual way out is to refinance into a conventional loan later, after your credit and equity have grown. So the low entry cost can mean a higher long-term cost if you stay in the loan for years.

<Callout type="info">
FHA mortgage insurance and conventional PMI are not the same thing. Conventional PMI can usually be cancelled once you reach about 20% equity. FHA's annual premium typically can't, unless you refinance out of the loan entirely.
</Callout>

## A Few Practical Details

Loan limits matter. FHA caps how much you can borrow, and the ceiling changes by county. A pricey coastal area allows a much bigger FHA loan than a rural county does, so check the limit where you're actually buying.

The house itself has to pass muster, too. FHA requires the property to meet certain safety and condition standards, and an appraiser checks for them during the appraisal. Peeling paint, a bad roof, or no working heat can hold up your approval until the seller fixes the issue. This protects you, but it can complicate a deal on an older or neglected home.

One piece of good news: gift funds are allowed. The entire 3.5% down payment can come from a family member as a documented gift, which is a big help if your savings are thin but your relatives are willing to chip in.

## Is It Right For You?

FHA shines when you're early in your financial life, rebuilding credit, or simply short on cash for a big down payment. The cost of admission is low. The long-term insurance is the price you pay for that.

If you've got strong credit and 10% or 20% saved, run the numbers against a conventional loan before you commit. For plenty of first-time buyers, though, FHA is the most realistic path from renting to owning, and that's exactly what it was designed to be.
