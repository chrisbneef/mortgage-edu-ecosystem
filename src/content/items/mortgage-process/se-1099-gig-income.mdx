If you drive, deliver, freelance, contract, or get paid on 1099s, you might assume the mortgage world sees you the same as anyone with a paycheck. It doesn't, and knowing that early saves you a lot of frustration. To a lender, 1099 and gig income is self-employment income, full stop.

That label has real effects. It means you can't just hand over two recent pay stubs and call it done. Lenders want to see that your income is durable, so they ask for a longer look back and more supporting documents than a W-2 employee would ever need.

## The two-year expectation

Most lenders want to see a two-year history of your 1099 or gig income. The logic mirrors any self-employment case: one strong year could be a fluke, but two years shows the income is real and likely to continue. In some cases a one-year history can work if you have a longer track record in the same line of work, but two years is the safe assumption to plan around.

The good news for gig workers is that you don't have to pick one source. If you earn from three different platforms, lenders can combine that income into a single qualifying figure, as long as you can document each stream. A driver who also freelances and rents out equipment can pull all of it together, provided the paper trail holds up.

What helps your case most is the trend. Steady or rising income reads as stable, and stability is the entire point. Income that bounces hard from year to year, or trends downward, makes underwriters slow down and ask harder questions.

## How the income actually gets counted

This catches almost everyone off guard, so it's worth being blunt about it. Lenders usually qualify you on your net income, the number left after business deductions, not the gross you were paid. They also tend to average your two years.

Here's a worked example. Say you earned $90,000 in gross 1099 income last year, but you wrote off $30,000 in mileage, phone, equipment, and supplies, leaving $60,000 net. The year before, your net was $54,000. The lender averages those two: about $57,000 a year, or roughly $4,750 a month, is what they'll use to qualify you. Not the $90,000 you actually collected.

That gap between what you earned and what counts is the single biggest surprise for gig workers, and it's why aggressive write-offs in the year before you buy can quietly shrink how much house you qualify for.

## The documents that do the work

This is where you can stack the deck in your favor before you ever apply.

Start with your 1099s, since they're the official record of what each payer reported. Pair them with bank statements that show those payments actually landing in your account, which confirms the income is real and recurring. For some buyers, a profit-and-loss statement helps fill in the picture between tax filings, especially if your most recent year is stronger than your returns yet show. You'll typically also be asked for two years of full tax returns, since that's where the net number lives.

The thread running through all of it is clean records. Deposit everything you earn into a clear account, keep your business money separate from your grocery money, and don't let cash income vanish off the books. Income a lender can't trace is income a lender can't count, no matter how hard you actually worked for it.

<Callout type="tip" title="Use one account">Run every dollar of gig income through one dedicated account. When your deposits line up neatly with your 1099s, an underwriter can verify your income fast, and a fast verification is a friendly one.</Callout>

## A loan built for 1099 earners

If your tax returns make your income look thin after deductions, there's a non-QM product worth knowing about: the so-called 1099 loan.

Instead of digging through your full tax return, this loan can qualify you based on the totals reported on your 1099s, sometimes with an expense factor applied. That can produce a much higher qualifying income for a gig worker who writes off a lot, because it starts from what you were paid rather than what was left after deductions. Like other non-QM loans, it usually comes with a higher rate and a bigger down payment, so it's a tool for when the standard path won't work, not an automatic first pick. It also won't help if your real problem is a short history or shaky <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">DTI</GlossaryTerm>, since those still get scrutinized.

## What trips people up

Beyond the net-versus-gross surprise, a couple of habits hurt gig buyers. Switching the type of work right before applying resets the clock, because the lender wants to see history in your current line. Mixing personal and business money in one account makes an underwriter's job slow and turns a clean file into a messy one. And cash income that never hits a bank simply doesn't exist as far as the loan is concerned, so the money you kept off the books can't help you qualify.

## Put yourself in a strong spot

None of this requires fancy maneuvering. It's mostly about habits you can start today, well before you talk to a lender.

Keep two years of clean, traceable income flowing through a dedicated account. Hold onto your 1099s and your tax returns. Watch whether your income is steady or climbing, and if it's climbing, that's a strong moment to apply. If you're planning to buy soon, talk to your tax preparer about balancing deductions against the income you'll need to show, since the two pull in opposite directions. Do those few things, and you walk into the process with the exact paperwork that turns "self-employed and hard to read" into "documented and approvable."

<Callout type="key" title="The takeaway">
Lenders qualify 1099 and gig workers on two years of averaged net income, not gross. Keep clean records in one account and watch how aggressive write-offs shrink the income that counts.
