The interest rate gets all the attention. It's the number you see on billboards, the number your friend brags about, the number you text your loan officer about. But the rate alone doesn't tell you what a loan actually costs. For that, you want the <GlossaryTerm slug="apr">APR</GlossaryTerm>, and once you understand the difference between the two, you'll shop a lot smarter.

## Rate versus APR, and why they're different

Your interest rate sets your monthly principal-and-interest payment. That's its whole job. A lower rate means a lower payment, full stop.

The APR is broader. It folds most of your loan fees into a single percentage so you can compare two loans on more even footing. Lender fees, certain points, and some closing costs get baked in. The result is almost always a bit higher than the rate itself.

Here's a quick worked example. Say you're looking at a $300,000 loan over 30 years. Lender A offers a 6.5% rate but charges $9,000 in fees. Lender B offers 6.75% with only $1,500 in fees. Lender A has the better rate, so it looks like the winner. But once you spread those fees across the loan, Lender A's APR might land near 6.78% while Lender B's sits around 6.83%. They're much closer than the rate alone suggested, and depending on how long you keep the loan, the higher-rate, lower-fee option could actually cost you less.

That's the lesson: a low rate paired with high fees can be more expensive than a slightly higher rate with low fees. The APR exists to surface that.

## What APR doesn't catch

APR is useful, but it isn't perfect, and people lean on it too hard. It assumes you keep the loan for its full term. Most people don't. The average homeowner sells or refinances years before a 30-year loan runs out, and when you pay off early, those upfront fees never get spread across the full timeline the APR assumed. So a loan with a great APR but heavy upfront costs can quietly be the wrong choice if you only stay five or seven years.

APR also doesn't include every cost. Things like title insurance and appraisal fees often sit outside it, and different lenders calculate it slightly differently. Use it as one comparison tool, not the only one. The clearest apples-to-apples view comes from your <GlossaryTerm slug="closing-disclosure">Closing Disclosure</GlossaryTerm>, the standardized form that lays out every cost in the same order for every lender.

## Fixed versus adjustable

A fixed rate never changes. Your principal-and-interest payment is the same in year one and year thirty. That predictability is why most people choose fixed, especially when they plan to stay put.

An adjustable-rate mortgage, or ARM, starts with a lower introductory rate that's locked for a set period, often five, seven, or ten years, then adjusts on a schedule after that. The intro rate is tempting because it's usually cheaper than a comparable fixed rate. The risk is what happens when it adjusts: your rate can climb, and with it your payment. ARMs come with caps that limit how much it can move at each adjustment and over the life of the loan, but you're still taking on uncertainty in exchange for that early savings.

An ARM can make sense if you're confident you'll sell or refinance before the fixed period ends. It's riskier if your plans are fuzzy, because "I'll just refinance later" assumes rates and your finances will cooperate, and they don't always.

## Points, and the math that makes them worth it

Paying points means handing the lender money upfront to buy a lower rate. One point typically costs 1% of your loan amount and lowers your rate by something like a quarter of a percent, though the exact trade varies by lender and day.

The question is always the same: how long until the lower payment pays back what you spent? Picture that $300,000 loan again. One point costs $3,000. If buying it shaves your payment by about $45 a month, you divide $3,000 by $45 and get roughly 67 months, a little under six years, to break even. Stay past that point and the points were a good deal. Sell or refinance before it and you lost money.

<Callout type="tip" title="Check the break-even">
Before you pay points, ask your loan officer for the break-even month and compare it honestly to how long you actually plan to keep the loan.
</Callout>

## What trips people up

The most common mistake is rate tunnel vision, chasing the lowest advertised rate without asking what it costs to get there. That advertised number often assumes you're paying points, so you're not comparing the same thing across lenders.

Another trap is shopping rates on different days. Rates move daily, sometimes more than once a day, so quotes from Monday and Thursday aren't really comparable. Try to gather quotes within the same short window.

And people forget that a rate isn't yours until it's locked. A quote is just today's snapshot. Until you lock, it can change before you close.

## What to do next

Get written quotes from two or three lenders, ideally on the same day, and ask each for both the rate and the APR. Then look at the Loan Estimate they're required to give you and compare the fee sections line by line. Ask yourself how long you realistically plan to stay in the home, because that single answer shapes whether a low rate, a low APR, or paying points is the smarter move for you. There's no universally best loan, only the one that fits your timeline and your budget.

<Callout type="key" title="The takeaway">
The rate sets your payment, but the APR plus how long you'll keep the loan is what tells you the real cost. Compare both on the same day, and let your timeline pick the winner.
</Callout>
