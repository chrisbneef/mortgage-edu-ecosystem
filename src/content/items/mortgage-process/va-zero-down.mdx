Zero down sounds too good to be real, so people assume there's a catch hiding somewhere. With a VA loan there mostly isn't. Eligible buyers really can purchase a primary home with 0% down and no monthly mortgage insurance. Understanding why the program works that way helps you use it with confidence, and it helps you spot the spots where a little planning saves you money.

## Why lenders can offer it

A normal lender wants a down payment because it cushions their risk. If you put 10% down and the loan goes bad, they have a buffer before they lose money. The VA loan replaces that buffer with a government guarantee.

The VA promises the lender that it will cover part of the loan if you default. That guarantee, backed by your entitlement, gives the lender the same comfort a down payment would. So they can say yes to zero down on terms that would be impossible otherwise. You're not getting a riskier loan; you're getting one with a different kind of safety net.

There's also no monthly mortgage insurance, which is a real ongoing savings. On an FHA or low-down conventional loan, you'd typically pay monthly mortgage insurance until you build enough equity. VA skips that entirely. In its place is a one-time VA funding fee, a percentage of the loan that you can usually roll into the balance rather than pay in cash. The fee is lower if you've made a down payment, and it's commonly waived for veterans receiving compensation for a service-connected disability, so it's worth asking whether you're exempt.

## What "no loan limit" really means

If you have full entitlement, there's no official VA loan limit on how much you can borrow with zero down. That rule changed a few years back and it's a genuine benefit, especially in higher-cost areas.

There's one honest caveat. The VA doesn't cap you, but each lender still sets its own ceiling based on your income and credit. So the practical limit becomes what you can actually qualify and budget for, not an arbitrary number. If you've used part of your entitlement already, the math is different, and a down payment may come back into play on a larger purchase.

## Costs you'll still cover

Zero down doesn't mean zero dollars. You'll still have closing costs: things like the appraisal, title work, recording fees, and prepaid items that go into <GlossaryTerm slug="escrow">escrow</GlossaryTerm>. On a typical purchase these can run a few thousand dollars.

A quick example puts numbers on it. On a $350,000 home with zero down, your loan is the full $350,000, but you might still see closing costs and prepaids in the neighborhood of $9,000 to $14,000 depending on your taxes, insurance, and local fees. That's the money that catches people off guard when they've focused only on the "no down payment" headline.

The good news is you have help available. A seller can contribute toward your costs, and on a VA loan they can cover up to 4% of the value in concessions, which can go toward closing costs and even paying down certain debts. In a buyer-friendly market, asking for that help is normal and often successful. Between seller credits and lender options, plenty of veterans get to the closing table with very little out of pocket.

<Callout type="tip" title="Keep a cash reserve">
Set aside a cash reserve even if you're putting nothing down. Lenders like to see a few months of mortgage payments in savings, and you'll want a cushion for the move, repairs, and the surprises that come with any new home.
</Callout>

## The residual income test

Here's a piece of VA underwriting you won't find on most other loans, and it works in your favor. Alongside the usual <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">DTI</GlossaryTerm> calculation, the VA looks at your residual income.

Residual income is simply the money left over each month after your major bills are paid: your mortgage, taxes, insurance, other debts, and basic living costs for your household. The VA sets minimum residual amounts that vary by region and family size. The reason this matters to you is that it's a real-world test of whether you can comfortably afford the home, not just whether the ratios line up on paper. Many veterans who'd struggle to qualify elsewhere clear VA underwriting because this measure rewards a low debt load and steady income. It can also cut the other way: a buyer whose ratios look fine but whose leftover cash runs thin for a big household may get asked for more, which is the program protecting you from a payment that would squeeze you.

## Where it doesn't apply, and what to do next

A couple of limits are worth knowing up front. The VA loan is for a primary residence, so it won't finance a vacation home or a pure investment property. The home also has to pass the VA's minimum property requirements, an appraisal that checks the place is safe, sound, and sanitary, which can surface repair issues on older or distressed homes. And you'll need that Certificate of Eligibility to confirm your entitlement before a lender can run the zero-down math.

<Callout type="key" title="A guarantee, not a gimmick">
The VA loan isn't a gimmick. It trades a down payment for a government guarantee, skips monthly mortgage insurance, and checks affordability with the residual income test. Zero down still means budgeting for closing costs and a reserve.
</Callout>

So your next move is straightforward: confirm your eligibility, ask a VA-experienced lender to estimate your closing costs and whether your funding fee is waived, and budget for those costs plus a reserve even though the down payment is zero. Put it together and the VA loan isn't a gimmick. It's a carefully built program that trades a down payment for a guarantee, then checks affordability in a sensible way. Knowing how it works lets you plan your purchase around real numbers.
