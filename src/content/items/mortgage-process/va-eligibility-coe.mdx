The VA home loan is a benefit you earned through service. It is one of the few mortgages that lets eligible buyers purchase with nothing down and no monthly mortgage insurance. Before you shop for a home, it helps to know whether you qualify and how you'll prove it.

## Who qualifies

VA loans are open to a wider group than many people expect. Eligible veterans qualify, and so do active-duty service members. Many National Guard and Reserve members qualify too, depending on how long they've served. Some surviving spouses are eligible as well, often without the usual length-of-service requirements.

Eligibility comes down to the length and type of your service. A peacetime veteran and a wartime veteran may have different minimum service periods, and the rules have shifted over the years. The character of your discharge matters too: an honorable discharge keeps the benefit intact, while some other-than-honorable situations need a closer look or a discharge upgrade. If you're not sure where you land, that's normal. Your lender deals with these cases every day, and the answer is rarely a flat no. When the picture is genuinely unclear, the right move is to confirm with the VA directly rather than guess.

## Your Certificate of Eligibility

The document that proves your benefit is the Certificate of Eligibility, or COE. Think of it as the VA's confirmation that you've met the service requirements and are entitled to the loan guarantee.

You don't have to chase this paperwork on your own. Most lenders can pull your COE online in a matter of minutes through the VA's system, often before you've finished your first conversation with them. If you'd rather request it yourself, you can do that too. You'll generally need your DD-214 (your discharge paperwork) for a veteran request, and the VA's online portal or a mailed form (VA Form 26-1880) can get you there.

One thing worth knowing: the COE confirms that you have the benefit, but it does not, by itself, approve you for a loan. You still have to qualify on credit, income, and the lender's other standards. People sometimes treat the COE as a green light for the whole mortgage, then get surprised when underwriting still asks the usual questions. Think of the COE as your ticket to the door, not the approval waiting behind it.

<Callout type="tip" title="Still serving?">
If you're still serving, a statement of service signed by your command can stand in for the DD-214. Ask your personnel office before you start the loan process, so the paperwork is ready when your lender needs it.
</Callout>

## What entitlement actually means

When people talk about VA eligibility, the word "entitlement" comes up a lot. Your entitlement is the amount the VA guarantees to the lender on your behalf if you ever default. It's not money you receive, and it's not a credit limit on what you can borrow. It's the backstop that makes lenders comfortable offering zero down.

There are two pieces of it. Basic entitlement is a set baseline amount, and there's an additional bonus tier that scales with home prices in your area. Together they cover a meaningful share of a typical loan, which is exactly why a lender will hand you favorable terms without a down payment.

Here's where it gets practical. If you have your full entitlement available, there's no VA-imposed cap on how much you can borrow with zero down; the limit becomes whatever the lender will approve based on your income and credit. That's a change many borrowers haven't caught up with, since the old "loan limits" still get mentioned online. The limits still matter in one situation, though, which is when you've already got a VA loan tied up elsewhere.

## Using the benefit when you've used it before

A common worry is that the VA loan is a once-in-a-lifetime perk. It isn't. You can use it more than once, and you can have entitlement available even if you've used a VA loan in the past. Once you've paid off and sold a previous VA-financed home, your full entitlement can be restored for the next purchase. Restoration isn't always automatic, so you may need to request it and show the prior loan is paid off.

In some cases you can carry remaining (partial) entitlement and use it again before the first loan is paid off, which matters a great deal during a permanent change of station. With only partial entitlement left, county loan limits can come back into play and you might need a small down payment on the second home. The numbers get specific there, so walk through your exact situation with a lender who knows VA rules and confirm the details with the VA.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="Get your COE early">The COE proves you earned the benefit, but it isn't loan approval; you still qualify on credit and income. Find out your eligibility early and get your COE in hand before you ever make an offer. Pull together your DD-214 or statement of service, ask a VA-experienced lender to request the COE for you, and have them confirm how much entitlement you have available. That single step turns a vague benefit into a concrete plan, and it tells you whether you're looking at a true zero-down purchase or one where a little cash up front makes sense.
