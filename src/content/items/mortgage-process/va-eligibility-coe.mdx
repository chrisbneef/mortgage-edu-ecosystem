The VA home loan is a benefit you earned through service. It is one of the few mortgages that lets eligible buyers purchase with nothing down and no monthly mortgage insurance. Before you shop for a home, it helps to know whether you qualify and how you'll prove it.

## Who qualifies

VA loans are open to a wider group than many people expect. Eligible veterans qualify, and so do active-duty service members. Many National Guard and Reserve members qualify too, depending on how long they've served. Some surviving spouses are eligible as well, often without the usual length-of-service requirements.

Eligibility comes down to the length and type of your service. A peacetime veteran and a wartime veteran may have different minimum service periods, and the rules have shifted over the years. If you're not sure where you land, that's normal. Your lender deals with these cases every day, and the answer is rarely a flat no.

## Your Certificate of Eligibility

The document that proves your benefit is the Certificate of Eligibility, or COE. Think of it as the VA's confirmation that you've met the service requirements and are entitled to the loan guarantee.

You don't have to chase this paperwork on your own. Most lenders can pull your COE online in a matter of minutes through the VA's system, often before you've finished your first conversation with them. If you'd rather request it yourself, you can do that too. You'll generally need your DD-214 (your discharge paperwork) for a veteran request, and the VA's eBenefits portal or a mailed form can get you there.

<Callout type="tip">
If you're still serving, a statement of service signed by your command can stand in for the DD-214. Ask your personnel office before you start the loan process, so the paperwork is ready when your lender needs it.
</Callout>

## What entitlement actually means

When people talk about VA eligibility, the word "entitlement" comes up a lot. Your entitlement is the amount the VA guarantees to the lender on your behalf if you ever default. It's not money you receive, and it's not a credit limit on what you can borrow. It's the backstop that makes lenders comfortable offering zero down.

There are two pieces of it. Basic entitlement is a set baseline amount, and there's an additional bonus tier that scales with home prices in your area. Together they cover a meaningful share of a typical loan, which is exactly why a lender will hand you favorable terms without a down payment.

## Using the benefit when you've used it before

A common worry is that the VA loan is a once-in-a-lifetime perk. It isn't. You can still have entitlement available even if you've used a VA loan in the past. Once you've paid off and sold a previous VA-financed home, your full entitlement can be restored for the next purchase.

In some cases you can carry remaining entitlement and use it again before the first loan is paid off, which matters a great deal during a military move. The numbers get more specific there, so it's worth walking through your exact situation with a lender who knows VA rules.

For now, the takeaway is simpler. Find out your eligibility early, get your COE in hand, and you'll know what you're working with before you ever make an offer. That single step turns a vague benefit into a concrete plan.
