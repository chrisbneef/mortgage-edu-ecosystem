The financing that got you your first rental won't carry you to your tenth. Conventional loans are
great early on, but they were never designed for a growing portfolio, and at some point they tap
out. Knowing where that happens saves you from getting stuck mid-purchase.

The first wall is the property count. Conventional financing caps out at ten financed properties.
That sounds like plenty until you realize the rules tighten well before you get there. After your
fourth financed property, lenders ask for more: bigger reserves, larger down payments, and tougher
documentation. The loans are still available, but each one gets a little harder to land.

## Where reserves bite

Reserves are the quiet thing that slows people down. With one rental, a lender might want a few
months of payments in the bank. With several, those requirements stack, because now the lender is
looking at your exposure across the whole portfolio, not a single property.

Put numbers on it. Say each of your rentals carries a payment around $1,800 a month including taxes
and insurance. Six months of reserves on one is about $10,800. Once you own five and the lender
wants reserves against the whole portfolio, you could be looking at $50,000 or more sitting in the
bank, untouched, just to stay eligible for the next loan. That cash isn't earning you a return on a
property, and it's frequently the real ceiling on how fast you can grow, more than the down payments
themselves. Investors who don't plan for it hit a wall that has nothing to do with finding good
deals.

<Callout type="tip" title="Get Pre-Approved Before the Fifth">
Before you buy the property that pushes you past four financed loans, get pre-approved with the
tighter rules in mind. Finding out about the extra reserve requirement after you're under contract
is a bad time to learn it.
</Callout>

## Loans built for more doors

This is where investor-specific financing earns its keep. DSCR loans don't have a property-count cap
the way conventional loans do. Each property qualifies on its own rent, so you can keep buying past
the point where conventional lending shuts the door. For investors planning to hold a lot of doors,
that alone is the reason to learn how DSCR works.

A quick word on how the DSCR number works, since it drives everything. DSCR stands for debt service
coverage ratio, and it's just the property's monthly rent divided by its monthly payment (including
taxes, insurance, and any HOA). A property renting at $2,000 with a $1,600 payment has a ratio of
1.25, meaning rent covers the payment with 25% to spare. Most DSCR lenders want to see a ratio of
at least 1.0, and often 1.2 or higher for the best pricing. A ratio under 1.0 means the property
doesn't cover itself, and you'll either need a bigger down payment or a different deal.

There's also the portfolio loan, sometimes called a blanket loan. Instead of one loan per property,
it bundles several properties under a single loan. That can simplify your life, one payment instead
of five, and it can free up conventional slots you'd otherwise burn. The trade-off is that the
properties are tied together, so you'll want to understand how a release clause works before you
sign. A release clause spells out what happens when you sell one property out of the bundle: whether
you can free it from the blanket loan and on what terms. Without a clean release, selling a single
door can mean unwinding the whole loan.

## How you hold them

As the portfolio grows, a lot of investors stop holding properties in their own name and move them
into an LLC for liability separation. Conventional loans generally won't lend to an LLC, which is
another spot where they get in the way. DSCR loans usually will, which is part of why investors
gravitate toward them once an LLC is in the picture.

One caution here: moving a property you already financed into an LLC can technically trip a
due-on-sale clause, the lender's right to call the loan when title transfers. It's a real clause,
even if lenders rarely enforce it on a performing loan. If you're restructuring how you hold
properties, talk to both your lender and an attorney first rather than quietly moving title and
hoping nobody notices.

## What trips people up

The most common scaling mistake is buying right up to a limit without realizing the next deal
changes the rules. Someone closes their fourth conventional loan, finds a great fifth property, and
only then learns the reserve and down-payment requirements jumped. Another is assuming DSCR and
conventional are interchangeable. They price differently, and a DSCR loan usually carries a somewhat
higher rate, so it's worth keeping conventional slots for the deals that qualify and saving DSCR for
when you've used them up.

## What to do next

Map your runway before your next purchase. Count how many financed properties you have, know where
the count and reserve rules tighten, and ask a lender to quote both a conventional and a DSCR path
on your next deal. If an LLC is in your future, sort the financing question before you transfer
title, not after. The investors who scale smoothly aren't the ones who get surprised at property
number five. They're the ones who lined up DSCR or portfolio financing while they still had
conventional options open, so they were never stuck choosing between a good deal and a loan that
could actually close.
