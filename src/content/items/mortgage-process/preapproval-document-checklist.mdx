A pre-approval moves only as fast as your paperwork. Gather it ahead of time and you can get an answer the same day. Hunt for it piece by piece and you'll stretch a quick process into a frustrating week. The good news is the list is short and predictable, and most of it is paperwork you already have lying around or can download in a few clicks.

## The core documents almost everyone needs

Most lenders ask for the same starting set, no matter how you earn your money:

- Pay stubs from the last 30 days
- W-2 forms from the last 2 years
- Federal tax returns from the last 2 years
- Bank statements from the last 2 months
- A government photo ID
- A list of your monthly debt payments

That debt list is the one people skip, and it matters more than it looks. The lender uses it to figure your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income</GlossaryTerm> ratio, which is one of the biggest factors in how much they'll lend. Write down each minimum monthly payment: car loans, student loans, credit cards, child support, anything that shows up every month. You don't need to list utilities or groceries, just the debts a credit report would show.

You'll also give consent for the lender to pull your credit. That part is on them once you say yes, so there's nothing to prepare beyond agreeing to it. One thing worth knowing: a mortgage credit pull is a hard inquiry, but if you're rate-shopping across several lenders within a short window, the credit scoring models typically treat those as a single inquiry, so shopping around won't tank your score.

## If you're self-employed or work 1099

Steady W-2 income is the simplest case. If you run your own business or work as a contractor, plan to show a little more.

Self-employed buyers should expect to add a profit-and-loss statement and business tax returns on top of the personal ones. Gig and 1099 workers should pull together their 1099 forms and be ready to point to the matching deposits in their bank statements. Lenders want to see that the money you report actually landed in your account.

Here's the part that catches self-employed buyers off guard: lenders qualify you on your net income after deductions, not your gross. If you write off a lot to lower your tax bill, you also lower the income the lender will count, which can shrink how much you qualify for. It's not a reason to panic, but if you know you're buying soon, it's worth a conversation with your accountant before tax season about how aggressive those deductions should be.

None of this means you're harder to approve. It just means your income takes a few more pages to prove, so having those pages ready matters even more.

<Callout type="info" title="Keep It Fresh">Pay stubs older than about 30 days and bank statements older than about 60 days usually get rejected as stale. Grab fresh copies right before you apply.</Callout>

## The two things that trip people up

First, timing. Documents go stale. A pay stub from two months ago or a bank statement from last quarter will likely bounce back with a request for something current. Pulling everything the same week you apply keeps you out of that loop.

Second, large deposits. Any deposit that isn't your normal paycheck can raise a question, because lenders need to know it isn't borrowed money sneaking in. Say a relative gives you $10,000 toward your down payment. The lender will likely want a short gift letter saying it's a gift you don't have to repay, plus a record of the transfer. The same goes for a tax refund, the sale of a car, or a bonus that doesn't match your usual paycheck. Each one needs a short paper trail and a sentence explaining where it came from. Sort that out before they ask and you avoid an awkward delay.

One more edge case: if you've recently changed jobs or have gaps in your work history, gather a quick explanation and any offer letters. A career move in the same field is usually fine, but the lender will want the story documented rather than guessed at.

## What to do next

Build one folder, digital or physical, with all of this inside. Keep it updated as new pay stubs and statements come in, so it's always current rather than a scramble to refresh. Name the files clearly, something like "Pay Stub - March" beats "Scan_0042," because a tidy package is genuinely faster for an underwriter to work through. When you finally sit down with a lender, you'll hand over a complete set instead of scrambling, and that single habit is often the difference between a same-day answer and days of back-and-forth.

<Callout type="key" title="The Bottom Line">Gather the core documents into one current folder before you apply. A complete, fresh package is what turns a pre-approval into a same-day answer instead of a week of back-and-forth.</Callout>
