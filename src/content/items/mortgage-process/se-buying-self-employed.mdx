Working for yourself changes how a lender sees you, even if you make great money. You're not less qualified. You just get measured with a different ruler, and knowing that ruler ahead of time is the whole game.

First, figure out whether the lender will treat you as self-employed at all. You usually land in that bucket if you own 25% or more of a business, get paid on 1099s, or file a Schedule C. A salaried W-2 paycheck with a side gig is one thing. Running the show is another, and the second one means more paperwork.

## They want to see a track record

Most lenders want a two-year history of self-employment income. The idea is simple: one good year could be luck, but two years starts to look like a pattern they can count on.

There's some flexibility. If you recently went out on your own but spent years in the same field as an employee, some lenders accept one year of self-employment plus that prior experience. A graphic designer who freelanced after five years at an agency has a much easier case than someone who switched industries last spring.

Here's the part that trips people up. Lenders don't just take your best year. They average your income across the two years, and if the second year is lower than the first, they may use the lower number or a declining-income average. Earning $90,000 then $70,000 doesn't read as "$80,000 average." It reads as "headed down," and that makes them cautious.

<Callout type="tip">If this year is shaping up stronger than last year, it can pay to wait until that better year is on file. Rising income is one of the friendliest stories you can tell an underwriter.</Callout>

## The write-off problem

This one catches a lot of self-employed buyers off guard. Every deduction that lowers your tax bill also lowers the income a lender can count.

Say your business brings in $150,000, but after the home office, the mileage, the equipment, and everything else, your tax return shows $65,000 in net income. The lender qualifies you on that $65,000, not the $150,000 that actually hits your accounts. You did nothing wrong. You just optimized for low taxes, and now you're asking the same paperwork to prove a high income.

If you know you'll buy in the next year or two, this is worth a conversation with your accountant. Easing off aggressive write-offs costs you a little more in tax now and can mean a much bigger approval later.

## Two roads to a yes

When you're ready, you'll generally choose between two paths.

The first is a conventional loan built on your tax returns. It usually has the best rates, and it works well when your returns show solid income after deductions. The second is a non-QM loan that uses bank statements instead of tax returns, reading your actual deposits as income. That one helps most when write-offs make your returns look thinner than your real cash flow.

Neither path is automatically right. The conventional loan saves you money if you qualify. The bank-statement loan saves the deal if your returns won't cooperate. A good lender will run both and show you which one fits.

The real takeaway: start early. The decisions that make you easy to approve, like how you file and how clean your records are, happen a year or two before you ever fill out an application. You can't redo a tax return after the fact, so the planning has to come first.
