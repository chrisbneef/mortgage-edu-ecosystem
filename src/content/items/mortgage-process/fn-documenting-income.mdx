Here's a relief for a lot of buyers: foreign income and foreign assets can count toward your loan. Money you earn abroad, savings held in another country, the salary that pays your bills back home. A U.S. lender can use all of it. There's just one condition. You have to be able to document and verify it.

That condition is where the real work lives, so let's get specific about what a lender wants and how to make it go smoothly. None of this is tax advice, and a cross-border accountant is worth their fee, but the documentation side is something you can get a real head start on yourself.

## What you'll be asked to provide

Bank statements are the backbone. The lender wants to see your money, where it sits, and that it's actually yours. They'll usually ask for the most recent two or three months at minimum, and sometimes longer for accounts that hold your reserves. If your statements are in another language, you'll often need them translated into English by a qualified translator. If the balances are in another currency, they'll be converted to U.S. dollars, typically using a published exchange rate around the time the document was issued.

You'll usually provide an employer letter that confirms your job, your role, and your income. For tax records, you'll bring the foreign equivalent of a tax return, the official document your home country uses to show what you earned.

If you're self-employed or your finances are a bit involved, a letter from your accountant can carry real weight. An accountant explaining your income in plain terms helps a U.S. lender understand numbers that come from a system they don't see every day.

## What the lender is really checking

Behind the paperwork, the lender is answering two questions. Where did this money come from, and is it stable?

Source matters because lenders need to know your funds are legitimate and traceable. A large deposit with no explanation raises questions; the same deposit with a clear paper trail does not. Stability matters because they want confidence the income will keep coming, not vanish after closing. Documents that show a steady history rather than a one-time windfall make that case for you.

A quick worked example. Say your statement shows a $40,000 deposit two months ago. To an underwriter, that's a flag until you explain it. If it came from selling a car, a copy of the sale agreement and the matching transfer clears it. If it's a gift from a parent, a short gift letter and a record of the transfer does the job. The money isn't the problem. An unexplained source is. Sort each large deposit before anyone asks, and you turn a week of back-and-forth into a one-line answer.

<Callout type="info" title="Why reserves matter">
Reserves help a lot here. Reserves are savings left over after your down payment and closing costs, ideally several months of mortgage payments. For a non-resident borrower, larger reserves reassure the lender, because they show a cushion even if some of your income is harder to verify from abroad.
</Callout>

## What Trips People Up

If a foreign national loan stalls, this is usually where. Not because anything's wrong, but because gathering and translating overseas documents takes time. A bank statement that's easy to pull at home might need to be requested, translated, and converted before a U.S. underwriter can use it. Multiply that across several documents and a few weeks can slip away.

Two specific traps catch people. The first is large unsourced deposits, the example above: money the underwriter can see but can't trace. The second is currency swings. If your reserves sit in a volatile currency and the rate moves between application and closing, the dollar value the lender counted can shift, so keep a little extra cushion rather than landing right at the minimum. A related snag is an account in someone else's name, like a family member's. Lenders generally count funds you actually own and control, so money parked under a relative's name may not qualify unless it's properly gifted and transferred to you first.

## What to Do Next

The fix is preparation. Get ahead of it and the whole process moves faster.

Start collecting early. Pull a year or two of bank statements, request your employer letter, and gather your foreign tax documents before you even apply. Find a translator you trust, since you'll likely need more than one document done, and ask your lender whether they require a certified translation so you don't pay twice. Keep a running record of where each large deposit came from, so when the lender asks, you answer in minutes instead of days.

Organization is genuinely your biggest advantage here. A buyer who hands over a clean, complete, translated package often sails through, while one who sends documents in pieces, untranslated, with gaps to chase, watches the timeline stretch.

<Callout type="key" title="What carries your file">
Foreign income and assets count, but only when you can document and verify them. A clean, translated, fully sourced package is your biggest advantage. Sort every large deposit before the lender asks.
</Callout>

You don't need perfect English or a U.S. financial history. You need a tidy paper trail and the patience to assemble it before the clock starts. Do that, and your foreign income and assets carry the same weight they would for any other buyer.
