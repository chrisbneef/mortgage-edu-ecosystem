Once you've decided a renovation loan makes sense, the next fork in the road is which one. The two big options are the FHA 203(k) and the Fannie Mae HomeStyle. They do the same job, financing the purchase and the repairs together, but they suit different buyers. The right pick comes down to three things: your credit, your down payment, and the size of your project.

## The FHA 203(k)

The 203(k) is an FHA loan, which means it's the more forgiving of the two on the front end. You can get in with 3.5% down, and the credit requirements are flexible, which makes it a real option for buyers who don't have a thick credit file or a huge down payment saved up.

It comes in two flavors. The limited version is for smaller projects: cosmetic work, new appliances, updated finishes, that kind of thing. The standard version is for major work, like moving walls, structural repairs, or anything more involved. The standard version requires a HUD consultant, a third party who helps scope the work, writes up the plan, and keeps the project on track. That's a useful guide on a big renovation, though it's one more person in the mix.

The trade-off with any FHA loan is the mortgage insurance. The 203(k) carries it, and on most FHA loans today that insurance sticks around for the life of the loan. The usual way out is to refinance into a conventional loan later, once your equity and credit have improved. That's a fine plan, just one to know about going in.

## The Fannie Mae HomeStyle

HomeStyle is the conventional cousin. It allows a wider range of work, including some upgrades the 203(k) won't touch, such as certain luxury items like a pool. If your vision goes beyond fixing what's broken into building something nicer, HomeStyle gives you more room.

The big long-term advantage is the mortgage insurance. Because it's conventional, the <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm> can be cancelled once you've built enough equity, usually around 20%. So unlike the FHA insurance that lingers, this cost can fall off over time without you having to refinance.

The catch is that HomeStyle asks for stronger credit. It's less forgiving up front than the 203(k), so buyers with thinner or rebuilding credit may not qualify, or may not get the best pricing.

<Callout type="info">The FHA 203(k) is easier to qualify for but keeps mortgage insurance longer. HomeStyle wants better credit but lets you drop PMI down the road and covers a wider range of work. Neither is "better," it depends on your situation.</Callout>

## How to pick

Run it through three filters. Start with your credit. If it's on the thinner or lower side, the 203(k) is probably your path. If it's strong, HomeStyle opens up.

Then look at your cash. The 203(k)'s 3.5% down is gentler if savings are tight. HomeStyle's conventional structure tends to reward a bigger down payment with a cleaner long-term cost.

Last, weigh the project. Modest updates and repairs fit either program comfortably. A large, complex job or one with higher-end upgrades may steer you toward HomeStyle's broader scope or the 203(k) standard version with its HUD consultant.

There's no universal winner here. A lender who handles both can look at your numbers and tell you which one you qualify for and which one actually costs less for your plans. Have that conversation early, before you're under contract, so the loan choice shapes your offer instead of scrambling to fit it afterward.
