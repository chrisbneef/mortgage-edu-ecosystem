Because a jumbo loan puts more risk on the lender, the lender asks for more proof that you'll pay it
back. The pieces are the same ones you'd see on any mortgage. They're just set at a higher bar. If
you understand what that bar looks like before you apply, you can shore up the weak spots early
instead of finding out about them mid-process.

Start with credit. Jumbo lenders want a strong <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">credit score</GlossaryTerm>,
and "strong" here usually means somewhere in the 700 to 740 range and up. A conforming loan might
work with a score in the 600s, but a jumbo lender sees a big loan and wants a clean track record
behind it. The higher your score, the more flexibility you'll have on other terms, like a slightly
lower down payment or a touch more wiggle room on your debt load.

## Debt, Down Payment, And Reserves

Next is your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income</GlossaryTerm> ratio, which compares your monthly
debt payments to your monthly income. Jumbo lenders tend to want this lower than they would on a
conforming loan, often capped around 43 percent and sometimes tighter. A big mortgage payment eats up
a lot of room, so they want to see that your income comfortably covers it with margin to spare.

Down payments run higher too. Where a conforming buyer might put down 3 to 5 percent, a jumbo buyer
often brings 10 to 20 percent or more. The exact number depends on the lender, the loan size, and how
strong the rest of your file is. A buyer with excellent credit and deep reserves might get away with
less down than the headline figure suggests.

Reserves are the part that surprises people. A jumbo lender often wants to see that you have several
months of mortgage payments sitting in the bank after you close, sometimes 6 to 12 months or even
more. They want proof you could keep paying if your income hit a rough patch. Retirement accounts and
investment balances can often count toward reserves, so don't assume it all has to be cash in
checking.

<Callout type="info" title="Reserves Scale With the Loan">Reserves are measured in months of your full housing payment, not a flat dollar
amount. A bigger loan means a bigger monthly payment, which means the reserve requirement scales up
with it.</Callout>

## How That Looks With Real Numbers

Picture a $1 million home with 20 percent down. That's a $200,000 down payment and an $800,000 loan.
Say the full monthly housing payment, principal, interest, taxes, and insurance, lands around $6,000.
If your lender wants 9 months of reserves, that's roughly $54,000 they expect to see sitting in your
accounts after closing, on top of the $200,000 you already brought to the table.

That's the moment a lot of buyers realize jumbo qualifying isn't just about the down payment. You
need the down payment, the closing costs, and a healthy cushion behind all of it. The good news is
that cushion doesn't have to be cash in checking. A brokerage account or a vested retirement balance
often counts, usually at a discounted percentage of its value.

## Expect Thorough Documentation

Jumbo lenders document everything. They'll fully verify your income and assets, which means tax
returns, pay stubs, bank statements, and a clear paper trail for any large deposit. The thing that
trips people up most is an unexplained deposit. Move $40,000 from a brokerage account into checking
right before applying and underwriting will want to see exactly where it came from. Sort out the
paper trail before they ask. Self-employed buyers should expect extra scrutiny on their business
income, often two full years of returns plus a profit-and-loss statement. None of this is personal.
It's the price of borrowing a large amount that the lender is keeping on its own books.

On a high-value home, you might also run into a second appraisal. Lenders sometimes order two
independent valuations to make sure the property is really worth what you're paying, since they have
more money on the line if a sale goes sideways. Build a little extra time into your closing timeline
to account for that, and don't be alarmed if it happens. It's standard on larger loans, not a sign
something's wrong.

## When The Bar Bends

These standards aren't carved in stone, and that cuts both ways. A borrower with a stellar score and
a year of reserves might negotiate a lower down payment than the usual range. On the flip side, a
thin reserve cushion can sink an otherwise strong file, so it's the spot worth shoring up first if
you're short somewhere.

Here's the part that catches people off guard, in a good way: after all those tougher requirements,
jumbo rates are often competitive with conforming rates. Sometimes they're even a touch lower for the
strongest borrowers, because lenders compete hard for affluent clients they hope to keep for other
business. So don't assume a jumbo loan automatically costs more. Get a real quote and compare.

## What To Do Next

Look at your file the way a lender will. Pull your credit and check where your score lands. Add up
your monthly debts and compare them to your gross monthly income to estimate your DTI. Then tally
your liquid reserves, including investment and retirement accounts, and ask yourself how many months
of a likely payment they'd cover. If one of those three is soft, you'll know exactly where to focus
before you ever fill out an application.

<Callout type="key" title="The Bottom Line">Jumbo sets a higher bar on credit, debt, and reserves, but the rate is often competitive with conforming. Shore up your weakest of the three first, then get a real quote.</Callout>
