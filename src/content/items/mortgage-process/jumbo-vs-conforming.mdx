When your loan amount lands right around the conforming limit, you're in an interesting spot. You're
not locked into jumbo, and you're not stuck with conforming either. You have a few ways to shape the
deal, and the cheapest path isn't always the obvious one. The trick is to run the numbers on each
option instead of guessing.

First, a quick definition so the rest makes sense. The conforming limit is the largest loan that
Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac will buy, and it resets each year. Stay at or under it and you're
conforming, with the broadest pool of lenders and usually the friendliest pricing. Go over it and
you're in jumbo, where lenders keep the loan on their own books and underwrite it more carefully.
When your number sits right on that line, small moves in your loan amount can flip you from one
world to the other.

The simplest move is to put more money down. If you're just over the line, bringing a larger down
payment shrinks the financed amount until it slips back under the conforming limit. That can hand you
a simpler approval and sometimes better pricing. The cost is the cash you tie up in the home, which
you could otherwise keep invested or in reserve. For some buyers that's an easy trade, for others
it's not.

Say you're buying a $900,000 home and the conforming limit in your county is $806,500 (limits change
yearly and run higher in high-cost areas, so treat this as illustrative). With 10% down you'd
finance $810,000, which is just over the line and lands you in jumbo. Bump your down payment by
about $4,000 and your loan drops under the limit into conforming territory. That tiny change in cash
can shift your whole loan into a cheaper, simpler box. Alternatively, you keep the 10% down and
split the financing so the first loan stays conforming, which is where the piggyback comes in.

## The Piggyback Approach

Another classic tactic is the piggyback loan, often written as an 80-10-10. The numbers describe the
structure: a first mortgage for 80 percent of the price, a second mortgage for 10 percent, and 10
percent down from you. The first loan stays at or under the conforming limit, which keeps it out of
jumbo territory entirely.

The piggyback does two jobs at once. It can keep your main loan conforming, and it can help you avoid
<GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm>, the insurance lenders usually require when you put down
less than 20 percent. Since your first mortgage is only 80 percent of the price, PMI doesn't kick in.
The catch is that the second loan usually carries a higher interest rate, and it's frequently a home
equity line with a variable rate that can climb later. So you're trading one cost for another, and
maybe trading a fixed cost for a moving one. Add it all up before you decide it's a win.

<Callout type="tip" title="Avoiding Jumbo Is Not Always Cheaper">Don't assume avoiding jumbo automatically saves money. A piggyback's second loan
can carry a steep rate, and a single jumbo loan with a competitive rate sometimes beats the split
structure once you total the lifetime cost.</Callout>

## High-Balance And The Real Comparison

There's a middle tier worth knowing about. In high-cost counties, there's a high-balance conforming
category that sits between the standard limit and full jumbo. These loans follow conforming rules but
allow larger amounts, so they can give you a bigger loan without crossing into jumbo. Pricing on
high-balance loans is sometimes a little higher than standard conforming but still friendlier than
jumbo. Ask your lender whether your county qualifies and whether your loan size fits.

So how do you actually choose? Line the options up side by side and compare total cost, not just the
interest rate on the biggest loan. Look at the monthly payment, any second-loan rate, whether PMI is
in the picture, and how much cash each path asks you to part with. A spreadsheet beats a gut feeling
here.

## What trips people up

A few things catch buyers in this band. One is chasing "conforming at any cost" and tying up so much
cash in a bigger down payment that they leave themselves thin on reserves, which can actually hurt
the approval. Another is forgetting that a piggyback's second loan often resets to a higher or
variable rate down the road, so a structure that looks cheaper in year one isn't necessarily cheaper
over the life of the loan. And many buyers assume jumbo automatically means a worse rate. For a
strong borrower with solid reserves, jumbo pricing is sometimes competitive with conforming, because
the lender wants that relationship. Don't write it off without a quote.

## What to do next

The smart habit is to ask your lender for at least two structured scenarios: one conforming or
high-balance path and one straight jumbo path, ideally a piggyback option too if you're near the
line. Put the real numbers next to each other, total monthly payment, any second-loan rate, PMI,
and cash out of pocket, and let the total cost make the call rather than a gut sense that one label
beats another. For a strong borrower, a single jumbo loan can be clean, simple, and priced
competitively. Splitting the loan adds a second payment and a second set of terms to track.
Sometimes that complexity is worth it to dodge a higher rate or PMI, and sometimes it just adds
hassle for a small saving. The numbers will tell you which.
