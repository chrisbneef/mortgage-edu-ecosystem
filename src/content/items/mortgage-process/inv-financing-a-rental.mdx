Financing a rental looks a lot like financing a home you'd live in, with the dials turned up. You
put more down, you pay a higher rate, and the lender wants to see more cash in reserve. None of it
is a deal-breaker once you know the numbers going in.

Start with the down payment. A conventional investment loan usually wants 15 to 25% down. The more
units, the higher that climbs, so a single-family rental tends to sit at the low end and a
four-unit building at the high end. There's no 3% down option here the way there is for a first
home. Lenders treat investment property as higher risk, and the bigger down payment is part of how
they manage it.

That same risk shows up in the rate. An investment loan carries a higher rate than the loan on a
primary residence, sometimes noticeably so. It's not a penalty aimed at you. If money gets tight,
people pay the mortgage on the house they live in before the one they rent out, and the pricing
reflects that.

## Run the numbers before you fall in love with a property

Here's a rough example to make the pieces concrete. Say you're buying a single-family rental at
$300,000 and you put 20% down, so you're financing $240,000. Between principal, interest, taxes,
and insurance, your <GlossaryTerm slug="piti">PITI</GlossaryTerm> might land somewhere around
$1,900 a month, give or take, depending on rate and local taxes. If the place rents for $2,200, the
gap looks comfortable on paper. But you haven't paid for vacancy yet, or the property manager's cut,
or the water heater that dies in year two.

A common rule of thumb is to budget for maintenance and capital repairs (think roofs, HVAC, big-
ticket items) plus a vacancy allowance, often a combined 10 to 20% of rent set aside, before you
call anything cash flow. When you run the deal that way, a property that looked like a clear winner
sometimes turns into a break-even at best. That's not a reason to walk. It's a reason to know
exactly what you're signing up for.

## Credit and reserves still matter

Your credit score follows you into investment lending, and a stronger score buys a better rate just
like it does on a primary home. A higher <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">FICO</GlossaryTerm> can also be
the difference between hitting a program's minimum and not, since investment-loan score floors
usually sit higher than they do for an owner-occupied loan.

Lenders also want reserves, meaning months of payments sitting in the bank after you close. The
idea is that you can ride out a vacancy or a busted furnace without missing a payment. Plan to show
that cushion, not just enough to close. On a property with the $1,900 payment above, six months of
reserves is roughly $11,400 the lender wants to see untouched after your down payment and closing
costs clear. People forget that reserve money sits on top of everything else, and it's a frequent
reason a deal that pencils on price still can't close.

If your personal income doesn't stretch far enough to cover another mortgage on paper, you have a
second path. A DSCR loan qualifies on the property's rent instead of your paycheck, which is why so
many investors lean on it as their portfolio grows. It's worth knowing both options exist before
you apply.

## Be honest about occupancy

This one's important, so here it is plainly. When you apply, the lender asks how you'll use the
property: primary residence, second home, or investment. Second homes and primary residences get
better rates because they're lower risk, and it can be tempting to label a rental as a second home
to grab that pricing.

Don't. Occupancy fraud is a federal crime, and lenders catch it. They check, the language is right
there in the documents you sign, and the consequences run from the loan being called due to criminal
charges. The higher investment rate isn't a loophole to dodge. It's the price of the product, and
it's a price worth paying to stay on the right side of the line.

<Callout type="info" title="Rent Can Help You Qualify">
On conventional loans, projected rent can actually help you qualify. A signed lease, or an
appraiser's rent schedule estimating market rent, lets the lender count part of that income, which
can offset the new payment in your numbers.
</Callout>

## What trips people up

A few things catch first-time investors off guard. One is assuming rent counts dollar for dollar.
It rarely does. Lenders usually apply a vacancy factor, often counting around 75% of market rent
toward your income, so don't build your budget around the full lease amount. Another is forgetting
that a property you already own can drag down your numbers if it isn't cash-flowing the way you
told yourself it was. And a big one is treating the down payment as the only cash hurdle. Between
closing costs, reserves, and a repair budget, the real cash to get started runs well past the
percentage you put down.

## What to do next

Talk to a lender before you start shopping, and ask two specific questions: what's the minimum down
payment and score for the property type you want, and how many months of reserves will they require?
Then build a simple spreadsheet for a property you're considering, with rent on one side and the
full carrying cost (payment, taxes, insurance, vacancy, maintenance, management) on the other. If
your own income makes qualifying tight, ask the lender to quote both a conventional investment loan
and a DSCR option so you can compare. The honest version of the deal is this: more down, a higher
rate, real reserves, and a truthful application. Hit those, and a rental loan is well within reach.
