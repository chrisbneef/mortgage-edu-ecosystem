Financing a rental looks a lot like financing a home you'd live in, with the dials turned up. You
put more down, you pay a higher rate, and the lender wants to see more cash in reserve. None of it
is a deal-breaker once you know the numbers going in.

Start with the down payment. A conventional investment loan usually wants 15 to 25% down. The more
units, the higher that climbs, so a single-family rental sits at the low end and a four-unit
building at the high end. There's no 3% down option here the way there is for a first home. Lenders
treat investment property as higher risk, and the bigger down payment is part of how they manage it.

That same risk shows up in the rate. An investment loan carries a higher rate than the loan on a
primary residence, sometimes noticeably so. It's not a penalty aimed at you. If money gets tight,
people pay the mortgage on the house they live in before the one they rent out, and the pricing
reflects that.

## Credit and reserves still matter

Your credit score follows you into investment lending, and a stronger score buys a better rate just
like it does on a primary home. Lenders also want reserves, meaning months of payments sitting in
the bank after you close. The idea is that you can ride out a vacancy or a busted furnace without
missing a payment. Plan to show that cushion, not just enough to close.

If your personal income doesn't stretch far enough to cover another mortgage on paper, you have a
second path. A DSCR loan qualifies on the property's rent instead of your paycheck, which is why so
many investors lean on it as their portfolio grows. It's worth knowing both options exist before
you apply.

## Be honest about occupancy

This one's important, so here it is plainly. When you apply, the lender asks how you'll use the
property: primary residence, second home, or investment. Second homes and primary residences get
better rates because they're lower risk, and it can be tempting to label a rental as a second home
to grab that pricing.

Don't. Occupancy fraud is a federal crime, and lenders catch it. They check, the language is right
there in the documents you sign, and the consequences run from the loan being called due to criminal
charges. The higher investment rate isn't a loophole to dodge. It's the price of the product, and
it's a price worth paying to stay on the right side of the line.

<Callout type="info">
On conventional loans, projected rent can actually help you qualify. A signed lease, or an
appraiser's rent schedule estimating market rent, lets the lender count part of that income, which
can offset the new payment in your numbers.
</Callout>

So the honest version of the deal is this: more down, a higher rate, real reserves, and a truthful
application. Hit those, and a rental loan is well within reach.
