Once you're in jumbo territory, you'll find lenders offer more ways to structure the loan than you
might expect. Some of these options exist because affluent buyers don't always fit the standard
mold of a steady paycheck and a flat 30-year payment. Used well, they solve real problems. Used
carelessly, they can backfire. The key is understanding the trade-off behind each one.

Take interest-only loans. For a set early period, you pay only the interest and none of the
principal. That keeps the early monthly payment lower, which can free up cash for other uses. The
catch is the one people gloss over: during that interest-only stretch, you build no equity. Your
balance doesn't shrink. If home values dip, you could owe nearly what you borrowed with little
cushion. When the interest-only period ends, the payment jumps as you start paying down principal on
a shorter remaining schedule.

A quick illustration shows why the later payment stings. Picture a $1,000,000 loan with a 10-year
interest-only period on a 30-year term. During those first 10 years, you pay only interest, so your
balance is still $1,000,000 when year 11 arrives. Now the loan has to fully pay off over the
remaining 20 years instead of 30, on a balance that never went down. The payment doesn't just rise,
it can rise sharply, because you're amortizing the whole original balance over a shorter window.
Buyers who treated the low early payment as their normal budget get a rude jolt. The structure works
beautifully when you actually use the saved cash for something productive, and it hurts when you
simply spend it.

<Callout type="warning" title="Plan for the Payment Jump">Interest-only payments feel cheap early because you're not paying down a
dime of what you borrowed. Make sure you have a clear plan for the higher payment that lands once the
interest-only period ends.</Callout>

Interest-only can make sense for a buyer with lumpy income, like someone paid in big annual bonuses
who plans to throw chunks at the principal between paychecks. It's a poor fit for someone counting on
a low payment forever. Know which one you are before you sign.

## Qualifying On Assets, Not A Paycheck

Plenty of well-off buyers have modest taxable income but large investment and cash balances. For
them, traditional income-based qualifying paints an unfair picture. Asset-based qualifying, sometimes
called asset depletion, solves this. The lender takes your liquid assets and converts them into a
notional monthly income, as if you were drawing the balance down over time. That figure helps you
qualify even if your tax returns look light.

A rough sense of the math: a lender might take a portion of your eligible liquid assets and divide
it across a set number of months to produce that notional income. So a buyer with, say, $2,000,000
in qualifying accounts might generate a meaningful monthly income figure on paper, even with a small
salary. The exact formula varies a lot between lenders, including which accounts count (often
retirement funds get discounted) and over how many months they spread the balance. This is a real
lifeline for retirees living off a portfolio, or for buyers who keep their wealth in investments
rather than salary. Ask exactly which accounts count and how they translate balances into income
before you assume you qualify.

## When You're Self-Employed

If you run your own business, your tax returns probably understate your real cash flow, since you
write off a lot of legitimate expenses. Bank-statement jumbo loans are built for this. Instead of
tax returns, the lender reviews 12 or 24 months of your bank statements and uses the deposits to
estimate your income. It's a way to show a lender what you actually earn rather than what's left
after deductions.

One thing to watch: lenders don't count every dollar that lands in your account. They typically
apply an expense factor, treating only a percentage of your deposits as income to account for
business costs, and they'll question large irregular deposits that don't look like steady revenue.
Keep your business and personal accounts clean and separate, because messy commingled statements
make this loan much harder to underwrite.

## Reserves tie it all together

One thread runs through all three of these structures: reserves. Whether you choose interest-only,
asset-based qualifying, or a bank-statement loan, the lender wants to see meaningful money in the
bank after closing. On a large loan, that can mean a year or more of payments held in reserve, not
just a few months. Strong reserves reassure the lender that you can ride out a slow stretch, and on
a jumbo balance that reassurance carries real weight.

## What to do next

The honest advice on interest-only deserves repeating, because it's the option most likely to bite
you. The lower early payment is genuinely useful for the right buyer, but you're betting on either
rising values or a plan to pay down principal yourself. If neither is solid, a standard amortizing
loan that builds equity from day one is the safer choice. Ask your lender to print both payment
schedules side by side, the interest-only years and the amortizing years that follow, so the future
payment isn't a surprise. And if you're self-employed or asset-rich but income-light, ask which
qualifying method fits your situation before you fill out a single form. Matching the structure to
your real finances up front saves you from chasing the wrong loan.
