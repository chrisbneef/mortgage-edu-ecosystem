If you've ever been turned down on a rental purchase because your tax returns showed too many
write-offs, a DSCR loan is built for exactly that problem. Instead of looking at your paycheck,
the lender looks at whether the property pays for itself.

DSCR stands for debt service coverage ratio. The math is simple: take the gross monthly rent and
divide it by the total monthly payment. That payment includes principal, interest, taxes,
insurance, and any HOA dues. If the rent covers the payment exactly, your DSCR is 1.0.

## How the ratio works

Say a property rents for $2,750 a month and the full payment lands at $2,500. Divide $2,750 by
$2,500 and you get a DSCR of 1.10. The rent covers the payment with a little cushion left over.

Most lenders want to see at least 1.0, and you'll get the best pricing once you're at 1.20 or
higher. Some will go as low as 0.75, where the rent doesn't fully cover the payment, but you'll
pay for that flexibility with a higher rate or a bigger down payment. The stronger the ratio, the
friendlier the terms.

It's worth seeing how a small change swings the result. Take that same $2,750 rental. If rates
tick up and your full payment climbs from $2,500 to $2,700, your DSCR slides from 1.10 to about
1.02, barely clearing the bar. Drop another $20,000 into the down payment and the payment might
fall back to $2,550, lifting the ratio to roughly 1.08 and the loan back into comfortable
territory. The rent didn't change. The financing did. That's the lever you actually control.

<Callout type="tip" title="Run the ratio first">
Run the DSCR yourself before you apply. If you're sitting below 1.0, a larger down payment lowers
the monthly payment and pulls the ratio back up.
</Callout>

## What you don't need

Here's the part investors like. There's no W-2, no pay stubs, and no personal
<GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income</GlossaryTerm> calculation. The property's income carries
the loan, so your day job, your other mortgages, and your business write-offs mostly stay out of
it. For someone with a complicated tax picture, that's a real door opener.

That doesn't mean nothing about you matters. Credit still counts, usually 620 and up. And lenders
want to see reserves, often around six months of the property's payments sitting in the bank, so
they know you can cover a vacancy or a repair without falling behind. Many lenders also lean on an
appraiser's rent schedule, a form called the 1007, to confirm what the place should rent for, so
your DSCR rides on market rent rather than an optimistic number you picked.

## What Trips People Up

The most common surprise is which rent the lender uses. If your unit is leased above market, some
lenders cap you at the appraiser's market rent, which can pull your ratio down from what you
expected. The reverse bites short-term rental buyers: a property that crushes it on a booking site
may still be underwritten on long-term market rent, ignoring your peak-season nightly rates. Ask up
front which rent figure a lender uses, because it changes the whole calculation.

A DSCR loan also won't work for a primary residence. It's an investment-property product, full
stop, so the home you live in needs a different loan. And prepayment penalties are common here.
Many DSCR loans charge a fee if you pay off or refinance in the first few years, which matters if
you're planning a quick flip or expect to refinance soon. Read that term before you sign.

## What you'll put down, and what to do next

Plan on 20 to 25% down for a purchase. If you're pulling cash out of a property you already own,
expect 25 to 30%. Rates run a bit higher than a conventional mortgage, which is the trade you make
for skipping the income documentation.

DSCR loans finance one to four unit rentals and many condos. Some lenders will even underwrite
short-term rentals, though they may use market rent rather than your Airbnb history. If you're
buying a small multifamily or a condo you plan to rent, this product likely fits.

<Callout type="key" title="The core idea">
A DSCR loan qualifies the property on its own rent, not your paycheck, so write-offs and a complex tax picture stay out of it. You trade that for a higher rate, 20 to 25% down, and reserves. Confirm which rent figure the lender uses before you count on a ratio.
</Callout>

A practical next move: before you make an offer, pull realistic rent comps for the area, estimate
the full payment with taxes and insurance, and run the DSCR yourself. Then call a couple of lenders
who do these regularly and ask their minimum ratio, their rent rules, and whether there's a
prepayment penalty. The honest trade-off is cost. You'll pay a little more in rate than a borrower
who fully documents their income, and the reserve and down payment requirements aren't light. But
for an investor who wants the property judged on its own numbers, that premium often pays for
itself in approvals you wouldn't have gotten otherwise.
