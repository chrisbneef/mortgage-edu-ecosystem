When you look at a VA loan, you'll run into a line called the funding fee. It sounds like just another charge, but it's doing real work. It's the reason the VA loan program can keep offering zero down to the next generation of buyers without leaning on taxpayers.

## What the fee is for

The VA funding fee is a one-time charge paid to the Department of Veterans Affairs. It takes the place of the monthly mortgage insurance you'd pay on most low-down-payment loans. On a conventional loan with under 20% down, you'd owe <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm> every month until you built enough equity. A VA loan skips that entirely.

That's a bigger deal than it first looks. Monthly mortgage insurance can run anywhere from $80 to $250 or more on a typical loan, every month, for years. The VA trades that recurring cost for a single fee, and for many buyers the math comes out well ahead.

## How much it costs

The fee is a percentage of the loan amount, not a flat dollar figure. Two things move that percentage: how much you put down, and whether this is your first time using the VA benefit.

A first-use buyer with no down payment pays a higher percentage than someone putting 10% down. And a repeat user of the benefit typically pays a bit more than a first-time user at the same down payment. The exact rates are set by the VA and adjust periodically, so ask your lender for the current figure tied to your situation. As a rough sense of scale, a first-use, zero-down buyer is often in the low-2% range of the loan.

Put real numbers on it. On a $300,000 loan, a funding fee around 2.15% works out to about $6,450. Bump up to a 5% down payment and the percentage drops, which can shave that fee by a meaningful chunk. Put 10% down and it falls further still. So if you're sitting on some cash, it's worth asking your lender to show you the fee at zero down versus 5% versus 10%, because a down payment does double duty here: it lowers your loan and lowers the fee percentage at the same time.

<Callout type="tip" title="You can finance the fee">
You don't have to bring the funding fee to the closing table in cash. Most buyers roll it into the loan, so it gets financed along with the home. That keeps your upfront costs lower, though it does mean a slightly larger balance and a little more interest over time.
</Callout>

## When you owe nothing

Here's the part too many eligible buyers miss. The funding fee is waived completely for veterans who receive VA disability compensation. If you have a service-connected disability rating that comes with compensation, you generally pay zero funding fee on your purchase. Active-duty service members who've received a Purple Heart often qualify for the waiver too.

A few other groups qualify as well. Surviving spouses receiving certain benefits often pay nothing, and some veterans who are eligible for disability compensation but receive retirement pay instead may also qualify. If you think you might be exempt, tell your lender early and make sure it's reflected on your Certificate of Eligibility. People have been charged a fee they didn't owe simply because the exemption wasn't flagged, and getting it corrected after closing is a hassle worth avoiding.

One more situation worth knowing about: if your disability claim is approved after you close, you may be owed a refund of a funding fee you already paid. If your effective date of compensation predates your loan closing, ask your lender or the VA about getting that money back. It's the kind of thing that's easy to forget once you're moved in. Because exemption rules have specific timing and documentation requirements, confirm your status with the VA and your lender rather than assuming either way.

## Seeing the full picture

When you weigh a VA loan against other options, don't look at the funding fee in isolation. Pair it against what you'd otherwise pay in monthly mortgage insurance over the years you expect to own the home.

For a buyer planning to stay put for a while, a one-time fee that can be financed, plus no monthly insurance, usually beats a smaller upfront cost followed by years of monthly premiums. The math can flip if you expect to sell or refinance quickly, since you'd pay the full fee up front but only avoid a few months of insurance, so factor in how long you actually plan to keep the loan.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="Check your exemption first">If you receive VA disability compensation, you generally owe no funding fee at all, so confirm your exemption with your lender and make sure it shows on your COE before closing. For everyone else, a down payment lowers both the loan and the fee percentage, so it's worth pricing zero down against a little cash up front.</Callout>

Ask your lender for two things: the current funding fee percentage for your exact situation, and a side-by-side of the fee at zero down versus a small down payment. Then confirm whether any exemption applies to you, and make sure it shows up on your COE before closing. Run your own numbers, and if you're exempt, your costs drop further still.
