Buying a place at the beach or in the mountains is a different financing animal than buying your
main home. Lenders treat a second home as its own category, with its own expectations, and they care
a lot about how you'll actually use it. Get clear on those rules before you make an offer, because the
loan you qualify for depends heavily on the answers.

The defining feature of a second-home loan is occupancy. The lender expects the property to be for
your personal use, a place you'll actually stay in. They also usually want it a reasonable distance
from your primary residence, since a vacation home next door to your main home raises eyebrows. And
it can't be a full-time rental. A second home is yours to enjoy, with maybe some occasional rental on
the side, not a year-round income property.

## What To Expect On Terms

Plan to put down at least 10 percent, and often more, depending on the lender and the price. Credit
expectations are healthy, similar to what you'd see on a strong primary-residence loan. Rates run a
bit higher than they would on your main home, because a second home is the property you'd walk away
from first if money got tight. Lenders price that risk in.

Reserves are where second homes get demanding. The lender wants to see that you can cover the
payments on both homes at once, not just the new one. That means tallying both mortgage payments,
both sets of taxes and insurance, and any HOA dues, then showing enough in the bank to carry it all
through a rough patch.

<Callout type="info">When you apply for a second home, the lender looks at your total housing load,
both properties together. Make sure your reserves and your debt-to-income picture account for carrying
both at the same time.</Callout>

## The Occupancy Trap

Here's the part that quietly trips people up. If you rent the vacation home out heavily, the lender
may reclassify it as an investment property. That's not just a label change. Investment loans
typically require a larger down payment, carry higher rates, and follow stricter rules. A loan you
took out as a second home isn't supposed to morph into a rental business after closing.

This matters more than ever because lenders tightened their second-home rules in recent years. They
got stricter about verifying that these properties really are personal-use homes and not investment
properties wearing a friendlier label. So the occupancy promise you make at application is one the
lender takes seriously.

That doesn't mean you can never rent it. Many buyers rent a vacation home for part of the year and
keep it as a personal second home the rest of the time, and that's usually fine within limits. The
trouble starts when the rental use dominates. If your real plan is to rent it out most of the year and
stop by occasionally, be honest with your lender from the start and look at investment-property
financing instead. Matching the loan to the actual use saves you headaches later.

The practical step is to describe your true plans for the property up front. Tell the lender roughly
how much you'll use it yourself and how much, if at all, you'll rent it. They'll point you to the
right loan, and you'll avoid the nasty surprise of a reclassification, or worse, a problem with the
terms you agreed to. A little honesty at the application stage keeps everything clean down the road.
