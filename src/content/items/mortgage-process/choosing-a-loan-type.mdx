There isn't one best mortgage. There's the one that fits your credit, your cash on hand, and what you're eligible for. The names sound technical, but the differences come down to plain trade-offs: how much you put down, how good your credit needs to be, and what the insurance costs over time. Here's how the four common types stack up.

## Conventional loans

This is the standard loan most buyers picture. You can put down as little as 3%, and good credit gets rewarded with better pricing. The catch shows up when you put down less than 20%: you'll pay <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm>, an extra monthly cost that protects the lender if you default.

The upside is that PMI isn't forever. Once you've built enough equity, usually around 20%, you can request to drop it, and by law it falls off automatically once you hit 22% equity based on the original schedule. So a conventional loan with a smaller down payment can get cheaper over time as you pay down the balance. Conventional loans tend to reward strong credit the most, so if your score is solid, this is often the cheapest path even with a low down payment.

## FHA loans

FHA loans are built for buyers with thinner credit or less saved. You can get in with 3.5% down, and credit requirements are more forgiving, often starting around 580 (and sometimes lower with 10% down). For someone rebuilding credit, that flexibility can be the door that opens.

The trade-off is the mortgage insurance. FHA charges both an upfront premium and an annual one, and on most FHA loans today that annual premium sticks around for the life of the loan. The usual way to shed it is to refinance into a conventional loan later, once your credit and equity improve. That's a fine plan, just one to go in knowing about rather than discovering afterward.

<Callout type="info" title="FHA vs Conventional">FHA flexibility comes with longer-lasting mortgage insurance. Conventional can be stricter up front but cheaper down the road. Neither is "better." It depends on where your credit is today.</Callout>

## VA and USDA loans

These two are narrower but genuinely powerful if you qualify.

VA loans are for eligible veterans, active service members, and certain surviving spouses. They offer 0% down and no monthly PMI, which is a rare combination. There's a one-time funding fee rolled in (sometimes waived for those with a service-connected disability), but the day-to-day cost is hard to beat. If you've served, this is almost always worth a serious look.

USDA loans also allow 0% down and are aimed at eligible rural and many suburban areas. There are income limits and location rules, so not every household or address qualifies. If the home you want sits in an eligible zone and your income fits, it's one of the most affordable ways in.

## A side-by-side example

Say you're buying a $300,000 home and you've got about $15,000 saved. With a conventional loan at 3% down, you'd put down $9,000 and carry PMI until you reach roughly 20% equity, then drop it. With FHA at 3.5% down, you'd put down $10,500, but the mortgage insurance would likely ride along for the life of the loan unless you refinance.

If your credit is around 760, the conventional loan is often cheaper month to month and gets cheaper still once PMI falls off. If your credit is closer to 600, FHA might be the only one that approves you at a reasonable rate. Same house, same budget, different best answer, and the deciding factor is your credit and your timeline. Now suppose you're a veteran: a VA loan could let you keep most of that $15,000 in the bank with zero down and no monthly mortgage insurance, which changes the comparison entirely.

## Where people trip up

The most common mistake is chasing the lowest down payment without looking at the lifetime cost. A loan that gets you in the door cheaply can cost more over five years if the insurance never goes away. Another is assuming you only qualify for one type; plenty of buyers are eligible for two or three and never ask. And some buyers skip VA or USDA because the names sound restrictive, when they'd actually qualify and save thousands.

One more: don't pick a loan based on a single quoted rate. The rate, the insurance, the fees, and how long you'll stay in the home all matter together. A slightly higher rate with no lifetime insurance can beat a lower rate that carries insurance for 30 years.

## A word on bigger homes, and what to do next

If the price runs above the conforming loan limit for your area, you're looking at a jumbo loan. Those come with stricter requirements: higher credit standards, larger down payments, and more cash reserves. It's a different conversation, and one your lender will flag if you get there.

For everyone else, the move is simple. Match the loan to your actual situation. Your credit, your cash, and your eligibility point to the right answer far better than any slogan ever will. When you talk to a lender about pre-approval, ask them to price out every type you might qualify for and compare the monthly cost and the lifetime cost side by side. Seeing the real numbers next to each other makes the choice obvious.
