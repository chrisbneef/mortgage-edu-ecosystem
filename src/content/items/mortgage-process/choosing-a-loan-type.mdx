There isn't one best mortgage. There's the one that fits your credit, your cash on hand, and what you're eligible for. The names sound technical, but the differences come down to plain trade-offs. Here's how the four common types stack up.

## Conventional loans

This is the standard loan most buyers picture. You can put down as little as 3%, and good credit gets rewarded with better pricing. The catch shows up when you put down less than 20%: you'll pay <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm>, an extra monthly cost that protects the lender.

The upside is that PMI isn't forever. Once you've built enough equity, usually around 20%, you can request to drop it, and it falls off automatically a bit later. So a conventional loan with a smaller down payment can still get cheaper over time as you pay down the balance.

## FHA loans

FHA loans are built for buyers with thinner credit or less saved. You can get in with 3.5% down, and credit requirements are more forgiving, often starting around 580. For someone rebuilding credit, that flexibility can be the door that opens.

The trade-off is the mortgage insurance. On most FHA loans today, it sticks around for the life of the loan, and the usual way to shed it is to refinance into a conventional loan later once your credit and equity improve. That's a fine plan, just one to go in knowing about rather than discovering afterward.

<Callout type="info">FHA flexibility comes with longer-lasting mortgage insurance. Conventional can be stricter up front but cheaper down the road. Neither is "better." It depends on where your credit is today.</Callout>

## VA and USDA loans

These two are narrower but genuinely powerful if you qualify.

VA loans are for eligible veterans, active service members, and certain surviving spouses. They offer 0% down and no monthly PMI, which is a rare combination. There's a one-time funding fee rolled in, but the day-to-day cost is hard to beat. If you've served, this is almost always worth a serious look.

USDA loans also allow 0% down and are aimed at eligible rural and many suburban areas. There are income limits and location rules, so not everyone or every address qualifies. If the home you want sits in an eligible zone and your income fits, it's one of the most affordable ways in.

## A word on bigger homes

If the price runs above the conforming loan limit for your area, you're looking at a jumbo loan. Those come with stricter requirements: higher credit standards, larger down payments, and more reserves. It's a different conversation, and one your lender will flag if you get there.

Match the loan to your actual situation. Your credit, your cash, and your eligibility point to the right answer far better than any slogan ever will. A good lender will walk you through which of these you qualify for, often in the same conversation as your pre-approval.
