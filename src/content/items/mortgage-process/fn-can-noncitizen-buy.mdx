If you're not a U.S. citizen and you've been wondering whether you can own a home here, the short answer is yes. There's no citizenship requirement to own real estate in the United States. People from all over the world buy houses, condos, and investment properties here every year.

This catches a lot of people by surprise. The American immigration system is complicated, so it feels like buying property should be locked behind some special status. It isn't. Owning a home and your right to live in the country are two separate questions, and the law treats them separately. To be clear, this is general education, not immigration or tax advice, and your specific situation deserves a real professional.

## What you can actually buy

You can buy pretty much any kind of property you'd want. A primary home to live in. A vacation place you visit a few weeks a year. A rental you never set foot in but collect income from. The property itself doesn't care about your passport.

Paying cash is always an option, and plenty of foreign buyers do exactly that. Cash sidesteps the whole financing question and can make your offer stronger to a seller. But most people want financing, and that's where your status starts to matter, because it changes which loans are open to you and on what terms.

## Where you land depends on your status

Think of it as a few groups, roughly sorted by how easy the financing is.

Green card holders, also called permanent residents, are treated a lot like citizens. With a steady job, some U.S. credit, and a normal down payment, you can often qualify for the same standard loans everyone else uses. Many lenders barely blink.

Visa holders are a big middle group. If you have a valid visa and you're working and living here, a lot of lenders will work with you. The specifics depend on your visa type and how much time is left on it, but the door is usually open. As a rough example, someone on a work visa with two years of U.S. pay stubs and an established credit score often qualifies much like a citizen would, while someone who just arrived may need to lean on a specialty program until a domestic track record builds up.

True non-residents are the third group. Maybe you live abroad and want a U.S. vacation home or investment property. You'd typically use what's called a foreign national loan, a program built specifically for buyers who don't live here. Expect a larger down payment and more documentation, since the lender is working without the U.S. credit and income history they usually rely on.

<Callout type="info" title="What a foreign national loan is">
A foreign national loan is a type of non-QM loan, meaning it sits outside the standard government-backed rules. That's not a red flag. It just means a lender is using its own guidelines to lend to someone a conventional loan wasn't designed for.
</Callout>

## What Trips People Up

Here's the part that catches people off guard. Not every bank does these loans. You could walk into a big national branch, get a confused look, and walk out thinking it's impossible. It isn't impossible. You were just talking to the wrong lender.

Foreign national and non-resident financing is a specialty. Some lenders handle it constantly and have the process down to a routine. Others have never touched it and won't know where to start. The difference between a smooth experience and a frustrating one usually comes down to whether the person across the table has done this before.

A second common stumble is confusing this with immigration status. Getting a mortgage doesn't change your visa, your green card timeline, or your right to stay, and owning a home doesn't grant you any. They're separate systems. Buying property here also doesn't give you a path to citizenship, despite a myth that pops up now and then.

## What to Do Next

When you start shopping, ask directly: do you regularly work with foreign national buyers, and how many of these do you close in a year? A lender who does this often will answer fast and clearly. Vague answers or long pauses are your signal to keep looking.

A few practical moves smooth the road. Start gathering your documents early, since paperwork from abroad takes longer to collect and may need translating. Open a U.S. bank account if you can, because down payments and closing costs flow through the domestic system. And get a written pre-approval before you shop for a home, so you know your real budget and sellers take your offer seriously.
