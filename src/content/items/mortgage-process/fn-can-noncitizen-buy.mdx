If you're not a U.S. citizen and you've been wondering whether you can own a home here, the short answer is yes. There's no citizenship requirement to own real estate in the United States. People from all over the world buy houses, condos, and investment properties here every year.

This catches a lot of people by surprise. The American immigration system is complicated, so it feels like buying property should be locked behind some special status. It isn't. Owning a home and your right to live in the country are two separate things.

## What you can actually buy

You can buy pretty much any kind of property you'd want. A primary home to live in. A vacation place you visit a few weeks a year. A rental you never set foot in but collect income from. The property itself doesn't care about your passport.

Paying cash is always an option, and plenty of foreign buyers do exactly that. But most people want financing, and that's where your status starts to matter, because it changes which loans are open to you.

## Where you land depends on your status

Think of it as a few groups, roughly sorted by how easy the financing is.

Green card holders, also called permanent residents, are treated a lot like citizens. With a steady job, some U.S. credit, and a normal down payment, you can often qualify for the same standard loans everyone else uses. Many lenders barely blink.

Visa holders are a big middle group. If you have a valid visa and you're working and living here, a lot of lenders will work with you. The specifics depend on your visa type and how much time is left on it, but the door is usually open.

True non-residents are the third group. Maybe you live abroad and want a U.S. vacation home or investment property. You'd typically use what's called a foreign national loan, a program built specifically for buyers who don't live here.

<Callout type="info">
A foreign national loan is a type of non-QM loan, meaning it sits outside the standard government-backed rules. That's not a red flag. It just means a lender is using its own guidelines to lend to someone a conventional loan wasn't designed for.
</Callout>

## The real key: finding the right lender

Here's the part that trips people up. Not every bank does these loans. You could walk into a big national branch, get a confused look, and walk out thinking it's impossible. It isn't impossible. You were just talking to the wrong lender.

Foreign national and non-resident financing is a specialty. Some lenders handle it constantly and have the process down to a routine. Others have never touched it and won't know where to start. The difference between a smooth experience and a frustrating one usually comes down to whether the person across the table has done this before.

So when you start shopping, ask directly: do you regularly work with foreign national buyers? A lender who does will answer fast and clearly. That's your signal you're in good hands.

Buying from abroad, or as a newcomer, takes a few extra steps. You'll likely face a bigger down payment and some added paperwork, and we cover those details in the other guides here. But the headline holds: you can do this, and people in your exact situation do it all the time.
