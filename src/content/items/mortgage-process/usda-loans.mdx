If saving a down payment is the wall between you and a home, the USDA loan deserves a serious look. It's one of the only mortgages that lets qualified buyers put down nothing at all. Zero percent. The full purchase price gets financed.

The program comes from the U.S. Department of Agriculture's Rural Development office, which tells you something about who it's for. It was created to help people buy homes outside dense city centers. The word "rural" makes people assume it only covers farms and remote countryside, but that's not how it plays out.

## "Rural" is broader than you think

This is the part that surprises most buyers. A lot of ordinary suburbs and small towns qualify, including neighborhoods you'd never describe as rural. The only way to know is to check the official USDA eligibility map and type in the address you're considering.

Plenty of people rule themselves out of USDA before checking, and they're often wrong. A house twenty minutes from a mid-sized city can sit inside an eligible area. So before you assume you don't qualify by location, look up the actual address. It costs you nothing and might save you tens of thousands.

<Callout type="tip" title="Check the exact address">
Check the USDA eligibility map for a specific address, not just a general region. Eligibility can change from one street to the next, and a home that looks suburban may still qualify.
</Callout>

## The income limits

Location is only half the test. USDA also has household income limits, and they're a real gate. The program is meant for low and moderate income buyers, so if your household earns well above the area's median, you may not qualify.

The limits depend on two things: where you're buying and how many people are in your household. A family of five gets a higher income ceiling than a single buyer in the same county, because the limit accounts for family size. The cutoffs vary widely by area, so a number that's fine in one county might be too high in another.

Here's a wrinkle that trips people up: USDA counts the income of everyone in the household, not just the people on the loan. If your adult sister lives with you and works, her income usually counts toward the household limit even though she's not borrowing. At the same time, the program allows certain deductions, like an amount per child and some childcare or medical costs, that can lower the income they actually measure. So if you're a little over on paper, it's worth having a USDA-savvy lender run the real calculation before you give up.

## What it costs instead of a down payment

USDA loans don't carry standard mortgage insurance. Instead, they have a guarantee fee, which works in a similar spirit. There's an upfront fee, often rolled into the loan, and a smaller annual fee that's spread across your monthly payments.

These fees tend to be lighter than FHA's mortgage insurance, which is part of what makes USDA attractive when you qualify. Picture a $250,000 purchase with nothing down. The upfront guarantee fee might add a few thousand dollars to your loan balance, and the annual fee, spread monthly, often lands in the neighborhood of a few hundred dollars a year, not the few thousand FHA can charge. The exact percentages are set by USDA and adjust over time, so confirm the current figures with your lender. The guarantee fee is essentially the price the government charges for making that zero-down loan possible.

## A few ground rules

The home has to be in an eligible area, and it has to be your primary residence. You can't use a USDA loan for a vacation house, a rental, or an investment property. It's for the place you'll actually live.

The property should also be in livable condition and meet basic standards, similar to other government-backed loans. Most move-in-ready homes clear this without trouble. Where USDA gets stricter is the kind of property: it's meant for modest single-family homes, so income-producing features like a working farm, a commercial building, or extra acreage you'd use to generate money can disqualify a property even when the address itself is eligible.

## Who it fits best, and who it doesn't

USDA is a strong match for moderate-income buyers who are open to living outside the urban core and who don't have a big chunk of cash saved. If you fit the income limits and you find a home in an eligible area, this loan can get you into ownership years earlier than waiting to save a down payment would.

Where it doesn't fit: high earners who blow past the income cap, anyone set on a downtown condo or a busy metro neighborhood, and buyers who actually have a 5 to 10% down payment and strong credit, since a conventional loan might cost them less over time. The geography and income caps narrow the pool on purpose.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="Two free checks decide it">USDA turns on two gates: the home must sit in an eligible area, and your household income must fall under the local cap. Both checks are free and take minutes, so confirm them before you rule yourself in or out.</Callout>

Two free checks tell you almost everything. First, type your target address into the USDA eligibility map. Second, look up the income limit for your county and household size, then compare it to your gross household income, remembering that everyone living there counts. If both come back green, talk to a lender who closes USDA loans regularly. Not every lender does, and the ones who do can tell you quickly whether the no-money-down path is open to you.
