Once you're under contract with a renovation loan, the process behind the scenes looks different from a regular purchase. The appraisal, the contractor, and the way money gets released all run on their own rules. Knowing how they work keeps you from being surprised when the timeline stretches or the lender asks for one more form.

## The appraisal values a house that doesn't exist yet

On a normal purchase, the appraiser values the home as it stands today. On a renovation loan, they do something stranger and more useful: they value the home "as completed." That means they're appraising the finished version of the house based on the plans and the contractor's bid, not the torn-up version you're actually buying.

This matters because the as-completed value is what your loan is built on. The appraiser takes the scope of work, the materials, the layout changes, and the local market, and arrives at what the home should be worth once all of it is done. If that number supports the deal, you're in good shape. If it comes in low, you may need to trim the project or rethink the budget, the same way a low appraisal complicates any purchase.

## Your contractor has to come correct

The lender isn't just trusting your word that the work will get done well. They want a licensed contractor with a detailed bid and a realistic timeline before they'll release a dollar. That bid spells out exactly what's being done, what it costs line by line, and roughly when each piece happens.

Choose your contractor carefully, because the lender is essentially betting on them too. A vague one-page estimate from someone unlicensed won't fly. A clear, itemized bid from a licensed pro with a track record is what keeps the loan moving.

One more rule that catches people off guard: on a 203(k), you generally cannot do the major work yourself, even if you're handy. The program wants licensed professionals on the significant repairs. So if part of your plan was sweat equity on the big stuff, check the rules before you count on it.

## How draws get released

The renovation money doesn't land in your account on day one. It's released in draws, and each draw comes after an inspection confirms that the work it's paying for is actually finished. Contractor completes the framing, an inspector verifies it, that portion of the funds gets released. Then on to the next stage.

This protects everyone. The contractor can't collect for work they haven't done, and you don't end up with a half-finished project and an empty account. It does mean the contractor needs enough cash flow to start each phase before getting paid for the last one, which is another reason an established pro matters.

<Callout type="tip">Document every change order. If you and the contractor agree to swap materials, add a task, or adjust the scope mid-project, get it in writing. Undocumented changes cause draw delays and disputes, and the paper trail protects you if anything goes sideways.</Callout>

## Expect delays, and plan around them

Renovation timelines slip. It's not a sign something's wrong, it's just the nature of the work. Materials get backordered, inspections get scheduled a week out, weather interferes, a wall opens up to reveal a problem nobody saw. Every one of these adds days.

So build in a buffer from the start. If you're renting while the work happens, don't assume you'll move in the day the original timeline says. Give yourself breathing room on both the schedule and the budget, and the inevitable delays become an annoyance instead of a crisis.

The whole draw-and-inspection system can feel slow when you're eager to move in. But that structure is what makes a renovation loan safe to do. The money follows the work, the inspections keep quality honest, and a good contractor with clear paperwork makes the whole thing run as smoothly as a renovation ever runs.
