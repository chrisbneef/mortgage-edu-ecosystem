These two words get used like they mean the same thing. They don't. And the gap between them can decide whether your offer gets taken seriously or quietly passed over. The short version: one is an estimate based on what you told the lender, and the other is a real review of your actual finances. That difference is everything when you're trying to win a house.

## What prequalification actually is

A prequalification is a quick estimate. You tell a lender your income, your debts, and roughly what you have saved, and they hand back a number you might be able to borrow. Often there are no documents involved. Sometimes there's a soft credit check, sometimes none at all.

It's useful early on. If you're just starting to wonder what's possible, a prequalification gives you a ballpark in a few minutes. Treat it as a sketch, not a promise. Because the lender hasn't verified anything you said, the number can shift a lot once real paperwork shows up. If you estimated your income high or forgot a monthly debt, the verified number can land well below what the prequal suggested, which is a rough surprise to hit after you've started shopping.

## What pre-approval is, and why it carries weight

A pre-approval is the real one. Here the lender verifies your income, checks your assets, and pulls your credit for real. At the end you get a letter stating a specific amount they're prepared to lend you.

That letter is what sellers and agents want to see. It tells them a lender already looked under the hood and is comfortable. In a competitive market, an offer without a pre-approval letter often gets skipped, because the seller has no reason to believe you can actually close. Many agents won't even schedule showings until they've seen one, since they don't want to spend a Saturday touring homes with a buyer who can't get financed.

A pre-approval is usually good for 60 to 90 days. After that, your financial picture may have changed, so the lender will want fresh documents and another look. Keeping it current is just a matter of sending updated pay stubs and statements when they ask.

## How this plays out in a real offer

Picture two buyers chasing the same $400,000 house. One submits an offer with a prequalification email saying they "may qualify for up to $420,000." The other submits the same price with a pre-approval letter naming a verified $420,000 and the lender's contact info. To the seller, the second offer simply looks more likely to close, even at the identical price. When offers are close, that's often the tiebreaker, and in a multiple-offer situation the prequalified buyer can get passed over without ever knowing why.

The mistake to avoid is treating a prequalification as good enough to start making offers. It feels like progress, and it is, but it's not the document that competes. Use it to orient yourself, then convert to a pre-approval before you're emotionally attached to a listing.

## A pre-approval is strong, but it's not the finish line

Here's the honest part. Even a pre-approval is not a final guarantee that your loan will fund. The home itself still has to clear underwriting, and the appraisal has to support the price you agreed to pay. If the appraisal comes in low, or if you take on new debt between the pre-approval and closing, the deal can still wobble. A classic trap is financing furniture or a car after you're pre-approved, which can change your debt picture enough to jeopardize the loan. Sit tight on big purchases until you've closed.

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't Add Debt Before Closing">Financing furniture or a car after your pre-approval can shift your debt picture enough to sink the loan. Hold off on big purchases until you've closed.</Callout>

So think of it this way. Prequalification answers "roughly, could I?" Pre-approval answers "yes, this lender is ready, pending the house checking out." That second answer is the one that gets you to the closing table.

## What to do next

If you only do one thing before you start shopping, make it the pre-approval. Reach out to a lender, ask for their document list, and gather it in one sitting so you can come back with a verified letter rather than a guess. It costs you a little paperwork now and saves you from falling for a home you can't actually offer on. Once that letter is in hand, then go tour, knowing exactly what you can spend and that any offer you make will be taken seriously.

<Callout type="key" title="The Bottom Line">A prequalification is an unverified estimate, but a pre-approval is a real review that sellers trust. Get the pre-approval before you shop, since it's the document that actually competes for the house.</Callout>
