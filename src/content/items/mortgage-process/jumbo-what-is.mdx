A jumbo loan sounds exotic, but the definition is boring in the best way. It's simply any mortgage
that's larger than the conforming loan limit. Cross that line by a single dollar and your loan is
jumbo. Stay a dollar under it and you're conforming.

So what's the line? Each year a federal agency sets a baseline conforming limit for the whole
country. In pricey counties, the limit is bumped up higher to reflect local home values. That means
the number isn't one fixed figure. It depends on where you're buying. A loan size that's jumbo in a
modest market might still be conforming in a high-cost coastal county.

## Why The Label Matters

The reason this line exists comes down to who's willing to buy your loan after you close. Conforming
loans follow rules written by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the two big entities that buy mortgages
from lenders. Because those loans fit a standard mold, they're easy to sell and the rules are
predictable.

Jumbo loans don't fit that mold. They're too big for Fannie and Freddie to back, so the lender
either keeps the loan on its own books or sells it to private investors. That puts more risk on the
lender, and lenders respond to risk by setting their own rules. There's no single rulebook for
jumbo. One bank might want a bigger down payment, another might care more about your cash reserves.

## The Line Moves Every Year

Here's the part people miss: the conforming limit is reset annually, and it has trended up for years
as home prices climb. A loan that's jumbo this year could be conforming next year if the limit rises
past your loan amount.

That timing can actually work in your favor. Say you're shopping in late fall and you're a little
over the current limit. If the new year's higher limit is about to take effect, waiting a few weeks
might drop you back into conforming territory with no change to your loan at all.

<Callout type="tip">If your loan amount lands just barely over the conforming limit, a slightly
larger down payment can pull the financed amount back under the line and keep you conforming.</Callout>

That down-payment trick is worth running the numbers on. Imagine the limit in your county is a clean
threshold and you need to borrow just $15,000 more than that. Bringing an extra $15,000 to closing
keeps you conforming, and conforming loans often come with simpler approval and sometimes friendlier
pricing. Whether that trade is smart depends on whether you'd rather keep the cash or keep the
simpler loan.

None of this means jumbo is bad. In expensive markets, jumbo loans are completely routine, and
plenty of well-qualified buyers use them every day. The label just tells you which set of rules your
loan will follow and who's ultimately holding the risk. Once you know which side of the line you're
on, you can ask your lender the right questions and compare your options with clear eyes.

The takeaway is to find out your county's current limit early, before you fall in love with a price
range. That one number tells you which side of the line you land on, and it shapes everything from
your down payment to your paperwork.

<Disclaimer>Conforming loan limits change annually and vary by county. Confirm the current limit for
your area with your lender.</Disclaimer>
