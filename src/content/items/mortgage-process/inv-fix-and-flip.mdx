A 30-year mortgage isn't built for a property you plan to fix and sell in four months. Fix-and-flip
loans are. These are short-term loans, often 6 to 18 months, made to buy a property, renovate it,
and then either sell or refinance once the work is done.

The biggest difference from a normal mortgage is how the loan gets sized. Instead of basing it on
what you're paying today, the lender looks at the ARV, the after-repair value. That's what the
property should be worth once the rehab is finished. Many lenders will lend up to 70 to 75% of that
ARV, which can cover most of the purchase and a chunk of the renovation.

Let's put numbers to it. Say you find a tired house you can buy for $180,000, and you figure it
needs $50,000 of work to make it shine. A local agent and your own comps suggest it'll be worth
about $300,000 fixed up, so your ARV is $300,000. A lender capping at 70% of ARV would lend up to
$210,000 against this deal.

That $210,000 covers your $180,000 purchase and leaves $30,000 toward rehab, which means you're
bringing the other $20,000 of the renovation out of pocket, plus closing costs and a few points.
Now run the exit. If you sell at $300,000, subtract the loan payoff, the holding costs you racked
up over four or five months, agent commissions on the sale (often 5 to 6%), and your own cash in
the deal. What looked like a $120,000 spread between cost and ARV is a much thinner number by the
time everything clears. That's normal, and it's exactly why you size deals with a calculator, not
optimism.

## How the money actually flows

You won't get the rehab budget in one lump. The lender holds it back and releases it in draws as
you finish work. Tear out the kitchen and pass an inspection, and that draw funds. This protects
the lender, and honestly it protects you too, since it keeps the project on a schedule.

The draw structure has a cash-flow wrinkle people miss: you often have to pay for a phase of work
before the lender reimburses you for it. Your contractor wants money to start the kitchen, but the
draw only releases after it's done and inspected. You need enough working capital to float that gap,
sometimes across several phases. Investors who run too lean on cash end up stalling a project not
because the work is hard, but because they can't fund the next draw.

During the project you're usually on an interest-only payment. That keeps your monthly cost lower
while you've got money tied up in materials and labor. The flip side is the price: rates are higher
than a conventional loan, and you'll often pay points up front to get the money out the door.

<Callout type="warning" title="Holding Costs Eat Profit">
Holding costs are the line investors forget. Every month you own the property, you're paying
interest, taxes, and insurance whether the work is moving or not. A two-month delay can quietly
eat your profit.
</Callout>

## Planning your exit

Every short-term loan needs an exit, and you should know yours before you buy. The classic move is
to sell the finished property and pay off the loan from the sale proceeds.

The other exit is to keep it. You refinance the bridge loan into a long-term rental loan, often a
DSCR loan, and hold the property as a rental. That's the BRRRR idea: buy, rehab, rent, refinance,
repeat. The bridge loan gets you in and funds the work, then a permanent loan takes its place once
the property is stabilized.

Two numbers decide whether any of this works. The first is your rehab budget, and you should pad it
for overruns, because surprises behind the walls are the norm, not the exception. A 10 to 20%
contingency on the rehab number is a sane starting point. The second is your holding cost, which
climbs the longer the project drags. Build both into your numbers before you write an offer, not
after.

## When a flip loan is the wrong tool

Fix-and-flip financing isn't for every messy-looking house. If the property only needs light
cosmetic work and you have the cash, a flip loan's points and higher rate can cost more than they're
worth. If your exit is a sale but the market is cooling, that short loan term turns into pressure:
the clock runs out whether or not a buyer shows up, and you may face an extension fee or a forced
refinance. And if your ARV estimate is shaky, everything downstream is shaky, since the whole loan
hangs off that one number. Pull real comparable sales before you trust it.

One more thing worth saying plainly: this is investor financing, not a way to buy a home to live in.
Fix-and-flip loans are for properties you intend to sell or rent, and trying to use one on a primary
residence will get the loan denied. If you're buying a house to live in, that's a different
conversation entirely.

## What to do next

Before you make an offer, write down four numbers and stress-test them: your purchase price, your
rehab budget with contingency added, a realistic ARV backed by recent comps, and your total holding
cost over a timeline longer than you expect to need. Then decide your exit up front and line up the
financing for it. If you plan to sell, know your likely sale costs. If you plan to hold, talk to a
DSCR lender before you start, so you're confident the refinance will actually pencil once the
property is rented.

Your loan, your budget, and your exit all ride on the after-repair value. Pull real comparable
sales to back it up, and size every deal with a calculator, not optimism.
</Callout>
