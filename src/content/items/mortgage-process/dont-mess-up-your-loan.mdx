You're under contract, you can picture the living room, and the furniture store has a sale. So you buy the couch. It seems harmless. It is not, and it's one of the most common ways people accidentally torpedo their own loan in the final stretch.

Here's the part nobody warns you about: your approval isn't final until the loan funds. The lender usually re-checks your credit right before closing, sometimes the very week you're set to sign. Many lenders also pull a quick "soft refresh" that flags new accounts and new inquiries. Anything you changed about your money since you applied can show up and reopen the whole file.

## The window where mistakes hurt most

Between going under contract and closing, your finances need to stay boring. This is the worst possible time to make a move that looks good in the moment but rattles your numbers. The lender built your approval on a specific snapshot, and they expect that snapshot to still be true at the finish line.

A few things to avoid until you have the keys: don't finance a car, furniture, or appliances; don't open or close credit cards; don't make large deposits you can't document; don't change jobs or jump from salaried to self-employed; and don't co-sign a loan for anyone, even family. Each of these looks small on its own. To an underwriter pulling fresh numbers, any one of them can be the thing that stalls or sinks your closing.

## A worked example with real numbers

Say you're approved for a $360,000 loan and your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income ratio</GlossaryTerm>, the share of your monthly income already spoken for by payments, sits at 41%. Your lender's ceiling for your loan is 43%. You've got about $200 a month of room before you cross the line.

Now you finance a $32,000 SUV at closing time. That's roughly a $550 monthly payment. On, say, an $8,000 gross monthly income, that one payment adds nearly 7 percentage points to your DTI and shoves you well past 43%. The underwriter sees the new tradeline, recalculates, and your approval doesn't fit anymore. Best case, you scramble to qualify for a smaller loan and renegotiate. Worst case, the closing falls apart and you lose the house. A car you could have bought a month later just cost you the home you were a week from owning.

Even a smaller move matters. A new credit card with a $4,000 balance can add a $120 minimum payment plus a fresh hard inquiry and a lower average account age, all of which can nudge your numbers the wrong way at the exact moment they're being re-examined.

## Why deposits and job changes cause trouble

New debt isn't the only landmine. Large unexplained deposits cause a different headache. The lender has to confirm your money is really yours and not a hidden loan, so a surprise $9,000 in your account triggers questions and paperwork right when you want things quiet. If money is genuinely a gift, document it as a gift from the start; if you sold something, keep the receipt.

Job changes are their own risk. Lenders want stable, predictable income, so switching employers, going from W-2 to 1099, or moving to a commission-heavy role mid-process can force a fresh income review even if you're earning more. Sometimes it works out fine. The problem is the timing, not always the move itself.

<Callout type="warning" title="Call Before You Act">If you absolutely must move money or make a big purchase before closing, call your loan officer first. A two-minute heads-up can be the difference between a non-issue and a delayed closing.</Callout>

## When the rules bend a little

This isn't about never touching your money. Paying your normal bills, keeping your usual paycheck flowing, and small ordinary spending are all fine. The danger is anything that adds debt, moves big sums, or changes your income picture. If something unavoidable comes up, a medical bill, a car that dies, an inheritance that lands, the answer isn't to panic or hide it. It's to loop in your loan officer so they can document it cleanly before underwriting stumbles on it.

## What to do next

The fix is refreshingly simple: do nothing dramatic. Keep your pay stubs coming, leave your accounts alone, save your big purchases for later, and resist the sales that are suddenly everywhere. The couch will still be there in a few weeks, and so will the SUV and the balance-transfer offer. If you're unsure whether a move is safe, ask before you act, not after.

Wait until the loan funds and the home is yours. Then go buy the couch with your feet up in your own living room. A few weeks of patience protects the biggest purchase you'll probably ever make.
