You're under contract, you can picture the living room, and the furniture store has a sale. So you buy the couch. It seems harmless. It is not, and it's one of the most common ways people accidentally torpedo their own loan in the final stretch.

Here's the part nobody warns you about: your approval isn't final until the loan funds. The lender usually re-checks your credit right before closing, sometimes the very week you're set to sign. Anything you changed about your money since you applied can show up and reopen the whole file.

## The window where mistakes hurt most

Between going under contract and closing, your finances need to stay boring. This is the worst possible time to make a move that looks good in the moment but rattles your numbers. The lender built your approval on a specific snapshot, and they expect that snapshot to still be true at the finish line.

A few things to avoid until you have the keys:

- Don't finance a car, furniture, or appliances
- Don't open or close credit cards
- Don't make large deposits you can't document
- Don't change jobs or go from salaried to self-employed
- Don't co-sign a loan for anyone, even family

Each of these looks small on its own. To an underwriter pulling fresh numbers, any of them can be the thing that stalls or sinks your closing.

## Why a small purchase matters so much

New debt changes your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income ratio</GlossaryTerm>, the share of your monthly income already spoken for by payments. Lenders set firm limits on that ratio, and your approval likely sits closer to the edge than you realize. A $450 car payment or a new credit-card balance can push you past the line and shrink your loan, or kill it.

Large unexplained deposits cause a different headache. The lender has to confirm your money is really yours and not a hidden loan, so a surprise $9,000 in your account triggers questions and paperwork right when you want things quiet.

<Callout type="warning">If you absolutely must move money or make a big purchase before closing, call your loan officer first. A two-minute heads-up can be the difference between a non-issue and a delayed closing.</Callout>

The fix is refreshingly simple: do nothing dramatic. Keep your pay stubs coming, leave your accounts alone, and resist the sales that are suddenly everywhere. The couch will still be there in a few weeks, and so will the SUV and the balance-transfer offer.

Wait until the loan funds and the home is yours. Then go buy the couch with your feet up in your own living room. A few weeks of patience protects the biggest purchase you'll probably ever make.

<Disclaimer>Lender requirements and DTI limits vary by loan program. Check with your loan officer before any major financial change while under contract.</Disclaimer>
