If your tax returns make you look broke even though your accounts say otherwise, there's a loan built for exactly that gap. It's called a bank-statement loan, and instead of asking for your returns, it asks to see where your money actually lands.

Here's the basic idea. Rather than pulling income off a tax form, the lender collects 12 to 24 months of your bank statements and averages your eligible deposits to figure out a monthly income. No tax returns. No W-2s. No pay stubs. The deposits do the talking. These loans fall under the "non-QM" umbrella, which just means they don't follow the standard qualified-mortgage rulebook that most conventional loans use. That label isn't a red flag. It's a different set of guidelines built for borrowers who don't fit the W-2 mold.

That's a big deal for self-employed buyers, because deposits show your gross cash flow before all the deductions that shrink a tax return. If your business brings in real money but your write-offs gut your net income, this loan can count what your returns hide.

## What deposits actually count

Not every dollar that hits your account counts as income, and this is where personal and business accounts get treated differently.

With a personal account, lenders often count close to your full eligible deposits, since that money is presumably yours to keep. With a business account, they usually apply an expense factor. The thinking is that a business has costs, so they only count a portion of the deposits as your income. A lender might use 50% of business deposits, figuring the other half went to running the business. Some lenders will accept a lower expense factor (meaning they count more of your deposits) if your accountant signs a letter estimating your true business expense ratio. If your real costs are well under 50%, that letter can meaningfully raise your qualifying income, so it's worth asking about.

That's why it's worth asking up front which statements a lender wants and how they'll treat them. Two lenders can look at the same accounts and arrive at different income numbers based on how they handle that expense factor.

<Callout type="info" title="Keep deposits clean">Keep your deposits clean. A random $8,000 transfer from your brother or a one-time sale of a car can get excluded, or worse, raise questions. Lenders want to see recurring business income, not surprise lump sums.</Callout>

## What trips people up

The most common surprise is how aggressively underwriters scrub the deposits. They're not just adding up the bottom of every statement. They strip out transfers between your own accounts, loan proceeds, tax refunds, one-time sales, and anything that doesn't look like recurring business revenue. People walk in expecting their $20,000-a-month average to hold, then watch it drop to $13,000 once the non-business deposits come out.

A second trap is overdrafts and negative balances. A statement that dips below zero, even briefly, can spook an underwriter who's trying to read your account as stable income. If you've got a couple of bouncy months in your recent history, it can be worth waiting until you have a clean 12-month stretch before you apply.

And watch the seasoning rules on your down payment. Lenders want to see that the cash for your down payment has been sitting in your account for a couple of months, not freshly parked there. Large recent deposits into the account you'll draw from get questioned the same way income deposits do.

## What it costs you

A bank-statement loan trades convenience for a higher price, and you should walk in knowing the numbers.

Credit requirements usually start around 620, which is reasonable. The down payment is the bigger ask: plan for 20 to 30% down, well above what a conventional loan might require. And the rate typically runs roughly 0.5 to 2 percentage points above a comparable conventional loan, because the lender is taking on more risk without standard tax-return proof. You may also see prepayment penalties on some non-QM programs, so read that part of the term sheet carefully if you might refinance or sell within the first few years.

On a $400,000 loan, a rate that's one percentage point higher works out to roughly $250 more a month, give or take, depending on the rate. That's real money over the life of the loan. So this loan makes the most sense when a conventional loan simply isn't on the table, not as a first choice when your returns would qualify you anyway.

## When it's the right tool, and when it isn't

Picture a contractor who grosses $200,000 a year but writes off enough that the tax return shows $70,000. A conventional lender qualifies that person on $70,000. A bank-statement lender averaging two years of deposits might see $14,000 or $15,000 a month before the expense factor, then maybe $7,500 a month after it. That's still a far larger qualifying income than the tax return alone would produce, and it can mean a meaningfully bigger loan.

That's the whole point. The loan exists for people whose real income lives in their deposits, not on their 1040. But it does not fit everyone. If your tax returns already show strong income, you'll usually do better with a conventional loan and its lower rate. If you can't put down at least 20%, you may be priced out of the program entirely. And if your business is brand new with only a few months of statements, you probably don't have enough history yet for a lender to average.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="When this loan fits">A bank-statement loan reads your deposits instead of your tax returns, so it shines when write-offs make your returns look thin. It costs more in rate and down payment, so reach for it only when a conventional loan built on your returns won't qualify you.</Callout>

Pull your last 12 to 24 months of statements and skim them yourself first. Add up only what looks like recurring business income, then knock off a rough expense factor, and you'll have a ballpark of what an underwriter might allow. Then ask a self-employed-friendly lender to price both options side by side: the conventional loan built on your tax returns, and the bank-statement loan built on your deposits. Seeing the two numbers together makes the trade-off obvious, and sometimes the conventional path wins even when you assumed it wouldn't.
