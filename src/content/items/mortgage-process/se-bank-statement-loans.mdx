If your tax returns make you look broke even though your accounts say otherwise, there's a loan built for exactly that gap. It's called a bank-statement loan, and instead of asking for your returns, it asks to see where your money actually lands.

Here's the basic idea. Rather than pulling income off a tax form, the lender collects 12 to 24 months of your bank statements and averages your eligible deposits to figure out a monthly income. No tax returns. No W-2s. No pay stubs. The deposits do the talking.

That's a big deal for self-employed buyers, because deposits show your gross cash flow before all the deductions that shrink a tax return. If your business brings in real money but your write-offs gut your net income, this loan can count what your returns hide.

## What deposits actually count

Not every dollar that hits your account counts as income, and this is where personal and business accounts get treated differently.

With a personal account, lenders often count close to your full eligible deposits, since that money is presumably yours to keep. With a business account, they usually apply an expense factor. The thinking is that a business has costs, so they only count a portion of the deposits as your income. A lender might use 50% of business deposits, figuring the other half went to running the business.

That's why it's worth asking up front which statements a lender wants and how they'll treat them. Two lenders can look at the same accounts and arrive at different income numbers based on how they handle that expense factor.

<Callout type="info">Keep your deposits clean. A random $8,000 transfer from your brother or a one-time sale of a car can get excluded, or worse, raise questions. Lenders want to see recurring business income, not surprise lump sums.</Callout>

## What it costs you

A bank-statement loan trades convenience for a higher price, and you should walk in knowing the numbers.

Credit requirements usually start around 620, which is reasonable. The down payment is the bigger ask: plan for 20 to 30% down, well above what a conventional loan might require. And the rate typically runs roughly 0.5 to 2 percentage points above a comparable conventional loan, because the lender is taking on more risk without standard tax-return proof.

On a $400,000 loan, a rate that's one point higher costs you a few hundred dollars more every month. That's not nothing. So this loan makes the most sense when a conventional loan simply isn't on the table, not as a first choice when your returns would qualify you anyway.

## When it's the right tool

Picture a contractor who grosses $200,000 a year but writes off enough that the tax return shows $70,000. A conventional lender qualifies that person on $70,000. A bank-statement lender averaging two years of deposits might see $14,000 or $15,000 a month before the expense factor, and qualify a far bigger loan.

That's the whole point. The loan exists for people whose real income lives in their deposits, not on their 1040. If your tax returns already show strong income, you'll usually do better with a conventional loan and its lower rate. But when write-offs have buried your qualifying income, this is the path that can get you to the closing table.

Ask any self-employed-friendly lender to price both options side by side. Seeing the conventional number and the bank-statement number together makes the trade-off obvious.
