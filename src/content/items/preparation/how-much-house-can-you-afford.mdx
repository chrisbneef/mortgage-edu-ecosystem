There's a number a lender will hand you, and there's a number you can actually live with. They're rarely the same. Learning the gap between them might be the most useful thing you do before you start shopping.

## The 28/36 rule

The classic guideline goes like this: spend no more than 28% of your gross monthly income on housing, and keep your total debt payments under 36% of that same income. Gross means before taxes come out.

Say you earn $6,000 a month before taxes. At 28%, that's about $1,680 for housing. The 36% piece means your housing payment plus your car loan, student loans, and credit card minimums should land under roughly $2,160 combined.

These percentages aren't magic. They're just a tested way to leave room in your budget for everything that isn't your house.

## Why the bank's max is a ceiling, not a target

Lenders look at your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income</GlossaryTerm> ratio, and many will approve you well past 36%. Stretching to 43% is common, and some loan programs go to 50%.

Read that as the edge of the cliff, not the spot to stand on. A payment that technically fits on paper can still leave you skipping vacations, eating out never, and panicking when the water heater dies. The bank is measuring whether you'll repay the loan. It isn't measuring whether you'll be happy.

<Callout type="warning">Just because you're approved for a number doesn't mean you should spend it. The maximum is the most a lender will allow, not the amount that leaves your life intact.</Callout>

## Your real payment is bigger than you think

When people picture a mortgage, they picture principal and interest. The actual monthly bill is <GlossaryTerm slug="piti">PITI</GlossaryTerm>: principal, interest, taxes, and insurance, all rolled together.

Property taxes and homeowners insurance can add a few hundred dollars a month, sometimes more, depending on where you buy. And if the home is in a community with an HOA, those dues stack on top of everything. None of that shows up in a quick "monthly payment" estimate online, so your real cost runs higher than the sticker.

You may have heard the old "buy 2 to 3 times your income" rule of thumb. It's a rough sketch at best. In an expensive metro with high rates, that multiple can feel impossible; in a cheaper area with low rates, it might be conservative. Use it as a sanity check, not a budget.

## Build the number from your real life

Here's the move that actually works: start from your take-home pay, not your gross. Look at what hits your account after taxes and retirement contributions. Then subtract the life you want to keep living: groceries, childcare, saving, the occasional dinner out.

Whatever's comfortably left is what you have for housing. Compare that to the lender's pre-approval, and you'll almost always find your honest number is lower. That gap isn't a problem. It's breathing room, and you'll be glad it's there the first time something breaks.
