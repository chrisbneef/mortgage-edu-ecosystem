When a lender decides how much you can borrow, one number does a lot of the heavy lifting. It's not your credit score, though that matters too. It's the share of your income already promised to other bills.

## What DTI means and how it's figured

Your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income ratio</GlossaryTerm> is simply your total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income. Gross means before taxes come out, which surprises some people. Lenders use the bigger, pre-tax number on both sides of the math.

Here's a quick example. Say your monthly debts add up to $2,000, and you earn $6,000 a month before taxes. That's a 33% DTI. The lower that percentage, the more room you have for a mortgage payment, and the easier you are to approve.

Most lenders want to see your DTI under 43%. Some programs stretch to 50% for the right borrower. Below 36% is comfortable territory and gives you the most options.

## Front-end and back-end, briefly

You'll sometimes hear lenders mention two ratios. The front-end ratio looks at only the housing payment as a share of your income. The back-end ratio counts everything: the future housing payment plus car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, and any other monthly debt.

The back-end number is the one that usually controls the decision, since it shows your whole obligation picture. So when you're trying to qualify, think about every recurring payment, not just the mortgage.

<Callout type="warning">A loan you co-signed for someone else still counts against your DTI, even if they make every payment. Lenders see your name on it as your obligation.</Callout>

## How to lower it before you apply

Good news: DTI responds quickly to a few smart moves, and you don't have to wait years.

Paying down credit card balances is the fastest lever, because it cuts the minimum payment lenders count. If you have a small loan with just a few payments left, paying it off entirely removes that whole line from your back-end ratio. And in the months before you apply, avoid taking on anything new. A fresh car loan or a financed furniture purchase can quietly push you over the line right when you need room.

Notice what's missing here: there's no trick to it. You're either lowering what you owe each month or raising your income, and the first one is usually faster to control.

Run your own numbers before you ever talk to a lender. Add up your monthly debts, divide by your gross monthly income, and you'll know roughly where you stand. If it's already under 43%, you're in shape to start. If it's higher, you now know exactly which balances to attack first.

<Disclaimer>DTI limits vary by lender and loan program. This is general education and not a determination of what you'll qualify for.</Disclaimer>
