When a lender decides how much you can borrow, one number does a lot of the heavy lifting. It's not your credit score, though that matters too. It's the share of your income already promised to other bills. Get a handle on this one ratio and you'll understand most of what a lender is thinking when they look at your file.

## What DTI means and how it's figured

Your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income ratio</GlossaryTerm> is simply your total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income. Gross means before taxes come out, which surprises some people. Lenders use the bigger, pre-tax number on both sides of the math, so a paycheck that feels smaller after withholdings still counts at its full size here.

Here's a quick example. Say your monthly debts add up to $2,000, and you earn $6,000 a month before taxes. That's a 33% DTI. The lower that percentage, the more room you have for a mortgage payment, and the easier you are to approve.

Most lenders want to see your DTI under 43%. Some programs stretch to 50% for the right borrower, usually when other parts of your file are strong, like a healthy credit score or solid savings. Below 36% is comfortable territory and gives you the most options. These aren't hard universal cutoffs; they shift by loan program, lender, and the rest of your picture, so treat them as a useful frame rather than a fixed law.

One thing people often get wrong: only payments count, not balances. A $40,000 student loan with a $250 monthly payment hits your DTI as $250, not $40,000. That's why two buyers with identical total debt can land in very different spots. It also means how a debt is structured, the term, the minimum, the rate, matters as much as the size.

## Front-end and back-end, briefly

You'll sometimes hear lenders mention two ratios. The front-end ratio looks at only the housing payment as a share of your income. The back-end ratio counts everything: the future housing payment plus car loans, student loans, credit card minimums, and any other monthly debt.

The back-end number is the one that usually controls the decision, since it shows your whole obligation picture. So when you're trying to qualify, think about every recurring payment, not just the mortgage.

Remember that the housing payment in your front-end ratio is the full <GlossaryTerm slug="piti">PITI</GlossaryTerm>: principal, interest, taxes, and insurance, plus mortgage insurance and any HOA dues. A lot of first-time buyers do the math on just principal and interest, then get surprised when the qualifying payment comes in higher. Taxes and insurance can add a few hundred dollars a month on their own.

<Callout type="warning" title="Co-Signed Loans Still Count">A loan you co-signed for someone else still counts against your DTI, even if they make every payment. Lenders see your name on it as your obligation.</Callout>

## A worked example, start to finish

Picture Maya. She earns $7,000 a month gross. Her debts are a $450 car payment, $120 in student loans, and a credit card with a $90 minimum. That's $660, or about a 9% back-end ratio before any housing. If she's shopping a home where the full PITI would run $1,840 a month, add that in and her back-end DTI lands near 36%. Comfortable, with room to spare.

Now change one thing. Say Maya finances a new $35,000 car two months before applying and her car payment jumps to $620. Her debts climb to $830, and that same house pushes her back-end ratio to about 38%. Still workable, but she's given up cushion she might have wanted for a slightly pricier home or a rougher month. Small decisions move this number more than people expect.

## How to lower it before you apply

Good news: DTI responds quickly to a few smart moves, and you don't have to wait years.

Paying down credit card balances is the fastest lever, because it cuts the minimum payment lenders count. If you have a small loan with just a few payments left, paying it off entirely removes that whole line from your back-end ratio. That's often a better use of cash than chipping at a big balance, since killing a payment helps your ratio more than shrinking one. And in the months before you apply, avoid taking on anything new. A fresh car loan or a financed furniture purchase can quietly push you over the line right when you need room.

Where this gets tricky is the self-employed or commission-based earner. Lenders typically average your income over the last two years and lean on what shows on your tax returns, not your gross sales. If you write off a lot of expenses, your qualifying income can look smaller than your bank account suggests, which inflates your DTI on paper. If that's you, talk to a lender early so there are no surprises about which income number counts.

Notice what's missing here: there's no trick to it. You're either lowering what you owe each month or raising your income, and the first one is usually faster to control.

## What to do next

Run your own numbers before you ever talk to a lender. Add up your monthly debts, divide by your gross monthly income, and you'll know roughly where you stand. If it's already under 43%, you're in shape to start a conversation. If it's higher, you now know exactly which balances to attack first.

<Callout type="key" title="Only Payments Count, Not Balances">DTI is your monthly debt payments divided by gross monthly income. Killing a whole payment helps more than shrinking a big balance, so target small loans first and add nothing new before you apply.</Callout>

Then get pre-approved rather than guessing. A lender pulls your actual credit, confirms your income, and tells you the real qualifying number for your situation, which is the only one that counts when you make an offer. Bring your pay stubs, recent statements, and a list of every monthly payment, and you'll get an answer you can trust.
