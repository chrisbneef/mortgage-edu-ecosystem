If you've been through a bankruptcy, you might assume homeownership is off the table for a decade or more. It isn't. A lot of people buy a home a couple of years after filing, and lenders deal with this every single day. The bankruptcy is a chapter in your story, not the end of it. Plenty of homeowners reading this once filed too, and you'd never know it from the outside.

The big thing to understand is the waiting period. That's the amount of time a loan program typically wants to see pass before it'll consider your application. The clock usually starts at your discharge or dismissal date, not the day you filed, so dig up that paperwork and find the exact date. It matters more than you'd think, and people often assume the wait is longer than it really is because they're counting from the wrong day.

## How long you'll wait

The numbers depend on which chapter you filed and which loan you want, and they can change, so treat these as common ranges and confirm the current rules with a lender.

For a Chapter 7 bankruptcy, FHA and VA loans typically want to see around two years from discharge. A conventional loan usually asks for something closer to four years. So if you're a couple of years out and shopping for an FHA loan, you may be closer than you realized.

Chapter 13 works differently because it involves a repayment plan. With FHA, you can often qualify after about a year of on-time plan payments, frequently with your bankruptcy trustee or the court signing off on the new debt. That surprises people, because they're still technically in the plan and already able to house-hunt. Conventional loans tend to count from the discharge or dismissal of the Chapter 13, so the path you're on shapes the timeline a lot.

## Use the wait, don't just count it

The waiting period isn't dead time. It's your runway. The single best thing you can do is rebuild your <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">FICO score</GlossaryTerm> so that when your wait is up, you actually qualify at a decent rate. The difference between a barely-approved score and a healthy one can be a real chunk of money every month for thirty years, so this stretch genuinely pays off.

Start small and steady. A secured credit card, one regular bill paid on time every month, and patience will do most of the work. Payment history is the heaviest factor in your score, so a clean run of on-time payments after a bankruptcy can move the needle a lot. Keep your balances well below your limits, ideally under about 30% of each card, and resist the urge to open a pile of new accounts at once. One or two accounts handled perfectly beats five handled nervously.

Here's a rough picture of how that runway can look. Imagine you come out of a Chapter 7 with a score in the low 500s. You open a secured card, charge a tank of gas a month, pay it in full, and never miss a due date on anything else. Over a year or two, it's common to see a score climb back into the 600s or higher, which is the range where FHA financing starts to open up. Nobody can promise an exact number, but the pattern of steady, boring, on-time payments is what does it.

Save while you wait, too. Even a modest down payment and a few months of reserves in the bank make your application stronger and give you a cushion if life throws something at you again.

## If you really can't wait

There's another path. Some non-QM loans (the "non-qualified mortgage" world) will let you buy sooner, sometimes within a year of discharge or even less. The tradeoff is cost. You'll usually pay a higher interest rate, a bigger down payment, and more in fees. For some buyers that math works, especially if waiting means missing a specific opportunity. For many, it's worth holding out a bit longer for a cheaper, government-backed loan. It's a tradeoff, not a trap, so run both versions of the numbers honestly before deciding.

One more thing that genuinely helps your case: documenting why the bankruptcy happened. If there was a clear, one-time cause outside your control, like a serious medical event or a sudden layoff, write it down and keep the proof. Lenders tend to look more favorably on a one-time hardship that's behind you than on a long pattern of missed payments. A short, honest letter explaining what happened, plus the records to back it up, can make a real difference when an underwriter reviews your file.

## What to do next

Start with one phone call. Find your discharge or dismissal date on your bankruptcy paperwork, then talk to a lender who works with credit recovery and ask two things: which loans you'd be closest to qualifying for, and what score and waiting period each one needs. From there you can work backward and build a simple plan, a target loan, a target date, and the credit habits that get you there.
