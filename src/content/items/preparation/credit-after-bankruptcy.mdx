If you've been through a bankruptcy, you might assume homeownership is off the table for a decade or more. It isn't. A lot of people buy a home a couple of years after filing, and lenders deal with this every single day. The bankruptcy is a chapter in your story, not the end of it.

The big thing to understand is the waiting period. That's the amount of time a loan program wants to see pass before it'll consider your application. The clock usually starts at your discharge or dismissal date, not the day you filed, so dig up that paperwork and find the exact date. It matters more than you'd think.

## How long you'll wait

The numbers depend on which chapter you filed and which loan you want.

For a Chapter 7 bankruptcy, FHA and VA loans typically want to see about two years from discharge. A conventional loan usually asks for around four years. So if you're a couple of years out and shopping for an FHA loan, you may be closer than you realized.

Chapter 13 works differently because it involves a repayment plan. With FHA, you can often qualify after about a year of on-time plan payments, frequently with your bankruptcy trustee or the court signing off. That surprises people, because they're still technically in the plan and already house-hunting.

<Callout type="info">These are common guidelines, not hard guarantees. Individual lenders can add their own stricter rules on top, so two lenders may quote you different timelines for the same situation.</Callout>

## Use the wait, don't just count it

The waiting period isn't dead time. It's your runway. The single best thing you can do is rebuild your <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">FICO score</GlossaryTerm> so that when your wait is up, you actually qualify at a decent rate.

Start small and steady. A secured credit card, one regular bill paid on time every month, and patience will do most of the work. Payment history is the heaviest factor in your score, so a clean stretch of on-time payments after a bankruptcy can move the needle a lot. Keep your balances low and don't chase a pile of new accounts.

Save while you wait too. Even a modest down payment and a few months of reserves in the bank make your application stronger and give you breathing room.

## If you really can't wait

There's another path. Some non-QM loans (the "non-qualified mortgage" world) will let you buy sooner, sometimes within a year of discharge or even less. The tradeoff is cost. You'll usually pay a higher interest rate, a bigger down payment, and more in fees. For some buyers that math works; for many it's worth waiting a bit longer for a cheaper, government-backed loan. It's a tradeoff, not a trap, so run the numbers honestly.

One more thing that genuinely helps your case: documenting why the bankruptcy happened. If there was a clear, one-time cause outside your control, like a serious medical event or a sudden layoff, write it down and keep the proof. Lenders look more kindly on a one-time hardship that's behind you than on a long pattern of missed payments. A short letter explaining what happened, plus the records to back it up, can make a real difference.

You filed, you got through it, and now you're rebuilding. That's the right order. Find your discharge date, pick a target loan, and work backward from there.

<Disclaimer>Waiting periods, exceptions for extenuating circumstances, and lender overlays change over time and vary by program. Confirm current rules with a licensed lender for your specific situation.</Disclaimer>
