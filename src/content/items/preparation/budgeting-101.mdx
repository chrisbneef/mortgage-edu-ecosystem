Being *pre-qualified* for a number isn't the same as that number being comfortable to live with. A lender can tell you the most they're willing to lend. Only you can tell what fits the life you actually want around the payment. The gap between those two numbers is where a lot of new homeowners get squeezed, and closing it before you shop is one of the most useful things you can do.

## Pre-qualified vs. affordable

A lender's maximum comes from ratios on paper. They look at your income and your debts and back into the biggest payment the formula allows. What that math doesn't see is your daycare bill, your savings goals, the travel you don't want to give up, or the fact that you'd rather not feel broke on the 28th of every month. Those things are real, and they're yours to weigh.

So treat the pre-qualified figure as a ceiling, not a target. Plenty of people who could "afford" the maximum on paper choose to spend well below it, and they almost never regret the breathing room.

It also helps to know that pre-qualification and pre-approval aren't the same thing. A pre-qualification is often a quick estimate based on numbers you tell the lender, while a pre-approval involves them actually checking your documents. Sellers take a pre-approval more seriously, but neither one is a promise about what you should spend. Both are about what a lender might lend, which loops right back to the gap we're talking about.

## Debt-to-income, briefly

Your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">DTI</GlossaryTerm> compares your monthly debt payments to your gross monthly income, the amount before taxes come out. Most programs look for a total DTI under roughly 43%, though it varies by loan type and some allow higher with strong credit or reserves.

Here's how it works with numbers. Say you earn $6,000 a month before taxes. A 43% DTI means lenders want your total monthly debts, including the new house payment, to stay under about $2,580. If you already pay $400 on a car and $150 on student loans, that leaves roughly $2,030 for the full housing payment. That's the lender's view of your maximum.

Now notice what's missing. That $6,000 is before taxes, but your paycheck is after them. The DTI math also ignores groceries, utilities, gas, childcare, and savings. So a payment that clears the 43% test on paper can still feel tight in real life, because the formula was never built to protect your comfort. It was built to protect the loan.

Remember the full payment is <GlossaryTerm slug="piti">PITI</GlossaryTerm>: principal, interest,
taxes, and insurance, not just principal and interest. When you see a payment estimate that's "just"
principal and interest, it's missing two real bills, and taxes and insurance can add hundreds a month.
</Callout>

## What trips people up

The most common mistake is anchoring on the pre-qualified number and house-hunting right at the top of it. Once you fall in love with homes at your ceiling, the smaller, calmer payment starts to feel like settling. Set your own target before you tour anything, and let that number guide the search.

The second trap is forgetting the costs that arrive after the purchase. New homeowners often face a property tax bill that grows over time, an insurance premium that can rise, and the steady drip of maintenance: a water heater here, a roof repair there. A rough rule many people use is to budget around 1% of the home's value each year for upkeep, separate from the mortgage. On a $300,000 home, that's roughly $3,000 a year, or $250 a month you won't see on any loan estimate.

## A simple rule of thumb

Skip the formulas for a minute and do the honest version. Write down your real monthly take-home pay, the money that actually lands in your account. Subtract everything you already spend and want to keep spending, including savings. Whatever's genuinely left over is what you have for housing, and remember that "housing" means the full PITI plus utilities and upkeep, not just the loan.

That leftover number, not the lender's ceiling, is your budget. If it lines up with the pre-qualified figure, great. If it's lower, trust your own math.

<Callout type="key" title="Set your own number">
The pre-qualified figure is a ceiling, not a target. Build your budget from your real take-home pay and the full PITI plus upkeep, then shop below the maximum. The comfortable payment is the one that still lets you live the rest of your life.
</Callout>

A quick gut check some people find useful: try living on your planned housing payment for a couple of months before you buy. If your current rent or mortgage is $1,400 and your future PITI plus utilities looks like $1,900, move that extra $500 into savings every month and see how it feels. You'll either prove you can handle it, or you'll learn something important while it's still easy to adjust. Either way, you walk in with real information instead of a hopeful guess.
