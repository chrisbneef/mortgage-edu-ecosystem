Your credit score is one of the first things a lender looks at. It shapes whether you qualify at all, and it quietly sets the interest rate you're offered, which follows you for years. The good part is that a credit score isn't a verdict on you as a person. It's a snapshot of recent habits, and snapshots can be improved with a little time and attention.

## What a credit score actually is

A credit score is a number, typically a <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">FICO</GlossaryTerm> score between 300 and 850, that predicts how likely you are to repay borrowed money on time. Lenders use it as shorthand. A higher number signals lower risk, so you get easier approval and better pricing.

One thing worth knowing early: you don't have just one score. You have several, because there are three major credit bureaus and a few scoring models. When a mortgage lender pulls your credit, they usually take a middle score from the three, so the number you see on a free app might not be the exact one your lender uses. Close, but not always identical.

<Callout type="tip" title="Check it for free, anytime">
Checking your own credit is a "soft pull" and does not lower your score. Check early and often so nothing on your report surprises you the week you apply.
</Callout>

## What moves your score

Five things drive most of it, and they don't carry equal weight.

Payment history is the heavyweight. One payment 30 days late can knock a strong score down by a noticeable amount, and it lingers on your report for years. Pay on time, every time, even if it's only the minimum.

Credit utilization comes next: how much of your available credit you're currently using. The common advice is to stay under 30%, and under 10% is even better. Length of history, your mix of credit types, and recent inquiries round out the rest. Those matter less, but they explain why closing your oldest card or opening three new accounts before applying can backfire.

## A quick worked example

Say you have two credit cards with a combined limit of $10,000, and you're carrying $4,500 across them. That's 45% utilization, which is high enough to drag your score down. Pay that down to $2,500 and you're at 25%. Get it to $900 and you're under 10%.

Here's the encouraging part: utilization updates fast. It's based on recent balances, not ancient history, so a payment you make this month can show up on your report within a billing cycle or two. That makes it one of the few credit levers that can help you in weeks rather than years, which matters a lot when an application is coming up.

<Callout type="key" title="Utilization is your fast lever">Paying down card balances is the quickest legitimate way to move your score, often within a billing cycle or two. Aim to get under 30%, and under 10% if you can, before you apply.</Callout>

## What trips people up

The classic mistake is opening a new credit card or financing a car right before applying for a mortgage. It feels productive, but a new account adds a hard inquiry, shortens your average account age, and can rattle an underwriter mid-application. The months before you apply are for sitting still.

Another one: closing an old card you never use. That shrinks your total available credit, which pushes your utilization up, and it can shorten your history. Let sleepy old accounts sit there quietly doing their job. And don't assume your report is correct. A paid-off loan still showing a balance, or a late payment you actually made on time, can quietly cost you points. Errors are common, and you have the right to dispute them.

People also confuse the score on a credit-card app or a free monitoring site with the one a mortgage lender pulls. Many of those use a different scoring model than the mortgage industry does, so the number you've been watching can be 20 or 30 points off from what shows up on your loan application. It's still useful for tracking your direction over time. Just don't treat it as the exact figure your rate will hinge on.

This guidance also shifts a bit depending on your starting point. If you're rebuilding from a thin file or past trouble, the fastest wins look different, and a loan officer can point you toward the right moves for your situation. A score in the low 600s, for instance, often has more to gain from clearing one collection or paying down a maxed card than from any general advice.

## What to do next

Pull all three of your credit reports and read them line by line. Set up autopay for at least the minimum on every account so a forgotten due date never costs you. Then pick the one or two balances dragging your utilization highest and start paying them down, ideally before each statement closes, since the statement balance is usually what gets reported.

Small moves made a few months ahead of an application stack up. A higher score doesn't just help you qualify. It can shave real money off your payment for the life of the loan.
