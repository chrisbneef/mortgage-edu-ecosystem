If a home is on your horizon, the months before you apply are some of the most valuable you have. Your credit score isn't fixed. With a few smart moves made ahead of time, you can walk into a lender's office with a better number and a better rate waiting for you.

The trick is knowing which moves actually matter, because not all of them do.

## Pay down your balances first

The fastest lever for most people is credit card utilization, which is just how much of your available credit you're using. Your <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">FICO score</GlossaryTerm> likes to see that number under 30%, and it likes it even better under 10%.

So if you have a card with a $5,000 limit, try to keep the balance below $1,500, and ideally closer to $500. The nice thing about utilization is that it updates fast. Pay a balance down this month, and the lower number can show up on your report within a billing cycle or two. That makes it one of the few credit moves that can help you in weeks rather than years.

<Callout type="tip">Pay your card down before the statement closes, not just before the due date. The balance reported to the credit bureaus is usually the statement balance, so a payment made earlier in the cycle is what lowers your utilization.</Callout>

## Don't undo your own work

Some well-meaning moves backfire, so it helps to know the traps.

Don't close old credit cards, even ones you never use. The age of your accounts helps your score, and closing an old card can shorten your history and shrink your available credit, which nudges utilization up. Let those old cards sit there quietly.

Don't open new accounts right before you apply, either. A new card or car loan adds a hard inquiry and a brand-new account, both of which can ding your score temporarily and rattle a lender mid-application. The few months before a mortgage are the time to sit still, not to shop.

And pull your credit report to check for errors. Mistakes are more common than you'd hope: a paid-off debt still showing a balance, an account that isn't yours, a late payment you actually made on time. Dispute anything wrong. Fixing a single bad entry can lift your score meaningfully.

## The on-time payment is king

Above everything else, pay on time. Payment history is the single biggest piece of your score, and a clean recent stretch carries a lot of weight. Set up autopay for at least the minimum on every account so a forgotten due date never costs you.

There's also a tool worth knowing about. If you correct an error or pay down a balance and you're in the middle of applying, your lender can request a rapid rescore. That's a behind-the-scenes update that can refresh your credit in a matter of days instead of waiting for the normal monthly cycle. You can't request it yourself, but your loan officer can, and it can be the thing that gets you across a score threshold right before closing.

None of this requires a miracle. It requires a head start. Small moves made a few months out, paying down a card, fixing an error, holding steady, stack up into a higher score and a cheaper loan. Start now, and let time do the quiet work.
