A lot of buyers save hard for the down payment and assume that's the whole bill. It isn't. The total you bring to closing, called cash to close, has a few more parts, and knowing them now keeps the final number from catching you off guard. The good news is that none of the parts are mysterious once you've seen them laid out, and several of them can be reduced with a little planning.

## What's actually in the number

Cash to close is the down payment plus a couple of other buckets, minus a few credits. Walk through it piece by piece and it stops feeling mysterious.

First, closing costs. These are the fees to set up the loan and transfer the home: lender charges, title work, the appraisal, recording fees, and similar items. They typically run about 2 to 5% of the loan amount. So on a $250,000 loan, that's somewhere around $5,000 to $12,500, which is a wide range and a good reason to compare loan estimates from more than one lender.

Then come prepaids. The lender collects some costs in advance and parks them in an <GlossaryTerm slug="escrow">escrow</GlossaryTerm> account so future bills get paid on time. This usually includes your first year of homeowners insurance plus some property taxes and a bit of prepaid interest to cover the days between closing and your first full payment. It feels like extra, but you'd owe these bills anyway. You're just funding them up front.

## The reserves nobody mentions

Here's the part that surprises people. Some loan programs want to see reserves, meaning money still sitting in your savings after closing is done.

The logic is simple: a lender feels better knowing you won't be flat broke the day you get the keys. Reserves are usually measured in months of housing payments, often two to six months depending on the loan and your profile. Not every loan requires them, but if yours does, factor that cushion in so you're not scraping the bottom of your account on move-in day. And even when reserves aren't required, having a few months of expenses set aside is one of the smartest things you can do, because the first months in a home tend to come with unexpected costs.

<Callout type="tip" title="Ask about reserves early">Ask your lender early whether your loan requires reserves, and how many months. It's far easier to plan for that cushion now than to find it at the last minute.</Callout>

## A worked example

Numbers make this concrete. Picture a $300,000 home.

Say you put down roughly 3%, which is about $9,000. Add closing costs and prepaids of around $9,000 together. That's roughly $18,000 in total. But you don't bring all of it the day of closing, because you've already paid an earnest money deposit, say $3,000, when your offer was accepted. That deposit gets credited back toward your total. Any seller credits you negotiated come off too.

So in this example, you'd owe somewhere near $15,000 at the table, not the full $18,000. Your real, exact figure shows up on the <GlossaryTerm slug="closing-disclosure">Closing Disclosure</GlossaryTerm>, which you receive at least three business days before closing by law. That waiting period exists so you can read the document carefully and ask questions. Compare it line by line to the loan estimate you got earlier, and flag anything that jumped.

## What trips people up

A few things catch first-time buyers every time. One is gift funds: if family is helping with the down payment, lenders need a paper trail, usually a signed gift letter and proof of the transfer, and they want to see the money seasoned in your account rather than appearing days before closing. Move that money early and document it.

Another is the wire. On closing day you'll typically send your cash to close as a wire transfer or bring a cashier's check, because personal checks generally aren't accepted for an amount this size. Wire fraud is real, so confirm the wiring instructions by calling the title or escrow company at a number you looked up yourself, never a number or account change that arrives by email. Scammers target this exact moment.

The last surprise is timing. Your cash to close can shift slightly in the final days as the exact tax and interest figures settle, so don't drain your account to the dollar. Leave a little slack.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="Plan for the whole number">
Cash to close is more than the down payment. Add closing costs, prepaids, and any required reserves, then subtract your earnest money and seller credits. Build your savings plan around that full figure so nothing catches you off guard at the table.
</Callout>

Ask your lender for an itemized loan estimate as early as you can, then build your savings plan around the full cash-to-close number, not just the down payment. Set aside your reserves separately so you're not tempted to spend them. Knowing the whole picture ahead of time means the only surprise at closing is how good it feels to get the keys.
