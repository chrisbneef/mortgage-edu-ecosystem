A lot of buyers save hard for the down payment and assume that's the whole bill. It isn't. The total you bring to closing, called cash to close, has a few more parts, and knowing them now keeps the final number from catching you off guard.

## What's actually in the number

Cash to close is the down payment plus a couple of other buckets, minus a few credits. Walk through it piece by piece and it stops feeling mysterious.

First, closing costs. These are the fees to set up the loan and transfer the home: lender charges, title work, appraisal, and similar items. They typically run about 2 to 5% of the loan amount.

Then come prepaids. The lender collects some costs in advance and parks them in an <GlossaryTerm slug="escrow">escrow</GlossaryTerm> account so future bills get paid on time. This usually includes your first year of homeowners insurance plus some property taxes and interest. It feels like extra, but you'd owe these anyway. You're just funding them up front.

## The reserves nobody mentions

Here's the part that surprises people. Some loan programs want to see reserves, meaning money still sitting in your savings after closing is done.

The logic is simple: a lender feels better knowing you won't be flat broke the day you get the keys. Reserves are usually measured in months of housing payments. Not every loan requires them, but if yours does, factor that cushion in so you're not scraping the bottom of your account on move-in day.

<Callout type="tip">Ask your lender early whether your loan requires reserves, and how many months. It's far easier to plan for that cushion now than to find it at the last minute.</Callout>

## A worked example

Numbers make this concrete. Picture a $300,000 home.

Say you put down roughly 3%, which is about $9,000. Add closing costs and prepaids of around $9,000 together. That's roughly $18,000 in total. But you don't bring all of it the day of closing, because you've already paid an earnest money deposit, say $3,000, when your offer was accepted. That deposit gets credited back toward your total. Any seller credits you negotiated come off too.

So in this example, you'd owe somewhere near $15,000 at the table, not the full $18,000. Your real, exact figure shows up on the <GlossaryTerm slug="closing-disclosure">Closing Disclosure</GlossaryTerm>, which you receive at least a few days before closing. Read it closely and compare it to your earlier estimates.

When the day comes, you'll usually send that money as a wire transfer or bring a cashier's check. Personal checks generally aren't accepted for an amount this size. Knowing the full picture ahead of time means the only surprise at closing is how good it feels to get the keys.

<Disclaimer>All figures here are illustrative examples. Your actual cash to close depends on your loan, your location, and your negotiated terms, and appears on your Closing Disclosure.</Disclaimer>
