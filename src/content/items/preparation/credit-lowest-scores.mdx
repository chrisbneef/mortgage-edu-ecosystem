A lot of people assume you need a credit score in the 700s to buy a house. You don't. The minimums are lower than the internet would have you believe, and several solid loan programs are built for buyers who are still rebuilding.

Let's walk through what each one actually allows.

## Where the floors sit

FHA loans are the most forgiving. They allow <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">scores</GlossaryTerm> as low as 580 with a 3.5% down payment. If your score is between 500 and 579, you can still qualify, but you'll usually need 10% down. That's a real path for a lot of people who think they're locked out entirely.

VA loans (for eligible veterans and service members) and USDA loans (for eligible rural and suburban areas) set no official minimum score at all. The catch is that most lenders add their own rule on top, called an overlay, and those overlays often land somewhere around 580 to 640. So while the program says no minimum, the lender in front of you will still have one.

Conventional loans typically start at 620. Below that, you're usually looking at FHA, VA, or USDA instead.

<Callout type="info">Because lender overlays vary so much, the same score can get a yes at one shop and a no at another. If one lender turns you down on score alone, it's worth asking a second.</Callout>

## A low score isn't free

Qualifying and qualifying well are two different things. With a lower score, you'll generally get a higher interest rate, and you may pay more for <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">mortgage insurance</GlossaryTerm>, which is the monthly cost lenders charge when your down payment is small. Both of those raise your payment, sometimes by a noticeable amount.

Picture a $250,000 loan. Moving from a higher rate to a lower one can swing your monthly payment by well over a hundred dollars, and across a 30-year loan that adds up to tens of thousands of dollars in interest. The score doesn't just decide whether you get in the door. It decides how much the house costs you for years.

## Why a small bump pays off big

Here's the encouraging part. You usually don't need a dramatic credit makeover to save real money. Raising your score even 20 to 40 points can move you into a better rate tier and lower your mortgage insurance at the same time.

Score tiers often shift in 20-point bands, so getting from a 599 to a 620, or from a 639 to a 660, can be the difference between two pricing tiers. That's a very reachable jump for most people: pay down a credit card or two, clear up a reporting error, and stay current for a few months. The work is modest. The payoff shows up in every payment you make for the life of the loan.

So if you're sitting just under a threshold, it's often worth pausing a few months to nudge your score up before you apply. A short delay now can mean a smaller payment for the next three decades.

The takeaway is simple. You can probably qualify sooner than you think, and a little credit work before you apply can lower what you pay for a long time.
