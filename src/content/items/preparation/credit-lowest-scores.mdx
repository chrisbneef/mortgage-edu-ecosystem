A lot of people assume you need a credit score in the 700s to buy a house. You don't. The minimums are lower than the internet would have you believe, and several solid loan programs are built for buyers who are still rebuilding. Here's what each one actually allows.

## Where the floors sit

FHA loans are the most forgiving. They allow <GlossaryTerm slug="fico">scores</GlossaryTerm> as low as 580 with a 3.5% down payment. If your score is between 500 and 579, you can still qualify, but you'll usually need 10% down. That's a real path for a lot of people who think they're locked out entirely.

VA loans (for eligible veterans and service members) and USDA loans (for eligible rural and suburban areas) set no official minimum score at all. The catch is that most lenders add their own rule on top, called an overlay, and those overlays often land somewhere around 580 to 640. So while the program says no minimum, the lender in front of you will still have one.

Conventional loans typically start at 620. Below that, you're usually looking at FHA, VA, or USDA instead.

<Callout type="info" title="Overlays vary by lender">Because lender overlays vary so much, the same score can get a yes at one shop and a no at another. If one lender turns you down on score alone, it's worth asking a second.</Callout>

## A low score isn't free

Qualifying and qualifying well are two different things. With a lower score, you'll generally get a higher interest rate, and you may pay more for <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">mortgage insurance</GlossaryTerm>, which is the monthly cost lenders charge when your down payment is small. Both of those raise your payment, sometimes by a noticeable amount.

Picture a $250,000 loan. Moving from a higher rate to a lower one can swing your monthly payment by well over a hundred dollars, and across a 30-year loan that adds up to tens of thousands of dollars in interest. The score doesn't just decide whether you get in the door. It decides how much the house costs you for years.

A real trap here is buying right at your floor without doing the math on the cost. Plenty of buyers qualify at the minimum, feel relieved, and lock in a payment that's tighter than it needed to be. If you're a few points under a tier break, the relief of getting approved can cost you more every single month than a short wait would have.

## When the score isn't the whole story

A common surprise: a borderline score doesn't automatically sink you, and a great score doesn't automatically save you. Lenders read your score alongside the rest of your file. If your score sits at 600 but you have steady income, low debt, and money in the bank, you may get a yes where someone with a higher score and a shaky file gets a no.

The reverse trips people up too. Some buyers obsess over their score while ignoring the other things underwriters weigh, like how much of your monthly income already goes to debt or how recently you had a late payment. A score in a strong range can still get held up by a collection account, a recent bankruptcy still inside its waiting period, or a debt load that's simply too high for the payment you want.

## Why a small bump pays off big

Here's the encouraging part. You usually don't need a dramatic credit makeover to save real money. Raising your score even 20 to 40 points can move you into a better rate tier and lower your mortgage insurance at the same time.

Score tiers often shift in 20-point bands, so getting from a 599 to a 620, or from a 639 to a 660, can be the difference between two pricing tiers. That's a reachable jump for most people: pay down a credit card or two, clear up a reporting error, and stay current for a few months. The work is modest. The payoff shows up in every payment you make for the life of the loan.

## What to do next

If you're sitting just under a threshold, it's often worth pausing a few months to nudge your score up before you apply. Pull your credit, look for any errors or single account dragging you down, and pay down your highest card balances first. Then talk to a loan officer about which program fits your number. Ask specifically whether their overlays would change the answer, because the same score can land differently from one lender to the next.

The takeaway is simple. You can probably qualify sooner than you think, and a little credit work before you apply can lower what you pay for a long time.
