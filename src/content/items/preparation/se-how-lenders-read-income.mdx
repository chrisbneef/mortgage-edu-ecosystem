When you're self-employed, the income you think you make and the income a lender will count are often two very different numbers. Closing that gap starts with understanding what an underwriter actually does with your paperwork.

On a conventional loan, the lender doesn't care about your gross revenue. They care about your net income, the number left after every deduction on your tax return. Then they take two years of that net figure and average it. So if your returns show $80,000 net one year and $90,000 the next, they're working with around $85,000, not whatever your business billed.

One more wrinkle most people miss: lenders usually want a two-year self-employment history before they'll count that income at all. There are exceptions, but if you went out on your own ten months ago, your business income may not qualify yet no matter how strong it is. That's worth knowing before you assume a recent leap into self-employment is ready to support a mortgage.

## The add-backs that help you

It's not all subtraction. Underwriters know that some "expenses" on a tax return aren't real cash leaving your pocket, so they add a few things back to your income.

Depreciation is the big one. When you write off the gradual aging of equipment or a vehicle, no money actually left your account that year, so lenders add that depreciation back in. Certain other paper deductions can come back too. This is why your qualifying income is sometimes a bit higher than your tax return's bottom line, and why a knowledgeable lender can find income a less experienced one misses.

That said, the add-backs only go so far. A genuine cash expense, like supplies you actually bought, stays subtracted. The add-backs recover paper losses, not real spending.

## Declining income is a warning sign

Underwriters are looking for stability, and nothing makes them nervous like a downward trend. If year two earned less than year one, they may use the lower year or treat the trend as a risk.

A single big one-time deduction can create that false signal. Maybe you bought a $40,000 truck and wrote a chunk of it off, which tanked one year's net income on paper even though your business was fine. You'll likely need to explain it, and sometimes a strong explanation helps the underwriter look past it. The lesson is that a single year's aggressive move can echo into your approval.

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch One-Time Write-Offs">A big equipment purchase or a one-time write-off right before you apply can make a healthy business look like it's shrinking. Time those moves carefully if a mortgage is on the horizon.</Callout>

## Why this drives your DTI

All of this matters because your qualifying income feeds directly into your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income</GlossaryTerm> ratio, which is one of the biggest factors in how much you can borrow.

Your DTI compares your monthly debts to your monthly qualifying income, and notice the word qualifying. It's that averaged, after-deductions number, not your gross revenue. So a business owner pulling in $200,000 of revenue but showing $60,000 of net income gets a DTI calculated on $5,000 a month, not the $16,000-ish their revenue might suggest. That's a completely different borrowing picture.

## A full example, start to finish

Walk through one. Dana runs a design studio. Year one nets $70,000, year two nets $86,000. The underwriter averages those to $78,000, or $6,500 a month. Then they spot $8,000 of depreciation across the two years and add roughly $4,000 a year back, nudging the average up to about $82,000, or $6,833 a month. Dana also writes off $12,000 in real software and contractor costs each year, and none of that comes back, because that money genuinely left the account.

So Dana's revenue was well into six figures, but the qualifying income the lender uses is about $6,833 a month.

With a $500 car payment and a $300 student loan, that leaves room for a housing payment up to roughly $1,900 at a 43% DTI. The depreciation add-back gave Dana a bit more room than the tax return's bottom line alone would have. A lender who didn't know to look for it would have quoted less.

## Two habits that pay off

A couple of plain practices make every step here smoother.

Keep your business and personal accounts separate. When your business income flows through one clearly labeled account, underwriters can follow the money quickly, and bank-statement options stay open to you. Mixed accounts slow everything down and invite questions you don't want to answer mid-loan.

And think honestly about how aggressively you write off in the two years before you buy. Every deduction is a tax savings today and a smaller qualifying income later. There's no universal right answer, but there is a right time to ask the question, and that time is before you file, not after.

<Callout type="key" title="Lenders Count Net, Not Revenue">Your qualifying income is two years of after-deduction net income, averaged, plus add-backs like depreciation. Know that number before you assume your revenue is what counts.</Callout>

A good next step is to pull your last two tax returns and find your net income, not your revenue. Average the two years, add back any depreciation you took, and you'll have a rough preview of the number a lender will start from. If it's lower than you expected, that's not bad news, it's early news, and early is exactly when you can still do something about it.
