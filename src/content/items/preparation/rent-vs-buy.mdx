"Renting is throwing money away." You've heard it. It's also not quite true. Renting buys you flexibility and a fixed cost, and sometimes that's exactly the right deal. The honest answer to rent versus buy isn't a slogan. It's a break-even point you can actually calculate.

## The break-even point

For most people, buying pulls ahead of renting somewhere around the three-to-five-year mark. Stay shorter than that, and renting usually comes out cheaper. Stay longer, and owning tends to win.

Why the delay? Because buying front-loads a pile of costs. Closing costs run roughly 2 to 5% of the price when you buy. Then, when you eventually sell, agent commissions and selling fees take another 6 to 9% off the top. Those expenses don't disappear; they just spread out over however long you own the place.

Stay two years and you've barely lived in the home long enough to earn back what it cost to get in and out. Stay ten years and those one-time costs become a rounding error.

## What actually tips the math

One variable matters more than all the others: how long you'll stay. Not how much you make, not the interest rate, not the color of the kitchen. How long.

If there's a real chance you'll move within two years, a new job, a relationship, a city you're not sure about, renting almost always wins. The transaction costs of buying and quickly selling can wipe out any gain.

Over a longer hold, owning usually edges ahead. You build equity with every payment, and the home may appreciate. Just don't bank on appreciation. It's happened a lot historically, but it isn't promised, and markets can fall. Treat any price gain as a bonus, not part of the plan.

<Callout type="tip" title="Add Maintenance to the Buy Side">Before you compare a rent check to a mortgage payment, add maintenance to the buy side. A rough rule is about 1% of the home's value per year. On a $300,000 home, that's around $3,000 annually for repairs and upkeep that a landlord used to handle.</Callout>

## A worked example

Say you're paying $1,800 a month to rent, and you're eyeing a $300,000 home. To get in, you spend maybe $9,000 in closing costs (call it 3%). Your full monthly payment, taxes and insurance included, might land around $2,100, and you should pencil in roughly $250 a month for maintenance on top of that. So owning costs you noticeably more month to month, plus a big upfront bite.

Here's where it turns around. A chunk of that mortgage payment goes to principal, which is money you keep, not money you spend. Over the first few years that's modest, but it adds up. Now imagine you sell after three years. Selling costs eat 6 to 9% of the price, somewhere around $21,000 on a $300,000 sale. Against the equity you built and any appreciation, three years often isn't quite enough to come out ahead. Push the hold to six or seven years and the same purchase usually looks like a clear win. Nothing changed except the calendar.

## What trips people up

The classic mistake is comparing rent to the mortgage payment alone and stopping there. Rent of $1,800 versus a payment of $2,100 looks like buying costs $300 more, but that ignores the equity you're building on the buy side and the maintenance and closing costs you're skipping on the rent side. The real comparison needs every line, not just the two headline numbers.

The other trap is treating appreciation as a sure thing. People assume the home will be worth far more in a few years and let that imagined gain justify a stretch. It might happen. It might not, and you don't want your whole decision resting on a guess about the market.

## When buying loses even over the long haul

The break-even logic assumes a few things hold. If you'd have to drain your emergency fund to close, or if the payment leaves you with no room to breathe, the math on paper doesn't matter much. A house you can't comfortably afford is a risk no break-even year erases. And if your job or life is genuinely unsettled, the flexibility of renting can be worth paying for outright, even when a calculator says owning wins. Buying ties you to a place and a payment, and that anchor is a cost too.

## Run your own numbers

A fair comparison looks like this. On the rent side: your monthly rent and any planned increases. On the buy side: the full monthly payment including taxes and insurance, plus maintenance, plus the closing costs spread across how long you'll stay, minus the equity you'll build.

There are free rent-versus-buy calculators that do this for you. Plug in your real rent, a realistic price, and an honest guess at how long you'll stay. The calculator will hand you a break-even year. If you plan to stay past it, buying likely makes sense. If not, keep renting and save with a clear conscience.

<Callout type="key" title="It Comes Down to Your Timeline">How long you'll stay matters more than your income, the rate, or the market. Find your break-even year, then compare it to how long you actually plan to stay.</Callout>

This is a math question, not a character test. Renting doesn't make you irresponsible, and owning doesn't make you a grown-up. It depends on your number and your timeline, and now you know how to find both.
