The down payment is often the biggest hurdle, but the "20% rule" is more myth than requirement. A lot of buyers put off homeownership for years because they think they need a fifth of the price in cash. For most loans, you don't. Understanding your real options here can move your timeline up by years.

## How much you actually need

The minimum depends entirely on the loan type, and the range is wider than most people expect:

- **Conventional:** as little as 3% down for qualified buyers.
- **FHA:** 3.5% with more flexible credit requirements.
- **VA & USDA:** 0% down for eligible buyers (military service or qualifying rural areas).

So on a $300,000 home, a conventional 3% down payment is $9,000, and FHA's 3.5% is $10,500. Twenty percent would be $60,000. That difference is the gap between buying next year and buying in five.

## What a smaller down payment really costs

Putting less down has a price, and it's fair to know it. With under 20% down on a conventional loan, you'll usually pay private mortgage insurance, often somewhere in the range of $30 to $70 a month per $100,000 borrowed, depending on your credit and down payment. On that $300,000 home with 3% down, PMI might add roughly $100 to $200 a month until you build about 20% equity, at which point you can typically request to drop it.

So the trade is real: a low down payment gets you in the door sooner but adds to the monthly payment for a while. For many buyers that's a smart deal, since waiting to save 20% can mean years of rising prices and rent. For others, a slightly larger down payment to skip or shrink PMI is worth it. There's no single right answer, only the one that fits your timeline and budget. FHA loans handle this differently, with mortgage insurance that often sticks for the life of the loan, so factor that in when you compare.

## Don't forget closing costs

Here's the line item that ambushes people: the down payment is not the only cash you need. Closing costs (lender fees, title, appraisal, prepaid taxes and insurance) typically run another 2 to 5% of the price. On a $300,000 home, that's roughly $6,000 to $15,000 on top of your down payment.

Plan for both buckets from day one. Saving a tidy $10,000 down payment and then discovering you owe thousands more at the closing table is a stressful way to learn this lesson. Your lender can give you a written estimate early so there are no surprises in the final week.

## Down payment assistance you might be missing

Beyond your own savings and family help, there's a whole layer of programs many buyers never look into: down payment assistance. State housing agencies, cities, and some nonprofits offer grants or low-interest second loans aimed at first-time and moderate-income buyers, and they can cover part or all of the down payment and sometimes closing costs too.

The catch is that they come with rules: income limits, a homebuyer education class, price caps on the home, and sometimes a requirement that you stay a certain number of years. They vary a lot by location, so what's available in one county may not exist in the next. It's worth asking your lender what they work with locally, since a program you didn't know about can be the difference between buying now and buying later.

## Gift funds

If family wants to help, many programs let a relative gift some or all of your down payment. The money has to be documented with a gift letter stating it's truly a gift, not a loan you'll repay, and lenders will usually want to see a paper trail of where it came from and how it landed in your account.

A common mistake is mishandling that trail. If Grandma hands you $10,000 in cash that you deposit with no record, or money shows up in your account that you can't explain, underwriters get nervous and may not let you use it. Move gift funds by traceable transfer, keep the documentation, and tell your lender early. Done right, gift funds are one of the most powerful tools a first-time buyer has.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="Forget the 20% Myth">Your real starting line is your loan's minimum down payment plus 2 to 5% for closing costs, minus any gift or assistance funds. That number is usually far smaller than 20% down.</Callout>

Figure out which loan types you might qualify for, then add up the real cash you'd need: the down payment plus 2 to 5% for closing costs. Compare that to what you have saved and what family might contribute. That number, not the 20% myth, is your actual starting line, and it's often a lot closer than you feared.
