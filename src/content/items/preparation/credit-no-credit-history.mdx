Maybe you've never had a credit card. Maybe you pay cash for everything, or you're young, or you're new to the country. Whatever the reason, you have little or no credit history, and a quick online check tells you that you can't get a mortgage. That check is wrong.

A thin or empty credit file is not the same as bad credit. You're not someone who mishandled money. You're just someone the standard scoring system hasn't met yet. And there's a process built for exactly this.

## What manual underwriting actually is

Most loans get a quick automated decision from a computer that reads your credit score. If you don't have enough of a credit file for that to work, a real person can step in and review your application by hand. That's manual underwriting.

Instead of leaning on a score you don't have, the underwriter looks at the financial life you actually live. They want proof that you pay your obligations on time, month after month. The good news is you've probably been doing that for years without realizing it counts.

## The bills that count as credit

Underwriters can build a picture of you from what's called alternative credit or nontraditional tradelines. These are regular payments that don't normally show up on a credit report but prove you're reliable.

Rent is the heavyweight here. A clean record of twelve months of on-time rent payments is some of the strongest evidence you can bring, because it's the closest thing to a mortgage payment you already make. Utilities count too: electric, gas, water, and trash. So can your phone bill, your internet bill, and your insurance premiums. Even regular payments to a daycare or a self-financed purchase can sometimes help.

<Callout type="tip">Pay these bills from a bank account, not cash, and keep the statements. A paper or digital trail showing twelve months of on-time payments is exactly what a manual underwriter needs to see.</Callout>

## Programs that allow it

This isn't a niche workaround. FHA and VA loans both allow manual underwriting, and these are mainstream, government-backed programs used by buyers everywhere. So you're not being pushed toward something exotic or expensive just because your credit file is thin.

The catch is that manual underwriting comes with closer scrutiny in other areas. The underwriter will look harder at your income stability, your savings, and how much of your monthly income goes toward debt. A few months of payment reserves in the bank and a steady job make a thin-file application much stronger. The cleaner the rest of your picture, the more comfortable the underwriter gets.

## How to set yourself up

Start gathering proof now. Pull together twelve months of rent receipts or canceled checks, and ask your landlord if they'll write a letter confirming your payment record. Save your utility and phone statements. Keep everything organized in one place so your loan officer can hand it straight to the underwriter.

There's another option too. A co-borrower who already has solid, established credit, often a spouse, a parent, or a partner, can apply alongside you. Their credit history strengthens the application, and you qualify together. It's a common arrangement, and it can open the door faster while you keep building a file of your own.

No credit history feels like a wall, but it's really just a detour. You've been proving you're reliable every month you pay your rent. Manual underwriting is simply the path that lets that record speak for you.
