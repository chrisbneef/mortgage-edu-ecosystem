If you've been waiting to save 20% before you buy, here's some good news: most buyers don't, and most loans don't ask you to. The 20% figure is real, but it's a guideline for one specific perk, not a price of admission. Let's clear it up.

## How little can you actually put down?

Quite a bit less than 20%. Here's the rough landscape:

- Conventional loans can go as low as 3% down.
- FHA loans ask for 3.5% down.
- VA and USDA loans offer 0% down for buyers who qualify.

On a $300,000 home, 3% is $9,000. That's a very different mountain to climb than $60,000. For a lot of people, that difference is the whole reason they can buy this year instead of in five years.

And you're not alone in putting down less. The median first-time buyer puts down somewhere closer to 6 to 9%, nowhere near 20%.

## So where does 20% come from?

It's the line where you usually avoid <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm> on a conventional loan. PMI protects the lender if you stop paying, and you're the one who covers the cost, tacked onto your monthly payment. Put down less than 20%, and you'll typically pay it until you reach about 20% equity in the home.

The good news: PMI isn't forever. As you pay down the loan and the home holds or gains value, you can usually request to cancel it once you've built enough equity. It's a temporary passenger, not a permanent one.

<Callout type="info">Gift money from family can count toward your down payment. Lenders just need a short gift letter confirming it's a gift and not a loan you'll have to repay.</Callout>

## The honest trade-off

Here's the real decision, laid out plainly.

Putting less down lets you buy sooner and keep more cash in reserve for emergencies, furniture, and the surprises that come with owning. That cushion matters more than people expect. The cost is a higher monthly payment and PMI for a while.

Putting 20% down skips the PMI, shrinks your monthly payment, and gives you instant equity. The cost is time. Saving that much can take years, and those are years of rising rents and missed appreciation.

Neither choice is the "responsible" one. A buyer who puts 5% down and keeps six months of savings in the bank is often in a safer spot than one who drains every account to hit 20%. Run your own numbers, weigh the wait against the cost, and pick the version that lets you sleep.
