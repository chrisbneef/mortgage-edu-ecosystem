If you've been waiting to save 20% before you buy, here's some good news: most buyers don't, and most loans don't ask you to. The 20% figure is real, but it's a guideline for one specific perk, not a price of admission. Let's clear it up.

## How little can you actually put down?

Quite a bit less than 20%. Here's the rough landscape:

- Conventional loans can go as low as 3% down.
- FHA loans ask for 3.5% down.
- VA and USDA loans offer 0% down for buyers who qualify.

On a $300,000 home, 3% is $9,000. That's a very different mountain to climb than $60,000. For a lot of people, that difference is the whole reason they can buy this year instead of in five years.

And you're not alone in putting down less. The median first-time buyer puts down somewhere closer to 6 to 9%, nowhere near 20%. The 20% number sticks around partly because it's the rule of thumb your parents grew up with, back when loan options were narrower. The menu is wider now.

One thing to keep straight: the down payment isn't the only cash you'll need. Closing costs are separate, and they typically run a few percent of the price on top of whatever you put down. So a "3% down" loan still asks for more than 3% at the table. Knowing that early keeps the surprise out of your savings plan.

## So where does 20% come from?

It's the line where you usually avoid <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm> on a conventional loan. PMI protects the lender if you stop paying, and you're the one who covers the cost, tacked onto your monthly payment. Put down less than 20%, and you'll typically pay it until you reach about 20% equity in the home.

The good news: PMI isn't forever. As you pay down the loan and the home holds or gains value, you can usually request to cancel it once you've built enough equity. It's a temporary passenger, not a permanent one.

<Callout type="info" title="Gift money is allowed">Gift money from family can count toward your down payment. Lenders just need a short gift letter confirming it's a gift and not a loan you'll have to repay.</Callout>

## A side-by-side on that $300,000 home

Numbers make the trade-off concrete. Put 3% down ($9,000) and you borrow $291,000, carry PMI for a while, and have a higher monthly payment. Put 20% down ($60,000) and you borrow $240,000, skip PMI entirely, and start with a smaller payment and instant equity.

Here's the catch most people miss: hitting 20% on that house means saving an extra $51,000. At a few hundred dollars a month, that's years of saving, and during those years you're likely paying rent that's rising and watching home prices do the same. The buyer who goes in at 3% pays PMI and a bigger payment, but they're building equity now instead of chasing a target that keeps moving. Neither path is automatically smarter. They just cost you in different currencies, cash now versus time.

## The honest trade-off

Putting less down lets you buy sooner and keep more cash in reserve for emergencies, furniture, and the surprises that come with owning. That cushion matters more than people expect. The cost is a higher monthly payment and PMI for a while.

Putting 20% down skips the PMI, shrinks your monthly payment, and gives you instant equity. The cost is time. Saving that much can take years, and those are years of rising rents and missed appreciation.

## When more down does make sense

Low-down-payment loans are the right move for many buyers, but not everyone. If your monthly payment at 3% down would stretch your budget uncomfortably, putting more down to shrink it can be the safer call. If you're buying in a flat or declining market, more equity gives you a buffer so you're not stuck owing more than the home is worth. And some buyers simply sleep better with a smaller balance, which is a legitimate reason on its own.

It's also worth knowing the low-down-payment perks have edges. The rock-bottom conventional options often carry income limits or first-time-buyer requirements, and VA and USDA loans require eligibility you either have or you don't. A loan officer can tell you in a few minutes which doors are actually open to you.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="The honest bottom line">The 20% rule is about avoiding PMI, not getting approved. Most buyers put down far less, so weigh buying sooner with cash in reserve against the time it takes to save 20%.</Callout>

Neither choice is the "responsible" one. A buyer who puts 5% down and keeps six months of savings in the bank is often in a safer spot than one who drains every account to hit 20%. Get a couple of payment quotes at different down-payment amounts, factor in the PMI, and look at what each leaves in your savings afterward. Then weigh the wait against the cost and pick the version that lets you sleep.
