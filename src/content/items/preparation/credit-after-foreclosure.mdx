Losing a home is hard, and it can feel like it brands you for life. It doesn't. Lenders see foreclosures and short sales constantly, and there's a well-worn path back to owning. The question isn't whether you can buy again. It's when.

That "when" comes down to a waiting period tied to how you lost the home and which loan you're after next. Waiting periods vary by program and can change, so use the ranges here as a starting point and confirm the current rules with a lender.

## The general timelines

For a foreclosure, the waiting period tends to run from around three years on an FHA loan up to roughly seven years on a conventional loan, with VA loans often landing somewhere in the middle. Those are sizable gaps, which is why it pays to know exactly when your clock started.

A short sale or a deed-in-lieu of foreclosure is usually treated more gently. Because you worked with the lender to resolve the debt instead of simply walking away, the waiting period is often shorter than a full foreclosure. That distinction can save you a year or more, so make sure your loan officer knows which one applies to you. People sometimes lump all of these together in their head and assume the worst-case timeline applies, when their actual path was the gentler one.

<Callout type="info" title="Pin down your start date">The clock generally starts on the date the home actually transferred or the foreclosure completed, not the day you first got behind. Pull the records and pin down that exact date, because being off by a few months can change which loans you qualify for.</Callout>

## Extenuating circumstances can shorten the wait

Here's the part a lot of people miss. If the foreclosure was driven by a one-time event you couldn't control, you may qualify for a reduced waiting period under what lenders call extenuating circumstances.

Think of a job loss, a medical crisis, a death in the family, or a divorce that cut your household income in half. If you can document it, some programs will reduce the wait significantly. The key word is document. You'll typically need to show what happened, demonstrate it was beyond your control, and show that your finances have recovered since. A clear paper trail does the talking for you.

This is exactly why you should hang on to everything from the event: the hardship letters, the medical bills, the layoff notice, the divorce decree, the correspondence with your old lender. Keep it all in one folder. Years from now, when you're applying again, that folder is what can turn a "no" into a "maybe" or a "yes." It's worth knowing that not every hardship counts: lenders usually draw a line between a genuine one-time shock and ordinary financial strain like overspending or taking on too much debt. The clearer and more out-of-your-control the cause, the more weight it tends to carry.

## What to do with the waiting time

Treat the wait as a project with two goals: rebuild your credit and build up savings.

On credit, the basics win. Pay every bill on time, keep credit card balances low, and let your accounts age. A secured card or a small installment loan paid off cleanly can rebuild a healthy history within a year or two. Here's a rough sense of the arc: someone whose score dropped into the mid-500s after a foreclosure, who then pays everything on time and keeps balances small, often sees it climb back toward the 600s and beyond over a couple of years. Nobody can promise a specific number, but steady, on-time payments are what move it. By the time your waiting period ends, you want a score that earns you a fair rate, not just bare approval, because the rate you get will follow you for decades.

One thing to watch: check your credit reports and make sure the foreclosure and any related accounts are reported accurately. Errors happen, and a mistake left uncorrected can drag your score or confuse an underwriter later. You can dispute anything that's wrong.

On savings, aim for a down payment plus a cushion of a few months' expenses. Lenders often like to see reserves after a foreclosure, and that cushion also protects you if life throws another curveball before you're ready to buy.

## If waiting isn't an option

Some non-QM lenders will finance you sooner, occasionally just a year or two after the event. As with any shortcut, you pay for the speed: typically a higher interest rate, a larger down payment, and steeper fees. For some buyers that's the right call, especially if waiting means missing a specific window. For most, holding out a little longer for a government-backed loan is the cheaper road over the life of the loan. Run both scenarios, side by side, before you decide.

So here's the practical next step: find the date your home transferred, decide which loan you're aiming for, and count forward to see how close you really are. Then spend the gap rebuilding credit and saving, and keep that hardship folder handy. You got through the hard part already. Now it's just time, good habits, and a little paperwork.
