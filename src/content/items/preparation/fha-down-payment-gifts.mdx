The biggest myth about buying a home is that you need 20% down. With an FHA loan, the real number is much smaller. You need just 3.5% of the purchase price, which changes the math for a lot of people who thought ownership was years away.

On a $280,000 home, 3.5% comes out to $9,800. That's still real money, but it's a fraction of the roughly $56,000 a 20% down payment would demand. For first-time buyers especially, that gap is the difference between waiting and buying.

## Gift Funds Can Cover All of It

Here's the part that helps when your own savings fall short. On an FHA loan, the entire down payment can come from a gift. If a parent, grandparent, or other close family member wants to help, they can hand you the full 3.5%, and you don't have to contribute a dollar of your own toward it.

The catch is paperwork. The person giving the money has to sign a gift letter, a short document stating clearly that the funds are a gift and not a loan you'll repay. Lenders take this seriously, because a hidden loan changes your real debt picture. Expect to show where the money came from, usually with a bank statement or a record of the transfer.

<Callout type="tip">
If family is gifting your down payment, get the gift letter and a paper trail together early. A clean record of the transfer keeps your loan from stalling when underwriting asks where the money came from.
</Callout>

## The Seller Can Help With Closing Costs

Down payment is one cost. Closing costs are another, and they can run several thousand dollars on their own. FHA lets the seller pitch in here. A seller can contribute up to 6% of the purchase price toward your closing costs.

In practice you negotiate this as part of the offer. On a $280,000 home, that 6% ceiling is up to $16,800, which is almost always more than your closing costs will actually be. Sellers won't always agree to it, especially in a hot market, but in a slower one it's a common way to bring your cash-to-close down to something manageable.

## Loan Limits Set the Ceiling

FHA doesn't let you borrow unlimited amounts. There's a cap, and it's set per county. The program uses a floor for lower-cost areas and a higher ceiling for expensive ones, so the most you can borrow depends entirely on where you're buying.

In a typical market, the limit is comfortably above the local median home price, so it rarely gets in the way. In high-cost regions, the ceiling is much larger to keep pace with prices. If you're shopping near the top of a market, it's worth confirming the FHA limit for that specific county before you fall in love with a house.

## Assistance Programs and Reserves

You can often stack down payment assistance on top of FHA. Many states, cities, and nonprofits run programs that offer grants or low-cost second loans to help with the down payment or closing costs, and these frequently pair with FHA financing. If your cash is tight, ask a lender what local assistance you might qualify for.

One more bit of good news: FHA usually doesn't require cash reserves, the extra savings some loans want you to have sitting in the bank after closing. That said, having a little cushion never hurts. Reserves aren't mandatory, but a few months of payments in savings strengthens your file and gives you breathing room once you own the place.

Put it together and the entry cost of an FHA loan is low by design. A modest down payment, gift money to fill the gap, seller help on closing costs, and possible assistance programs can make buying real long before you've saved a conventional pile of cash.

<Disclaimer>FHA loan limits, contribution rules, and assistance programs vary by area and can change. Confirm current details with a lender before you plan around them.</Disclaimer>
