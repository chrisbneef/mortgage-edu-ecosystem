Buying your first home can feel like a wall of paperwork and strangers asking for your tax returns. It's a lot. But the path is actually pretty well-worn, and once you see the whole shape of it, the nerves settle. Here's the journey, start to finish, in eight steps, plus the stuff nobody warns you about along the way.

## The eight steps, start to finish

**1. Get a rough budget.** Before you fall for a house, do a five-minute gut check. A common starting point is the 28/36 idea: aim to keep housing around 28% of your gross monthly income, and all your debt payments combined under 36%. This isn't a hard rule, just a sane place to begin.

**2. Check your credit.** Pull your reports and look at your scores. Your credit shapes the rates you'll be offered, and small fixes (paying down a card, disputing an error) can move the number before you ever apply.

**3. Start saving.** You'll need a down payment, and you'll need cash to close on top of it. Closing costs and the down payment are two separate buckets, and people forget the second one. More on that later in the library.

**4. Learn the loan types at a high level.** Conventional, FHA, VA, USDA. You don't need to be an expert. You just need enough to ask good questions and know which doors might be open to you.

**5. Get pre-approved.** A lender reviews your income, debts, and credit, then tells you in writing what they're willing to lend. This is the step that turns you from a browser into a real buyer.

**6. Shop with a real estate agent.** A good agent knows the local market, spots problems you'd miss, and handles the back-and-forth. For buyers, their guidance is usually a big help early on.

**7. Make an offer and go under contract.** You find the one, you offer, and you negotiate. When the seller accepts, you're "under contract," and the clock starts on the final steps.

**8. Inspection, appraisal, and closing.** An inspector checks the home's condition. The lender orders an appraisal to confirm the value. If both clear and your loan is approved, you sign a stack of documents and get the keys.

## What it actually looks like with real numbers

Picture a couple earning $6,000 a month combined, before taxes. At 28%, that points them toward roughly $1,680 a month for the whole housing payment, taxes and insurance included. They've got $20,000 saved.

If they put 5% down on a $250,000 home, that's $12,500 for the down payment. But they also need cash to close, often another 2 to 5% of the price, so call it another $7,500 or so for closing costs. Add it up and most of their $20,000 is spoken for. That's the part the listing photos never mention: the down payment is only half the cash you bring to the table.

So before they even tour a home, they know two things. Their comfortable monthly number is around $1,680, and their savings can cover a home in roughly the $250,000 range. Now they're shopping with a map instead of a dream. Everything after this gets easier when you've done this math first.

## How long does all this take?

Months, not days. Saving up is often the longest stretch, and that part can run a year or more depending on your starting point. Once you're actually shopping and under contract, closing typically takes around 30 to 45 days.

So don't expect to scroll listings on Saturday and own a home by Friday. That's normal. The slow parts are the parts that protect you.

<Callout type="tip" title="Budget before you browse">Browsing homes early is fine and even useful. But run a quick budget check first. It saves you from falling in love with a place that was never going to fit, and that heartbreak is real.</Callout>

## Where first-timers trip up

A few mistakes show up over and over, and all of them are avoidable once you know to watch for them.

The first is shopping before getting pre-approved. People tour homes for weeks, fall hard for one, and only then learn what they actually qualify for. Sometimes the number is lower than they hoped, and now they're heartbroken instead of just informed. Flip the order.

The second is opening new credit during the process. You get pre-approved, then finance a new couch or a car for the new place. That new debt can change your <GlossaryTerm slug="dti">debt-to-income</GlossaryTerm> ratio and jeopardize the loan you already lined up. Lenders re-check your credit right before closing, so keep your finances boring until you have the keys.

The third is forgetting the cash-to-close bucket entirely. People save a down payment and assume that's the whole bill, then get blindsided by closing costs in the final week. Plan for both from the start.

## When the roadmap bends

This eight-step path fits most buyers, but not every situation maps to it cleanly. If you're self-employed, the income and pre-approval steps look different, since lenders read your tax returns rather than a pay stub, and that deserves its own prep. If you're buying with a VA or USDA loan, the down payment step nearly disappears, since those can go to zero down for eligible buyers. And in a hot market with bidding wars, the offer step gets more involved than "you offer, they accept." None of that breaks the roadmap. It just means a couple of steps stretch or shrink for your case.

## What makes an offer feel real to a seller

Here's the part worth repeating: a pre-approval is what makes your offer count. When a seller gets two similar offers and one buyer has a pre-approval letter while the other just says "I think I can afford it," that's not really a contest.

Sellers want to know the deal will actually close. The letter is your proof. Get it before you start writing offers, not after you've found the house you want.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="The one thing to remember">The path is eight plain steps, and the order matters most. Get pre-approved before you shop, plan for both the down payment and closing costs, and keep your credit boring until you have the keys.</Callout>

Pick step one and only step one. Pull your credit reports this week (you can get them free), and run that quick 28/36 budget with your real income. Those two moves cost nothing, take an afternoon, and tell you whether you're months or years from being ready. You don't have to do all eight steps at once, and you don't have to do them perfectly. Take them one at a time, in order, and the wall of paperwork turns into a staircase.
