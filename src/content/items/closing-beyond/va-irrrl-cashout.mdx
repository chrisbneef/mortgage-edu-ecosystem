Your VA benefit doesn't stop being useful once you own the home. If rates drop or you build equity, the VA gives you two ways to refinance. Knowing which one fits your goal saves you time and money.

## The IRRRL: a simpler way to lower your rate

The mouthful is the Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan, and almost everyone just calls it the IRRRL or the VA streamline. It does one job well: it lowers the rate on a loan you already have through the VA.

What makes it a streamline is how little it asks of you. There's often no new appraisal, the income documentation is lighter, and the whole process tends to move faster than a typical refinance. You generally need to already hold a VA loan to use it, since it's refinancing one VA loan into another. If your rate today is meaningfully higher than what's available now, this is usually the first option to price out.

## The VA cash-out: turning equity into cash

The cash-out refinance is the other tool, and it does something different. It lets you replace your current loan with a larger one and take the difference in cash, drawing on the equity you've built.

People use it for real reasons: paying off higher-interest debt, funding a home repair, or covering a large expense. One nice wrinkle is that a VA cash-out can refinance a non-VA loan into a VA loan, so it isn't limited to people who already have one. Because you're borrowing against your home, it deserves more caution than the streamline. Pulling cash out resets your balance and can stretch your payoff timeline, so be clear on why you're doing it.

## Reading the real cost

Both options carry a funding fee, and both carry closing costs, so a lower interest rate alone doesn't tell you whether a refinance is worth it. The number to watch is the <GlossaryTerm slug="apr">APR</GlossaryTerm>, which folds those costs into a single yearly rate you can compare apples to apples.

<Callout type="tip">
When a lender quotes you a tempting rate, ask for the APR right beside it. A low rate paired with heavy fees can carry a higher APR than a slightly higher rate with low costs. The APR is where the fees show themselves.
</Callout>

## The break-even math

Here's the simplest way to decide. Add up everything the refinance costs you: the funding fee, lender charges, and closing costs. Then figure out how much the new loan lowers your monthly payment. Divide the total cost by the monthly savings, and you get the number of months until the refinance pays for itself.

Say it costs you $4,800 and saves you $200 a month. Divide and you break even at 24 months. If you plan to keep the home well past that point, the refinance makes sense. If you might sell or move within a year or two, the savings may never catch up to the cost. That's especially worth weighing if PCS orders could be on your horizon.

Run that calculation before you commit, and the decision gets a lot clearer. Both VA refinance options are solid tools. The right move is the one where the savings outlast the costs by a comfortable margin.
