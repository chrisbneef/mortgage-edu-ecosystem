When you rented, a broken water heater was someone else's problem and a phone call away. Now it's yours. That sounds heavier than it is, because most home maintenance is small, cheap, and predictable when you stay ahead of it. The trick is catching little issues before they grow into the kind of repair that empties your savings. A simple seasonal rhythm makes that easy.

## Budget for it before you need it

A good rule of thumb: set aside about 1% of your home's value each year for upkeep. On a $300,000 home that's roughly $3,000 a year, or about $250 a month. Some years you'll spend far less and the cushion grows.

Other years a major system fails and you'll be grateful the money is there. Keeping a small repair fund is the single best thing you can do, because it turns an emergency into an errand.

<Callout type="tip" title="Open a repair account">Open a separate savings account just for home repairs and auto-transfer a little each month. When something breaks, you reach for that account instead of a credit card.</Callout>

## Do these on day one

Before you worry about seasons, learn two things about your house. Find your main water shutoff valve and your electrical panel, and make sure everyone in the home knows where they are. The time to find the water shutoff is not while a burst pipe is soaking your floor at midnight. Walk the house, locate them, and label the breakers in the panel while you're at it.

A couple of things deserve attention all year, not just on a schedule. Change your HVAC filters every couple of months, because a clogged filter strains the system and runs up your energy bill. And test your smoke and carbon monoxide detectors regularly, swapping batteries when they chirp. These take minutes and protect both your wallet and your family.

## Work through the seasons

Grouping tasks by season keeps the list from ever feeling overwhelming.

In **spring**, get the air conditioner serviced before the first heat wave so you're not on a three-week waitlist in July. Clean out the gutters so spring rain drains away from the house instead of pooling at the foundation. Walk the exterior and check the caulk and seals around windows and doors, touching up anything cracked to keep water and bugs out.

In **fall**, flip your attention to heat. Have the heating system serviced before you really need it. Clean the gutters again, since autumn leaves clog them fast. And before the first hard freeze, shut off and drain your outdoor faucets and hoses, because water left in those lines can freeze, expand, and crack a pipe inside your wall.

Once a year, flush your water heater to clear out sediment that builds up at the bottom. It's a straightforward job that helps the unit run efficiently and last longer, and there are clear instructions for your model online.

## Know what's cheap to prevent versus brutal to fix

The whole point of maintenance is that small, dull tasks head off expensive, dramatic ones. A few examples make the trade obvious. A $20 box of HVAC filters keeps a system running that costs thousands to replace. An afternoon of gutter cleaning protects a foundation that costs tens of thousands to repair when water pools against it for years. Draining an outdoor faucet before winter takes five minutes; a burst pipe behind a wall can mean water damage, drywall, and a remediation bill that runs into the thousands.

That's the mental model to carry: when you're deciding whether a chore is worth the Saturday, ask what it's protecting. The cheap, boring jobs almost always guard something far more expensive, which is exactly why they're easy to skip and costly to ignore.

## Where new owners go wrong

The most common mistake is doing nothing until something breaks, then treating every repair as a surprise emergency. It isn't a surprise that gutters fill with leaves or that a water heater eventually fails; those are scheduled events wearing a costume. The seasonal rhythm exists so they stop ambushing you.

The second mistake is the opposite: trying to DIY a job that really needs a pro. Roof work, electrical panels, gas lines, and anything involving a ladder two stories up are places where a mistake costs more than the repair would have. Knowing your limits is part of maintenance. And one caveat worth naming: if your home is brand new or you bought a condo, your list looks different. New builds often come with warranties that cover major systems for the first year, so document issues and call the builder rather than fixing things yourself. Condo owners typically aren't responsible for the roof, exterior, or grounds at all; the HOA handles those, and your dues pay for it.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="Stay a little ahead">Find your water shutoff and electrical panel this week, put the seasonal tasks on a calendar, and start an automatic transfer into a repair fund. Small, boring jobs done on schedule head off the expensive emergencies.</Callout>

Don't try to memorize all of this. Put the seasonal tasks on a calendar with reminders, or stick a printed list inside a kitchen cabinet where you'll actually see it. Set the first reminder for this week to find your water shutoff and electrical panel, since those matter the day an emergency hits. Then open that separate repair savings account and start the automatic transfer.

None of this requires you to become a handyman overnight. Knock out a few items each season, and lean on your repair fund when something bigger comes up. Stay a little bit ahead, and the house mostly takes care of itself.
