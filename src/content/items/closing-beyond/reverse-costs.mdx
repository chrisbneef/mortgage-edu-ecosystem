A reverse mortgage isn't cheap, and anyone who tells you otherwise is leaving something out. The costs come in three flavors: what you pay up front, what you pay every year, and the interest that quietly builds on the balance. Understanding all three is the only way to judge whether the loan is worth it for you. This is a major financial decision, so it's worth slowing down and seeing the whole picture before anyone hands you a glossy brochure.

## The upfront costs

The biggest one is usually the FHA mortgage insurance premium. On a HECM, the upfront premium runs around 2% of your home's value (technically the appraised value or the lending limit, whichever is lower). On a $400,000 home, that's roughly $8,000 before you've done anything else. This premium is what funds the non-recourse protection, the promise that you'll never owe more than the home is worth when the loan is settled.

On top of that you'll pay an origination fee to the lender, which is capped by the program but can still run a few thousand dollars. Then come the standard closing costs you'd see on any mortgage: appraisal, title work, recording fees, and so on. Add it up and the total upfront cost on that $400,000 home could land somewhere in the low five figures.

Most people roll these costs into the loan rather than paying out of pocket. That feels easier, and it keeps cash in your pocket today. The catch is that you start out owing more, and interest begins accruing on those rolled-in costs from day one. You're paying interest on your fees, in other words.

## The ongoing costs

The FHA insurance doesn't stop after closing. There's an annual mortgage insurance premium of about 0.5%, charged on your loan balance each year and added to what you owe. It's small as a percentage, but it never goes away.

And then there's interest. A reverse mortgage charges interest like any loan, but here's the twist: you're not paying it down. Each month's interest gets added to the balance, and the next month's interest is calculated on that larger number. That's compounding, and it runs in reverse of what you're used to. With a normal mortgage, compounding works for you as the balance shrinks. Here it works against you as the balance grows.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Balance Grows Every Month">
Because nothing is being paid down, the balance grows every single month. A loan that starts at $150,000 can grow well past that over ten or fifteen years as interest and insurance pile on. The longer you hold the loan, the more it costs, which is the opposite of how most debt feels.
</Callout>

## A worked example

Numbers make this real. Say you take out a reverse mortgage and the starting balance, after rolled-in fees, is $150,000. Imagine the all-in rate (interest plus that ongoing insurance) averages somewhere in the mid-single digits over time. Rates vary and nobody can promise yours, but use a round figure to see the shape of it.

In rough terms, a balance growing at that kind of rate roughly doubles over a span of years. So a $150,000 starting balance could climb toward $250,000 or more over a decade, and higher still beyond that, with no payments from you. None of that is a payment shock, because there's no monthly bill. It's an equity shock that shows up later, when the home is sold and you or your heirs see how much of the value the loan absorbed.

## Why the total is hard to pin down

With a 30-year mortgage, you can look at an amortization schedule and see exactly what you'll pay. A reverse mortgage doesn't work that way. There's no fixed end date. The loan ends when you sell, move out, or pass away, and none of those are scheduled events. Combine an unknown timeline with a compounding balance, and the total cost becomes genuinely unpredictable. You can model scenarios, and a good counselor will help you do exactly that, but you can't get one clean number.

This is also where people trip up. They focus on the cash they'll receive and barely glance at the cost of carrying the loan for years. The uncertainty cuts both ways. Stay in the home a long time, and the loan can be a reasonable deal for the cash flow it provided across all those years. Need to move after just a couple of years, and you'll have paid a lot of fixed upfront costs for a short benefit, which is one of the clearest cases where a reverse mortgage doesn't make sense.

## Compare before you commit

The high upfront costs are the strongest argument for shopping around first. A home equity line of credit usually costs far less to set up, though it requires monthly payments and income to qualify. A cash-out refinance is another option for some. Even downsizing to a smaller home can free up equity without any of these fees, if you're open to moving.

None of those is automatically better, and none is automatically worse. The point is to put the reverse mortgage next to the simpler choices and see the price tag side by side. As a next step, ask a lender for the full fee breakdown in writing, then bring those numbers to your required HUD counseling session and run the comparison there. If the loan still makes sense after that, you'll know it's a considered decision and not a sales pitch you fell for.

<Callout type="key" title="The One Thing to Remember">
The longer you hold a reverse mortgage, the more it costs, because the compounding balance grows the whole time. Get the full fees in writing and compare them against simpler options before you commit.
</Callout>
