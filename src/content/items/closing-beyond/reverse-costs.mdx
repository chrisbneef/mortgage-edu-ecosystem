A reverse mortgage isn't cheap, and anyone who tells you otherwise is leaving something out. The costs come in three flavors: what you pay up front, what you pay every year, and the interest that quietly builds on the balance. Understanding all three is the only way to judge whether the loan is worth it for you.

## The upfront costs

The biggest one is the FHA mortgage insurance premium. On a HECM, the upfront premium runs around 2% of your home's value. On a $400,000 home, that's roughly $8,000 before you've done anything else. This premium is what funds the non-recourse protection, the promise that you'll never owe more than the home is worth.

On top of that you'll pay an origination fee to the lender, which is capped by the program but can still be a few thousand dollars. Then come the standard closing costs you'd see on any mortgage: appraisal, title work, recording fees, and so on.

Most people roll these costs into the loan rather than paying out of pocket. That feels easier, but it means you start out owing more, and interest begins accruing on those costs from the start.

## The ongoing costs

The FHA insurance doesn't stop after closing. There's an annual mortgage insurance premium of about 0.5%, charged on your loan balance each year and added to what you owe.

And then there's interest. A reverse mortgage charges interest like any loan, but here's the twist: you're not paying it down. Each month's interest gets added to the balance, and the next month's interest is calculated on that larger number. That's compounding, and it works against you over time.

<Callout type="warning">
Because nothing is being paid down, the balance grows every single month. A loan that starts at $150,000 can grow well past that over ten or fifteen years as interest and insurance pile on. The longer you hold the loan, the more it costs, which is the opposite of how most debt feels.
</Callout>

## Why the total is hard to pin down

With a 30-year mortgage, you can look at an amortization schedule and see exactly what you'll pay. A reverse mortgage doesn't work that way.

There's no fixed end date. The loan ends when you sell, move out, or pass away, and none of those are scheduled events. Combine an unknown timeline with a compounding balance, and the total cost becomes genuinely unpredictable. You can model scenarios, and a good counselor will help you do exactly that, but you can't get one clean number.

This uncertainty cuts both ways. Stay in the home a long time, and the loan can be a reasonable deal for the cash flow it provided. Need to move after just a couple of years, and you'll have paid a lot of fixed costs for a short benefit.

## Compare before you commit

The high upfront costs are the strongest argument for shopping around first. A home equity line of credit usually costs far less to set up, though it requires monthly payments and income to qualify. A cash-out refinance is another option for some. Even downsizing to a smaller home can free up equity without any of these fees.

None of those is automatically better. The point is to put the reverse mortgage next to the simpler choices and see the price tag side by side. Run the numbers with a counselor before you sign anything. If the loan still makes sense after that comparison, you'll know it's a considered decision and not a sales pitch you fell for.
