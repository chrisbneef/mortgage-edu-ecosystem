A reverse mortgage flips the usual mortgage relationship. With a regular loan, you send the lender a payment every month. With a reverse mortgage, the lender pays you. You're tapping the equity you've built in your home and turning some of it into cash you can use now.

The most common version is backed by the federal government and called a HECM, short for Home Equity Conversion Mortgage. It's insured by the FHA, and it's the one most people mean when they say "reverse mortgage." To get one, you need to be 62 or older.

## How you get the money

You have choices about how the cash comes to you, and they matter more than people expect.

You can take a lump sum all at once. You can set it up as monthly payments that arrive like a paycheck. You can open it as a line of credit and draw on it only when you need to. Or you can mix these, say a little up front and a credit line for later.

The line of credit option is worth understanding because the unused portion can grow over time, giving you access to more money down the road. Many counselors point retirees toward this setup rather than a big lump sum, since a lump sum starts racking up interest on day one whether you spend it or not.

## You still own your home

This is the part that surprises people. A reverse mortgage doesn't hand your house to the bank. Your name stays on the title, and you keep living there as long as it's your primary residence. The lender has a lien, the same way any mortgage lender does, but you're still the owner.

<Callout type="warning">
A reverse mortgage has no required monthly mortgage payment, but you're still responsible for property taxes, homeowners insurance, and keeping the home in good repair. Fall behind on those, and the loan can be called due. This is the single most common way borrowers get into trouble.
</Callout>

## When the loan comes due

A reverse mortgage doesn't have a set payoff date the way a 30-year loan does. Instead, it becomes due when a triggering event happens:

You sell the home. You move out permanently, which generally means being away for more than 12 months, often because of a move into long-term care. Or the last borrower on the loan passes away.

When one of those happens, the balance gets paid off, usually by selling the home. Whatever equity is left after the loan is settled belongs to you or your heirs. If you owe more than the home sells for, the FHA insurance covers the gap, and nobody in your family has to make up the difference.

## The trade-off in one line

Because you're not making payments, the balance grows instead of shrinks. Interest and insurance get added on top each month, so the amount you owe climbs over the years. That's the cost of getting cash now without a monthly bill, and it's why this isn't a casual decision.

A reverse mortgage can be a sensible tool for the right person, often someone who is house-rich but cash-tight and plans to stay put. It can also be an expensive mistake for someone who might move in a few years or who has cheaper ways to get the money.
