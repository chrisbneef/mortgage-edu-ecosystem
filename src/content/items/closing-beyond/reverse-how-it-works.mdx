A reverse mortgage flips the usual mortgage relationship. With a regular loan, you send the lender a payment every month and your balance shrinks. With a reverse mortgage, the lender pays you and the balance grows. You're tapping the equity you've built in your home and turning some of it into cash you can use now, without selling and moving.

The most common version is backed by the federal government and called a HECM, short for Home Equity Conversion Mortgage. It's insured by the FHA, and it's the one most people mean when they say "reverse mortgage." To get one, you need to be 62 or older. How much you can borrow isn't simply your full equity. It's based on the youngest borrower's age, your home's value (up to a federal lending limit), and current interest rates. Older borrowers and lower rates generally free up a larger share of the equity, while a younger borrower or higher rates shrink it.

## How you get the money

You have choices about how the cash comes to you, and they matter more than people expect. You can take a lump sum all at once. You can set it up as monthly payments that arrive like a paycheck. You can open it as a line of credit and draw on it only when you need to. Or you can mix these, say a little up front and a credit line for later.

The line of credit option is worth understanding because the unused portion can grow over time, giving you access to more money down the road. Many counselors point retirees toward this setup rather than a big lump sum, since a lump sum starts racking up interest on day one whether you spend it or not. Picture a borrower who takes $40,000 up front for a roof and a car repair, then leaves the rest as a standby credit line. Only the $40,000 accrues interest. The untouched portion sits quietly and can even grow, ready if a medical bill shows up in a few years.

## You still own your home

This is the part that surprises people. A reverse mortgage doesn't hand your house to the bank. Your name stays on the title, and you keep living there as long as it's your primary residence. The lender has a lien, the same way any mortgage lender does, but you're still the owner. You can still sell whenever you want, and you can pay the loan off early if your situation changes.

<Callout type="warning" title="No Payment Is Not No Cost">
A reverse mortgage has no required monthly mortgage payment, but you're still responsible for property taxes, homeowners insurance, and keeping the home in good repair. Fall behind on those, and the loan can be called due. This is the single most common way borrowers get into trouble.
</Callout>

That callout is the thing that trips people up most. Folks hear "no monthly payment" and assume the home costs nothing to keep. It doesn't. Taxes and insurance still come due, and to the lender they function like a payment. If money is tight enough that those bills are already a struggle, a reverse mortgage can make the situation worse, not better, which is a real reason it doesn't fit everyone.

## When the loan comes due

A reverse mortgage doesn't have a set payoff date the way a 30-year loan does. Instead, it becomes due when a triggering event happens: you sell the home, you move out permanently (generally being away for more than 12 months, often because of a move into long-term care), or the last borrower on the loan passes away.

When one of those happens, the balance gets paid off, usually by selling the home. Whatever equity is left after the loan is settled belongs to you or your heirs. If you owe more than the home sells for, the FHA insurance covers the gap, and nobody in your family has to make up the difference. That's the non-recourse protection, and it's a genuine safeguard.

## The trade-off in one line

Because you're not making payments, the balance grows instead of shrinks. Interest and insurance get added on top each month, so the amount you owe climbs over the years. That's the real cost of getting cash now without a monthly bill, and it's why this is a major decision rather than a casual one.

A reverse mortgage can be a sensible tool for the right person, often someone who is house-rich but cash-tight and plans to stay in the home for the long haul. It can also be an expensive mistake for someone who might move in a few years, who has cheaper ways to get the money, or who's already stretched thin on taxes and upkeep. A good next step is the required HUD counseling session, where someone with no stake in selling you the loan can run your specific numbers.
