Not everyone can get a reverse mortgage, and the rules exist for good reason. They're meant to make sure the loan fits your situation rather than setting you up to fail. Here's what you need to qualify for the FHA-backed HECM, and what the required counseling step actually involves. None of this is a formality, so it helps to know what you're walking into.

## The basic requirements

You have to be at least 62 years old. This is the age the program is built around. If you're married and one spouse is younger, the older spouse can still borrow, and there are protections for the younger one that the heirs piece in this series covers. Age also affects how much you can borrow: the older the youngest borrower, the larger the share of equity the program lets you access.

The home has to be your primary residence. A reverse mortgage isn't for a vacation place or a rental. You need to live there as your main home, and you'll certify that you still do each year. The property also has to be a qualifying type. Single-family homes are the clearest fit, FHA-approved condos work, and some manufactured homes qualify if they meet specific standards. A few property types don't make the cut, so this gets checked during the process.

You also need a good amount of equity. Because the loan is based on what you've built up in the home, more equity generally means more money available to you. People who still owe a lot on a regular mortgage may find a reverse mortgage doesn't free up much, which is one of the most common surprises in this whole process.

## What happens to your current mortgage

A lot of people ask whether they can get a reverse mortgage if they still owe on their house. Often yes, but with a catch worth understanding. Any existing mortgage has to be paid off first, and the reverse mortgage proceeds are used to do it.

Run a quick example. Say your home is worth $400,000 and the program lets you draw, after costs, around $200,000 (the exact figure depends on your age and rates). If you still owe $80,000 on your current mortgage, that $80,000 comes off the top to clear it. The upside is real: your monthly payment disappears. The trade-off is that you're left with roughly $120,000 to actually use, not the full $200,000. This is where some people's expectations and reality part ways, so it's better to see the math up front.

## The financial assessment

Lenders run a financial assessment before approving you. They're not checking whether you can make a mortgage payment, since there isn't one. They're checking whether you can keep up with property taxes and homeowners insurance going forward, and they'll look at your income, assets, and credit history to judge it.

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch for a Set-Aside">
If the assessment raises concerns about your ability to cover taxes and insurance, the lender may require a set-aside, where part of your loan proceeds are reserved to pay those bills. That protects you from default, but it also reduces the cash you can actually use.
</Callout>

A set-aside catches people off guard. They expect a certain amount of usable cash and learn that a slice is being held back for taxes and insurance. It's not the lender being difficult. It's the guardrail that keeps a borrower from defaulting a year or two in. Knowing it can happen means it won't blindside you.

## The counseling step is required, not optional

Before you can even apply, HUD requires you to complete counseling with an approved, independent counselor. This isn't a sales call. The counselor doesn't work for the lender and doesn't earn anything by talking you into the loan. The session walks through how the loan works, what it costs, how it affects your heirs, and whether other options might serve you better. You'll usually pay a modest fee, though it can sometimes be waived or rolled into the loan, and you'll get a certificate when you finish that the lender needs on file.

Treat this conversation as a gift rather than a hoop to jump through. It's your chance to ask blunt questions of someone with no stake in your answer: Is this really my best option? What happens to my spouse? What if I need to move in five years? A good next step is to schedule that session early and bring a family member along, plus a list of those questions written down so you don't forget them in the moment. Coming out of it, you'll have a much clearer sense of whether a reverse mortgage belongs in your plan, and that clarity is worth more than the certificate.

<Callout type="key" title="The One Thing to Remember">
You must be 62, live in the home as your primary residence, hold solid equity, and pass a financial assessment. HUD counseling with an independent counselor is required before you can apply.
</Callout>
