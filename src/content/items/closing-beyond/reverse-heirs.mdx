The questions that keep people up at night about reverse mortgages usually aren't about the cash. They're about what happens at the end. Will my kids lose the house? Could my family owe money? What happens to my spouse if I go first? These are fair worries, and the answers are more reassuring than the rumors suggest, though there are real details that matter and a few traps to avoid.

## When the loan actually comes due

A reverse mortgage doesn't have a due date on the calendar. It comes due when one of three things happens: the last borrower sells the home, the last borrower moves out for good (generally being away for more than 12 months, often because of a move into assisted living or long-term care), or the last borrower passes away.

That word "last" matters. If two spouses are both on the loan, it doesn't come due when the first one dies or moves out. It waits until neither borrower is living there anymore. There's also a quieter way the loan can come due early: if the borrower stops paying property taxes or homeowners insurance, or lets the home fall into serious disrepair, the lender can declare a default. So "the end" isn't always tied to death or a move. Keeping up with those obligations is part of keeping the loan in good standing.

## What your heirs can do

When the loan comes due after the last borrower is gone, your heirs have choices, and they get time to make them rather than being rushed. After the lender is notified, heirs generally get an initial window of around six months to act, with possible extensions while they arrange financing or a sale, as long as they stay in communication with the loan servicer.

They have three basic paths. They can keep the home by paying off the loan balance, often by refinancing into a regular mortgage in their own name. They can sell the home, pay off the reverse mortgage from the proceeds, and keep whatever equity is left over. Or they can sign the home over to the lender and walk away, which makes sense if there's little or no equity remaining. Whatever equity is left after the balance is paid belongs to your family. The lender doesn't take more than what's owed.

## The protection that surprises people

Here's the part that calms a lot of nerves. The FHA version is non-recourse. That means if the loan balance has grown larger than the home is worth, your heirs and your estate are not on the hook for the difference.

<Callout type="warning" title="It Doesn't Preserve the Inheritance">
Non-recourse protects your family from owing more than the home's value, but it doesn't preserve their inheritance. The growing balance steadily eats into equity over the years. If leaving the home or its value to your children is a top priority, weigh that honestly before taking the loan.
</Callout>

Say the home is worth $300,000 when the loan comes due, but the balance has grown to $340,000. Your heirs can sell the home, the FHA insurance covers the $40,000 gap, and nobody in your family pays it. If they want to keep the home in that situation, they can usually do so by paying 95% of the current appraised value rather than the full $340,000 balance. So even when the home is "underwater" on the loan, keeping it isn't out of reach.

## What trips families up

The most common problem isn't the loan itself, it's silence. Heirs who don't know the reverse mortgage exists, or don't know the clock starts ticking once the lender is notified, can lose the window to act and end up with a rushed forced sale. The fix is simple and free: tell your heirs the loan exists, who the servicer is, and where the paperwork lives. A short conversation now prevents a scramble later.

Two other things catch people. First, if your kids hope to keep the home, they generally need to qualify for their own financing to pay off the balance, so the keep-it plan depends on their finances, not just their wishes. Second, heirs sometimes assume they personally owe the full balance and panic. They don't. The home secures the debt, and non-recourse caps it.

## A word on your spouse

This is where careful setup pays off. If both spouses are borrowers on the loan, the surviving one can keep living in the home with no change, and the loan won't come due until they leave or pass away too. That's the cleanest arrangement, and when both spouses are 62 or older, it's usually the one to aim for.

If one spouse is too young to be a borrower (under 62 at the time), they may still be able to stay in the home as a non-borrowing spouse under specific federal protections. The catch is that those protections have conditions, and they have to be set up correctly at closing. The younger spouse usually can't pull more money from the loan after the borrower is gone, and they have to keep up with taxes, insurance, and the home.

If you're married and there's an age gap, make this the first thing you ask your HUD counselor about, before anything else. Getting the paperwork right at the start is what keeps a surviving spouse safely housed later. This is too important to leave to assumptions, and it's exactly the kind of question a counselor with no stake in the sale is there to answer.
