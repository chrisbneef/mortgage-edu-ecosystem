The questions that keep people up at night about reverse mortgages usually aren't about the cash. They're about what happens at the end. Will my kids lose the house? Could my family owe money? What happens to my spouse if I go first? These are fair worries, and the answers are more reassuring than the rumors suggest, though there are details that matter.

## When the loan actually comes due

A reverse mortgage doesn't have a due date on the calendar. It comes due when one of three things happens.

The last borrower sells the home. The last borrower moves out for good, which generally means being away for more than 12 months, often because of a move into assisted living or long-term care. Or the last borrower passes away.

That word "last" matters. If two spouses are both on the loan, it doesn't come due when the first one dies or moves out. It waits until neither borrower is living there anymore.

## What your heirs can do

When the loan comes due after the last borrower is gone, your heirs have choices, and they get time to make them rather than being rushed.

They can keep the home by paying off the loan balance, often by refinancing into a regular mortgage in their own name. They can sell the home, pay off the reverse mortgage from the proceeds, and keep whatever equity is left over. Or they can simply walk away and let the lender handle the sale, which makes sense if there's little or no equity remaining.

Whatever equity is left after the balance is paid belongs to your family. The lender doesn't take more than what's owed.

## The protection that surprises people

Here's the part that calms a lot of nerves. The FHA version is non-recourse. That means if the loan balance has grown larger than the home is worth, your heirs and your estate are not on the hook for the difference.

<Callout type="warning">
Non-recourse protects your family from owing more than the home's value, but it doesn't preserve their inheritance. The growing balance steadily eats into equity over the years. If leaving the home or its value to your children is a top priority, weigh that honestly before taking the loan.
</Callout>

Say the home is worth $300,000 when the loan comes due, but the balance has grown to $340,000. Your heirs can sell the home, the FHA insurance covers the $40,000 gap, and nobody in your family pays it. If they want to keep the home in that situation, they can usually do so by paying 95% of the appraised value rather than the full balance.

## A word on your spouse

This is where careful setup pays off. If both spouses are borrowers on the loan, the surviving one can keep living in the home with no change, and the loan won't come due until they leave or pass away too.

If one spouse is too young to be a borrower (under 62 at the time), they may still be able to stay in the home as a non-borrowing spouse under specific federal protections. The catch is that those protections have conditions, and they need to be set up correctly at closing. The younger spouse usually can't pull more money from the loan after the borrower is gone, and they have to keep up with taxes, insurance, and the home.

If you're married and there's an age gap, make this the first thing you ask your HUD counselor about. Getting the paperwork right at the start is what keeps a surviving spouse safely housed later. It's too important to leave to assumptions.

<Disclaimer>
Spousal and heir protections depend on how the loan is set up and on current FHA rules. Confirm the specifics with a HUD-approved counselor and review the details with your family before deciding.
