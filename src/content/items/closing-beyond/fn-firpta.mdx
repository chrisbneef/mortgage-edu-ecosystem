When you buy U.S. property as a foreign owner, there's a tax rule worth understanding now, long before you ever sell. It's called FIRPTA, and knowing about it early can save you a real shock at the closing table down the road. The name stands for the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act. It's a mouthful, but the idea is simple: it's how the IRS makes sure it collects tax when a foreign owner sells U.S. real estate.

The IRS can't easily chase a seller who lives in another country after the money has left, so the law puts the job on the one party who's physically at the closing table with the cash: the buyer. That's why FIRPTA can feel like it lands on you sideways. The tax is yours, but the collection happens through the person buying your home.

## What actually happens when you sell

Here's the part that surprises people. When you, as a foreign seller, sell your U.S. property, the buyer is generally required to hold back a percentage of the gross sale price and send it straight to the IRS. The most common withholding rate is 15%.

Read that again, because it matters: 15% of the sale price, not 15% of your profit. On a $500,000 sale, that's $75,000 set aside for the IRS before you see your proceeds, even if your actual gain was only $80,000. The money is essentially a prepayment toward whatever tax you might owe on the sale. It's a deposit against your tax bill, not a final tax. Whether you actually owe that much is a separate question entirely.

There are some moving parts here. The 15% rate is the standard, but a lower 10% rate can apply in certain cases, and some sales of lower-priced homes a buyer intends to live in can qualify for reduced withholding or none at all when the price is under a set threshold. These exceptions have specific conditions, and they don't apply automatically. Don't assume you'll get one without confirming it.

## You may get money back

This is the relief part. The withholding is just an estimate based on the sale price. Your actual tax owed is based on your real gain, which is roughly the difference between what you paid for the property (plus improvements and certain costs) and what you sold it for.

Picture a concrete case. You bought a condo years ago for $360,000, put $40,000 into a renovation, and sold it for $500,000. Your taxable gain is closer to $100,000, not $500,000. The tax on that gain is likely far less than the $75,000 that got withheld. If your true bill comes out lower than the amount held back, you don't lose the difference. You file a U.S. tax return for the year of the sale and claim a refund of the overage.

You can also get ahead of this. By applying to the IRS for a withholding certificate before or around the time of closing, you may be able to reduce the amount held back to something close to your actual expected tax, so you're not waiting on a large refund. That application takes time and paperwork, so it's something to start early, not the week before closing.

The key word is plan. FIRPTA isn't a penalty and it isn't optional, but it's very manageable when you see it coming. The sellers who get blindsided are the ones who didn't know it existed until closing day.

## What trips people up

A few mistakes show up again and again. The first is confusing withholding with the actual tax. People hear "15%" and panic, thinking they owe that on top of everything. They don't. It's a prepayment, and a chunk of it often comes back.

The second is losing the paperwork. Your refund depends on proving what you paid for the property and what you spent improving it. If you can't document your original purchase price, your closing statements, and your renovation receipts, your provable gain looks larger than it really was, and you could leave money on the table. Keep these records from the day you buy, not the year you sell.

The third is assuming a real estate agent or title company will handle the tax side. They handle the closing mechanics, but they're generally not your tax advisor. FIRPTA also reaches further than many expect: it can apply to inherited or gifted U.S. property you later sell, and the rules around foreign-owned entities, partnerships, and trusts add layers this guide can't cover.

## What to do next

Know that this withholding exists from the moment you buy, not the week you sell. Build it into your thinking up front. Keep clean records of your purchase price and any improvements, because those numbers drive both your real tax and your possible refund. And factor the withholding into your cash flow so you're not counting on money that's temporarily parked with the IRS.

Most of all, get the right help, and get it early. A tax professional who knows FIRPTA, often a CPA or an enrolled agent with international experience, can tell you exactly what applies to your sale, file for a withholding certificate when you qualify, and handle the return that gets your refund back. If you don't already have a U.S. tax identification number, ask about that too, since you'll generally need one to claim a refund.

Think of FIRPTA as a known cost of owning U.S. property as a foreign national. This is a significant decision and a real tax matter, so don't navigate it on assumptions. It's predictable and plannable, and with the right advisor in your corner, it's nothing to fear.
