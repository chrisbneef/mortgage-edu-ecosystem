When you buy U.S. property as a foreign owner, there's a tax rule worth understanding now, long before you ever sell. It's called FIRPTA, and being aware of it early can save you a real shock at the closing table down the road.

FIRPTA stands for the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act. The name is a mouthful, but the idea is simple: it's how the IRS makes sure it collects tax when a foreign owner sells U.S. real estate.

## What actually happens when you sell

Here's the part that surprises people. When you, as a foreign owner, sell your U.S. property, the buyer is generally required to hold back a percentage of the sale price and send it straight to the IRS. The most common withholding rate is 15%.

Read that again, because it matters: 15% of the sale price, not 15% of your profit. On a $500,000 sale, that's $75,000 set aside for the IRS before you see your proceeds. The money is essentially a prepayment toward whatever tax you might owe on the sale.

It feels like a big bite, and it can be. But it's important to understand what it really is. This is a deposit against your tax bill, not a final tax. Whether you actually owe that much is a separate question.

## You may get money back

This is the relief part. The 15% is just a withholding estimate. Your actual tax owed is based on your real gain, which is roughly the difference between what you paid for the property and what you sold it for, with some adjustments.

If your true tax bill comes out lower than the amount withheld, you don't lose the difference. You can apply for a refund of the extra. In some cases you can even apply ahead of the sale for reduced withholding, so less gets held back in the first place. Either way, the system isn't designed to keep money you don't owe. It just collects first and sorts out the exact figure afterward.

<Callout type="info">
The key word is plan. FIRPTA isn't a penalty and it isn't optional, but it's very manageable when you see it coming. The buyers and sellers who get blindsided are the ones who didn't know it existed until closing day.
</Callout>

## Plan for it well before closing day

The worst version of FIRPTA is the surprise version, walking into a sale expecting your full proceeds and learning a big chunk is headed to the IRS. The best version is the one you mapped out months ahead.

So a few things to keep in mind as a foreign owner. Know that this withholding exists from the moment you buy, not the week you sell. Keep good records of what you paid for the property and any improvements you made, because those numbers determine your actual gain and your possible refund. And factor the withholding into your cash flow, so you're not counting on money that's temporarily parked with the IRS.

Most of all, get the right help. FIRPTA is a tax matter, and the rules around rates, exemptions, reduced withholding, and refunds have plenty of detail this guide can't cover. A tax professional who knows FIRPTA can tell you exactly what applies to your sale, file the paperwork to lower your withholding when you qualify, and make sure you get back anything you overpaid.

Think of FIRPTA as a known cost of owning U.S. property as a foreign national. It's predictable, it's plannable, and with the right advisor in your corner, it's nothing to fear.
