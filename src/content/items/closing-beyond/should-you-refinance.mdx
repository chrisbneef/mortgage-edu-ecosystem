Rates dropped, a friend just refinanced, and now you're wondering if you should too. Refinancing means replacing your current loan with a new one, usually to get a lower rate or a different term. It can save real money. It can also cost you money if you do it for the wrong reasons or at the wrong time. There's one calculation that cuts through the noise, and it's simple enough to do on a napkin.

## Refinancing isn't free

People hear "lower rate" and picture pure savings. But a refinance is basically a new loan, with new closing costs, and those usually run about 3 to 6% of the loan amount. On a $250,000 loan that's somewhere between $7,500 and $15,000 in costs. Sometimes those costs get rolled into the new balance so you don't pay them at the table, but you're still paying them. They don't disappear.

So the real question isn't "is the new rate lower." It's "will the savings actually outrun the costs before I move or sell."

## The break-even test

Here's the whole method. Take your total refinance costs and divide them by how much you'll save each month. The answer is how many months it takes to come out ahead. That's your break-even point.

Say the refinance costs $4,500 and it drops your payment by $150 a month. Divide 4,500 by 150 and you get 30. It takes 30 months, two and a half years, just to recover what you spent. After that, the savings are genuinely yours.

Now line that up against your plans. If you're settled in and plan to stay well past 30 months, the refinance probably pays off. If there's a decent chance you'll sell or move in the next year or two, you'd likely lose money, because you'd never reach break-even.

<Callout type="tip">Run break-even with the costs you'd actually pay, including any rolled into the loan. A "no-cost" refinance usually just hides the cost in a slightly higher rate.</Callout>

## Don't wait for an old rule of thumb

You may have heard you should only refinance if you can drop your rate by a full 2%. Ignore that. It was a reasonable guideline back when closing costs were higher relative to loan sizes, but today even a 0.5 to 0.75% drop can be worth it, depending on your loan balance and how long you'll stay. The break-even number tells you the truth; a blanket rule doesn't.

When you compare offers, look at the <GlossaryTerm slug="apr">APR</GlossaryTerm>, not just the interest rate. APR folds in most of the fees, so it's a fairer way to compare two loans side by side. A loan with a slightly lower rate but heavy fees can have a higher APR than one with a slightly higher rate and low fees.

One last thing that's easy to miss. If you refinance into a brand-new 30-year loan, you're resetting the clock. A lower rate is great, but stretching the payoff back out to 30 years means more years of payments and, often, more total interest over the life of the loan even at the lower rate. If you've already paid down several years, ask about a shorter term, like a 20 or 15 year, so a lower rate doesn't quietly cost you in the long run.
