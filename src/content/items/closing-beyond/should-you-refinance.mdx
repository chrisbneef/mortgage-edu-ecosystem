Rates dropped, a friend just refinanced, and now you're wondering if you should too. Refinancing means replacing your current loan with a new one, usually to get a lower rate, a different term, or to pull out some equity. It can save real money. It can also quietly cost you money if you do it for the wrong reasons or at the wrong time. There's one calculation that cuts through the noise, and it's simple enough to do on a napkin.

## Refinancing isn't free

People hear "lower rate" and picture pure savings. But a refinance is basically a brand-new loan, with brand-new closing costs, and those usually run about 3 to 6% of the loan amount. On a $250,000 loan that's somewhere between $7,500 and $15,000 in costs. The lineup looks a lot like the fees from when you first bought: lender origination charges, an appraisal, title work, recording fees, and prepaid items like taxes and insurance.

Sometimes those costs get rolled into the new balance so you don't pay anything at the table. That feels free, but it isn't. You're financing the fees, paying interest on them for years, and your loan balance goes up instead of down. The money doesn't disappear; it just changes hiding spots.

So the real question isn't "is the new rate lower." It's "will the savings actually outrun the costs before I move, sell, or refinance again."

## The break-even test

Here's the whole method. Take your total refinance costs and divide them by how much you'll save each month. The answer is how many months it takes to come out ahead. That's your break-even point.

Say the refinance costs $4,500 and it drops your payment by $150 a month. Divide 4,500 by 150 and you get 30. It takes 30 months, two and a half years, just to recover what you spent. After that, the savings are genuinely yours and keep adding up every month you stay.

Now line that up against your plans. If you're settled in and expect to stay well past 30 months, the refinance probably pays off. If there's a decent chance you'll sell or move in the next year or two, you'd likely lose money, because you'd never reach break-even. The longer you'll hold the loan past break-even, the stronger the case.

<Callout type="tip" title="Use Your Real Costs">Run break-even with the costs you'd actually pay, including any rolled into the loan. A "no-cost" refinance usually just hides the cost in a slightly higher rate, so compare the real monthly savings, not the advertised one.</Callout>

## Where people trip up

The biggest mistake is comparing your old payment to your new payment without checking what's inside each one. If your current payment includes taxes and insurance through <GlossaryTerm slug="escrow">escrow</GlossaryTerm> and the new quote doesn't, you're comparing two different things. Make sure both numbers cover the same stuff before you decide you're "saving" anything.

The second trap is resetting the clock without noticing. If you're five years into a 30-year loan and you refinance into a fresh 30-year loan, you just signed up for 35 total years of payments on this house. A lower rate is great, but stretching the payoff back out can mean more total interest over the life of the loan even at the better rate. If you've already paid down several years, ask your lender to price a 20-year or 15-year term too, so a lower rate doesn't quietly cost you in the long run.

A third one: forgetting that pulling cash out is a different decision than lowering your rate. A cash-out refinance can make sense for the right reason, but it raises your balance and your payment. Don't let a "while we're at it" pitch turn a simple rate-and-term refinance into a bigger loan you didn't plan on.

## When refinancing doesn't make sense

Skip the old rule that you should only refinance if you can drop your rate by a full 2%. That guideline made sense when closing costs were higher relative to loan sizes, but today even a 0.5 to 0.75% drop can be worth it depending on your balance and how long you'll stay. The break-even number tells you the truth; a blanket rule doesn't.

It also may not pay off if you're planning to move soon, if your credit has slipped since you bought (which can mean a worse rate than you expect), or if you'd be paying to cancel mortgage insurance you're already close to dropping for free. And if you're early in a loan you only got a year ago, the savings often haven't had time to justify a whole new set of costs.

## What to do next

Pull your current note rate and your current payment, then ask one or two lenders for a written quote that lists the new rate, the total closing costs, and the new payment broken out the same way. Do the division yourself: total costs divided by monthly savings equals your break-even in months.

When you compare offers, look at the <GlossaryTerm slug="apr">APR</GlossaryTerm>, not just the interest rate. APR folds in most of the fees, so it's a fairer way to line up two loans side by side. A loan with a slightly lower rate but heavy fees can carry a higher APR than one with a slightly higher rate and low fees. If the math clears your break-even with room to spare and you plan to stay put, a refinance can be a genuinely smart move. If it's close, there's no harm in waiting.
