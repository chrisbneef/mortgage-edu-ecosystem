You signed a stack of papers, got the keys, and now you're waiting for the first bill. Here's the part that surprises almost everyone: it doesn't show up as fast as you'd think, and once it does, the number can drift over time. Both of those things are normal, and once you understand why, your monthly budget gets a lot less mysterious.

## When the first payment is actually due

Your first mortgage payment usually isn't due the month after you close. It's due after a full month has gone by.

Say you close on July 4. Your first payment isn't due August 1. It's due September 1. The whole month of August sits there with no payment, which feels like a gift but isn't really. It works that way because mortgage interest is paid in arrears, meaning you pay for the month you just finished living in the home, not the month ahead. At closing you already prepaid the bit of interest covering the rest of July, so the meter doesn't start a fresh cycle until August. September 1 is when that first full cycle comes due.

If you want the exact date instead of the general rule, it's printed on your <GlossaryTerm slug="closing-disclosure">Closing Disclosure</GlossaryTerm>. That document is the one to trust.

<Callout type="tip">Close near the end of a month if cash is tight at move-in. You'll prepay less interest at closing and your first payment still won't hit for several weeks.</Callout>

## What you're actually paying each month

Your payment isn't just the loan. Most homeowners pay <GlossaryTerm slug="piti">PITI</GlossaryTerm>, which stands for principal, interest, taxes, and insurance. Principal chips away at what you borrowed. Interest is the cost of borrowing it. Those two go to your lender and shrink the loan.

Taxes and insurance are the other half, and they don't go to your lender to keep. They go into an <GlossaryTerm slug="escrow">escrow</GlossaryTerm> account, which is basically a holding tank. A slice of every payment lands there all year, and when your property tax bill and homeowners insurance premium come due, your lender pays them out of that account on your behalf. You don't get a separate scary bill twice a year; you've been paying it in small monthly pieces all along.

## Why your payment can change

This is the part that trips people up. Your principal and interest stay fixed on a fixed-rate loan, but your total payment can still go up or down.

Once a year your lender runs an escrow analysis. They look at what your property taxes and insurance actually cost versus what they collected. If your county raised property taxes or your insurance premium climbed, there's a shortfall, and your monthly payment rises to cover it going forward. If they over-collected, you might get a refund check and a slightly lower payment. Taxes and insurance tend to creep up over the years, so don't be shocked when the payment nudges higher even though your interest rate never moved.

A few habits make this smoother. Open the escrow statement when it arrives instead of filing it unread, since it explains exactly why the number changed. Keep a little cushion in your budget for the annual adjustment. And if a payment change ever looks way off, call your servicer and ask them to walk you through the math. They do this all day and the answer is usually simple.
