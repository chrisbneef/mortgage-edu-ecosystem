If you bought with less than 20% down on a conventional loan, you're almost certainly paying for <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm> every month. It protects your lender if you stop paying, not you, and it can quietly cost you somewhere between $50 and a few hundred dollars a month depending on your loan. The good news: it's not forever. You can get rid of it, and on plenty of loans you can get rid of it years earlier than you'd guess.

To put a number on it: on a $300,000 loan, PMI might run somewhere in the range of $100 to $250 a month. That's $1,200 to $3,000 a year going to protect your lender, not you. Canceling it even a year or two early can put real money back in your pocket, which is why it's worth understanding exactly how the rules work.

## The two numbers that matter

PMI lives and dies by your loan-to-value ratio, or LTV, which is just your loan balance compared to the home's value. Two thresholds do the work.

The first is **80% LTV**. Once your balance drops to 80% of the home's original value, you can ask your lender to cancel PMI. It's a request, not automatic, so you have to speak up. The lender will usually want your payment history to be current and may ask for an appraisal to confirm the value hasn't dropped.

The second is **78% LTV**. At this point federal law steps in. The Homeowners Protection Act says that once your balance reaches 78% of the original value, the lender has to drop PMI automatically, no request needed, as long as you're current on payments. So even if you forget all about it, the clock runs out eventually.

<Callout type="info" title="Based on original value">The 78% rule is based on the home's original value from when you bought it, following your loan's payment schedule. Reaching it sooner usually takes deliberate action on your part.</Callout>

Here's how that plays out with real numbers. Say you bought a $300,000 home and borrowed $285,000, which is 95% LTV. To reach the 80% line where you can request cancellation, you need your balance down to $240,000. On the normal payment schedule, that can take roughly eight to ten years. The automatic 78% cutoff, a $234,000 balance, lands a little later still. That's a long time to keep paying, which is exactly why the next part matters.

## How to get there faster

You don't have to wait for the regular payment schedule to grind you down to 80%. A couple of moves speed it up.

Paying extra toward principal is the most reliable one. Every dollar you send beyond the regular payment lowers your balance and pushes your LTV down faster. Even modest extra payments can pull the cancellation date in by a year or more.

The other path is a rising home value. If your area has appreciated or you've made real improvements, your home might already be worth enough that your current balance is under 80% of its value today. In that case you can ask your lender about canceling based on a new appraisal. You typically pay for that appraisal, but if it kills a monthly bill for good, the math often works in your favor.

One edge case to know: when you cancel based on a *new* (higher) value rather than the original one, lenders often hold you to a stricter bar, sometimes 75% LTV instead of 80%, and they may want you to have held the loan for a couple of years. Ask about those specifics before you pay for an appraisal so you don't spend $400 to $600 only to find out you're not quite there yet.

## The FHA exception worth knowing

Here's a trap people fall into. If you have an FHA loan, the mortgage insurance is not PMI and these rules don't apply. FHA mortgage insurance often sticks around for the life of the loan, and the usual way out is to refinance into a conventional loan once you have enough equity. If you're on an FHA loan and assuming it'll just fall off at 78%, check your paperwork, because it probably won't.

A few other situations also break the simple rule. If you've been late on payments, lenders can delay cancellation until you're back in good standing. If you have a second mortgage or home equity line, that balance can count against your LTV. And on some loans the automatic termination date is tied to the midpoint of your loan term rather than a clean 78%. None of these are common, but they're the reason it pays to ask your servicer for their exact requirements rather than assuming.

## What to do next

<Callout type="key" title="Put it in writing and ask first">When you reach 80% LTV, request cancellation in writing and ask your lender for their exact requirements up front. Otherwise the 78% rule will remove it automatically later, but that costs you months of extra payments.</Callout>

When you're ready to cancel, put your request in writing and keep a copy. Ask your lender for their specific requirements up front, the payment history they expect, whether they need an appraisal, and which LTV threshold applies, so you're not stuck redoing it. Pull your most recent mortgage statement to see your current balance, and check your original Closing Disclosure for the home's original value; those two numbers tell you how close you are.

And once it's gone, take that freed-up money and put it somewhere useful, like extra principal, instead of letting it vanish into everyday spending. You were already living without it, so redirecting it is the easiest win you'll get all year.
