A reverse mortgage gets sold hard on late-night TV, and it gets criticized just as hard online. The truth sits in the middle. It's a real financial tool with genuine benefits and genuine costs. For some people it's a smart fit. For others it's an expensive way to solve a problem that had a cheaper solution. This is a major decision, not a quick fix, so here's an honest accounting of both sides.

## What's genuinely good about it

The headline benefit is no required monthly mortgage payment. If a house payment is squeezing a fixed retirement income, removing it can change your month-to-month life in a real way. That freed-up cash flow is often the whole point.

The cash is flexible. You can use it to cover everyday expenses, handle a medical bill, delay claiming Social Security so your eventual benefit is larger, or set up a standby line of credit for emergencies. Nobody tells you what to spend it on. And under current rules, the money you receive is generally treated as loan proceeds, not taxable income, though you should confirm your own situation with a tax advisor.

You keep ownership. Your name stays on the title, and you go on living in your home. That matters to people who want to age in place rather than sell and move. And the FHA version is non-recourse, a technical word for a real protection: you can never owe more than the home is worth when the loan is settled. If the balance grows past the home's value, FHA insurance absorbs the difference, not you and not your kids.

## What's genuinely hard about it

The upfront costs are high. Between the FHA insurance premium, origination, and standard closing costs, you can spend thousands before you see a dollar. On a smaller loan, those fixed costs eat a bigger share of what you get, which is why the loan tends to make less sense the less you need to borrow.

The balance grows over time. You're not paying interest down, so it compounds along with the ongoing insurance premium. Here's a quick example of the shape of it: a balance that starts around $150,000 can grow toward $250,000 or more over a decade, with no payments from you, depending on rates. That growth comes out of your equity, which means less is left for your heirs. If passing the home or its value to your family is important to you, a reverse mortgage works directly against that goal.

<Callout type="warning" title="Taxes and Insurance Still Apply">
You're still on the hook for property taxes, homeowners insurance, and upkeep. Miss those, and the loan can be declared in default and called due, which in a worst case can lead to foreclosure. Budget for these the same way you would a mortgage payment, because to the lender, they function like one.
</Callout>

## What trips people up

A few specific mistakes turn a workable plan into a regret. The first is moving sooner than expected. Pay all those upfront costs, then relocate to be near family or into assisted care two years later, and you've spent a lot to borrow for a short window. The loan rewards staying put, not flexibility.

The second is treating a lump sum like found money. Taking the maximum cash up front and spending it quickly means you've started the interest clock on the whole amount while losing the cushion a credit line would have preserved. The third is leaving a younger spouse or your heirs out of the conversation, then discovering at a hard moment that nobody knew the loan existed or how it works.

## When it doesn't fit

If there's a decent chance you'll move within a few years, the high upfront costs probably aren't worth it. If you simply need to tap some equity and you have income, a regular home equity line of credit can be cheaper, since a HELOC usually has lower upfront costs (it does require monthly payments and income to qualify, which is the very hurdle a reverse mortgage is built to clear). And if you're already struggling to cover taxes and insurance, this loan can deepen the problem rather than solve it. The right answer depends on your income, your timeline, and how long you plan to stay.

## What to do next

Run the numbers against the simpler options before you commit. Ask a lender what the loan will likely cost over the years you actually expect to stay, in writing. Talk to your family, since this affects what they inherit and they may have perspective you don't. And use the HUD counseling session, which is required anyway, to ask hard questions of someone who isn't paid to sell you the loan: Is this really my best option? What happens to my spouse? What if I move in five years?

A reverse mortgage can be the right call for a homeowner who is house-rich, cash-tight, and planning to stay put for the long haul. Go in with clear eyes, take your time, and you'll know whether you're one of them.

<Callout type="key" title="The One Thing to Remember">
The loan rewards staying put. The longer you stay, the more the no-payment benefit is worth; move within a few years and the high upfront costs rarely pay off.
</Callout>
