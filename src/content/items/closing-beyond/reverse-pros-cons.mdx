A reverse mortgage gets sold hard on late-night TV, and it gets criticized just as hard online. The truth sits in the middle. It's a real financial tool with genuine benefits and genuine costs. For some people it's a smart fit. For others it's an expensive way to solve a problem that had a cheaper solution.

Here's an honest accounting of both sides.

## What's genuinely good about it

The headline benefit is no required monthly mortgage payment. If a house payment is squeezing a fixed retirement income, removing it can change your month-to-month life in a real way.

The cash is flexible. You can use it to cover everyday expenses, handle a medical bill, delay claiming Social Security, or set up a standby line of credit for emergencies. Nobody tells you what to spend it on.

You keep ownership. Your name stays on the title, and you go on living in your home. That matters to people who want to age in place rather than sell and move.

And the FHA version is non-recourse. That's a technical word for a real protection: you can never owe more than the home is worth when the loan is settled. If the balance grows past the home's value, FHA insurance absorbs the difference, not you and not your kids.

## What's genuinely hard about it

The upfront costs are high. Between the FHA insurance premium, origination, and standard closing costs, you can spend thousands before you see a dollar. On a smaller loan, those fixed costs eat a bigger share of what you get.

The balance grows over time. You're not paying interest down, so it compounds along with the ongoing insurance premium. A loan that starts modest can grow substantially over ten or fifteen years.

That growth comes out of your equity, which means less is left for your heirs. If passing the home or its value to your family is important to you, a reverse mortgage works directly against that goal.

<Callout type="warning">
You're still on the hook for property taxes, homeowners insurance, and upkeep. Miss those, and the loan can be declared in default and called due, which in a worst case can lead to foreclosure. Budget for these the same way you would a mortgage payment, because to the lender, they function like one.
</Callout>

## It isn't right for everyone

Two situations should give you real pause.

If there's a decent chance you'll move within a few years, the high upfront costs probably aren't worth it. You'd be paying a lot to borrow for a short window.

And if you simply need to tap some equity, a regular home equity line of credit can be cheaper. A HELOC usually has lower upfront costs, though it does require monthly payments and income to qualify, which is exactly the hurdle a reverse mortgage is designed to clear. The right answer depends on your income, your timeline, and how long you plan to stay.

## How to weigh it

Run the numbers against the simpler options before you commit. Ask what the loan will likely cost over the years you expect to stay. Talk to your family, since this affects what they inherit. And use the HUD counseling session, which is required anyway, to ask hard questions of someone who isn't paid to sell you the loan.

A reverse mortgage can be the right call for a homeowner who is house-rich, cash-tight, and planning to stay put for the long haul. Go in with clear eyes, and you'll know whether you're one of them.
