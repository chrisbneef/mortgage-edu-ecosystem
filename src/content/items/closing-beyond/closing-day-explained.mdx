Closing is the finish line. It's signing day, when the title legally transfers and the home becomes yours. The whole event usually takes about an hour, sometimes less, and most of that time is spent signing your name on a stack of documents you've mostly already seen. Knowing what's in that stack and what could trip you up is the difference between a smooth hour and a stressful one.

## The Closing Disclosure is your homework

You'll receive your <GlossaryTerm slug="closing-disclosure">Closing Disclosure</GlossaryTerm> at least three business days before closing. That three-day window exists by law for a reason: it gives you time to actually read it before you sign anything. Don't waste it.

Sit down and compare this document line by line against the Loan Estimate you got back when you applied. Your loan amount, interest rate, monthly payment, and the cash you need to bring should all look familiar. Small movements in some fees are normal, but a few categories aren't allowed to increase at all, and others can only move within set limits. If your interest rate jumped, your loan term changed, or a fee ballooned with no explanation, that's your cue to call your lender before closing day, not after.

<Callout type="tip" title="Use the three-day window">If any number on the Closing Disclosure surprises you, ask about it the moment you spot it. Once you sign, fixing a mistake gets much harder, and the three-day window is there precisely so you can push back before it's final.</Callout>

## Where your money goes

Closing costs usually run somewhere in the range of 2% to 5% of the purchase price, though it varies a lot by location and loan type. On a $300,000 home, that's roughly $6,000 to $15,000, separate from your down payment. It helps to know what that money is actually buying.

Some of it is lender fees: the cost of originating and underwriting the loan, plus any points you chose to buy to lower your rate. Some is third-party services you can't skip, like the appraisal, title search, and title insurance that protects against ownership disputes. And a big chunk is often prepaids and your initial <GlossaryTerm slug="escrow">escrow</GlossaryTerm> deposit.

<Callout type="info" title="Escrow is bundled in">
Funds for taxes and insurance are often collected into an escrow account and bundled into your monthly payment.
</Callout>

That escrow piece is where people get surprised. Your lender typically collects several months of property taxes and a full year of homeowners insurance up front to seed the account, so the cash you owe at closing can be higher than you expected even though it isn't a "fee" in the usual sense. It's prepaid money you'd owe anyway, just collected early.

## What trips people up

The single most common closing-day headache is the wire. Closing funds usually have to come as a wire transfer or cashier's check, and personal checks won't cut it. Banks also have daily transfer limits and cutoff times, so a Friday-afternoon wire can miss the window and delay everything. Arrange the funds a day or two early.

Wire fraud is the other big risk. Scammers send fake "updated wiring instructions" by email right before closing, and the money is nearly impossible to recover once it's gone. Before you send a dime, call the title or escrow company using a phone number you looked up yourself, not one from the email, and confirm the account details out loud.

A few smaller things derail people too. Big purchases or new credit cards between approval and closing can re-trigger underwriting and blow up your loan, so hold off on the furniture shopping until the keys are in your hand. Lenders often re-pull your credit and re-verify employment in the final days, so don't quit a job, change jobs, or move large sums between accounts without telling your loan officer first. And the final walkthrough, usually the day before or morning of, is your last chance to confirm the home is in the condition you agreed to and that anything the seller promised to fix actually got fixed.

It's also worth knowing closing day doesn't always go perfectly, and that's survivable. Sometimes a document needs re-signing, a number doesn't match, or funds arrive late and closing slips a day. A short delay is annoying but rarely fatal to the deal. The bigger risk is signing something you don't understand just to keep things moving, so slow down and ask questions even if the room feels rushed. Everyone at that table has done this a hundred times; you haven't, and that's fine.

## What to bring and what to do next

Bring a government-issued photo ID, your wire confirmation or cashier's check, and your patience for a long stack of signatures. If you're closing with a co-borrower, they need to be there with their ID too, or arrange a power of attorney in advance.

<Callout type="key" title="Verify the wire by phone">Before sending closing funds, call the title or escrow company at a number you looked up yourself and confirm the wire details out loud. Wire fraud is the costliest closing-day mistake, and the money is nearly impossible to recover.</Callout>

After you sign, keep your copy of the Closing Disclosure and the rest of the packet somewhere safe; you'll want it at tax time and if you ever refinance. Confirm when and where your first payment is due, since it's often not the very next month. Then change the locks, set up your utilities, and breathe. The hard part is behind you.
