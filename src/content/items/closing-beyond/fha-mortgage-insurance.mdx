If you've got an FHA loan, you're paying mortgage insurance, and you probably feel it every month. Understanding how it works is the first step to knowing when and how you can stop paying it.

FHA insurance comes in two parts. There's an upfront premium, usually around 1.75% of your loan amount, which most people roll into the loan instead of paying in cash at closing. Then there's the annual premium, which sounds like a once-a-year bill but actually gets divided up and added to your monthly payment. That monthly slice is the part you notice.

Put real numbers on it. On a $300,000 FHA loan, the upfront premium is about $5,250, typically folded into the balance so you don't write a check for it. The annual premium, often somewhere around 0.55% of the loan, works out to roughly $1,650 a year, or about $138 a month tacked onto your payment. Your exact rate depends on your loan size, term, and down payment, but that's the ballpark, and it's why this cost is worth paying attention to.

## Why It Doesn't Just Go Away

Here's the frustrating truth. On most FHA loans, the annual premium does not cancel on its own. This is the big difference from conventional <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm>, which falls off automatically once you've paid your balance down to about 78% of the original value.

FHA doesn't work that way. If you put down less than 10%, the insurance generally stays for as long as you keep the loan. You could pay your mortgage faithfully for fifteen years, build a pile of equity, and still be paying that premium every single month. It's tied to the loan itself, not to how much equity you've earned.

There's one exception worth knowing. If you put down 10% or more at the start, the annual premium drops off after 11 years. Most FHA buyers put down the minimum 3.5%, though, so this rule helps fewer people than you'd hope. And if you have one of the older FHA loans from before mid-2013, different rules may apply to you, so it's worth checking the date on your paperwork.

Don't assume your FHA insurance will disappear once you hit 20% equity. For the typical low-down-payment FHA loan, it won't. Removing it almost always requires action on your part.
</Callout>

This is the spot where FHA and conventional loans really part ways. Conventional <GlossaryTerm slug="pmi">PMI</GlossaryTerm> is built to fall off; it cancels automatically at about 78% of the original value, and you can even request removal sooner. FHA insurance on a low-down-payment loan has no such off-ramp. That difference is the single most important thing to understand about an FHA loan, because it shapes your entire exit plan.

## The Realistic Way Out: Refinance

For most homeowners, the path to dropping FHA insurance is refinancing into a conventional loan. The logic is simple. Once you have roughly 20% equity and decent credit, a conventional loan often won't require any mortgage insurance at all. You trade your FHA loan for a new one, and the monthly premium goes with the old loan.

Your equity can grow two ways: by paying down the balance, and by your home's value rising. If you bought a few years ago and prices in your area went up, you might already be closer to that 20% mark than you think. A current appraisal during the refinance settles the question.

A common mistake here is assuming you need a full 20% equity to benefit. You don't always. Even refinancing with less equity can drop you to a smaller conventional PMI payment that *does* eventually cancel, which still beats an FHA premium that never goes away. The cleanest outcome is hitting 20% and erasing mortgage insurance entirely, but it isn't the only move that helps.

## Do The Math Before You Move

Refinancing isn't free. You'll pay closing costs again, often a few thousand dollars, and you'll likely reset your loan term. So the question is whether your monthly savings justify that.

Try a quick comparison. Say your FHA insurance costs $180 a month and a refinance costs $5,000. Divide $5,000 by $180 and you get a payback period of about 28 months, a little over two years, just to break even on the insurance alone. If you plan to stay in the home well past that, refinancing usually wins. If you might sell or move within a year or two, the costs may not pay off, and you're better off keeping the FHA loan and waiting.

Also watch interest rates. If rates have climbed since you bought, your new conventional loan could carry a higher rate, which can erase the savings from dropping the insurance. When rates are similar to or lower than your FHA rate, the refinance math gets a lot friendlier. Resetting a loan you're ten years into also restarts the clock on interest, so factor in the full term, not just the monthly difference.

## What To Do Next

Start by figuring out where your equity actually stands. Pull your latest mortgage statement for your current balance, then look up rough comparable sale prices in your neighborhood to estimate your home's value today. If your balance is near or below 80% of that estimate, it's worth getting refinance quotes from a couple of lenders to see real numbers.

The move that makes sense depends on your equity, your credit, current rates, and how long you'll stay. Run those four numbers, and you'll know whether it's time to refinance or time to keep paying and wait. There's no shame in waiting; sometimes the answer is "not yet," and knowing that saves you thousands in needless closing costs.
